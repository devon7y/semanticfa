A BSTRACT
Large language models (LLMs) have revolutionized natural language processing (NLP). Yet when
used to assess psychological constructs in text, they are generally not evaluated for the types of
validity, reliability, and standardization typically expected from traditional questionnaires with rating
scales. This study bridges that gap by demonstrating how to evaluate the psychometric properties of
text-based models, which we call Text Psychometrics.
We first review different NLP methods, compare their ability to address key challenges in psychological research such as explainability, and outline methods for evaluating them on many desirable
psychometric properties. We then demonstrate this through two empirical studies. Study 1 classifies
thousands of crisis counseling conversations and Reddit posts into different types of mental health
issues and introduces a novel method to evaluate text models for content validity —the extent to
which a test captures the full range of expressions of a construct. Study 2 examines prospective
criterion validity by estimating how 49 known suicide risk factors predict imminent risk in crisis
counseling conversations.
In sum, NLP studies in psychology often rely on only a few validation metrics; here, we demonstrate
the need for broader psychometric evaluation and provide a practical blueprint and future directions
for achieving it.

Introduction

Text data is ubiquitous: it is present in social media, direct messages, clinical interview transcripts, survey responses,
electronic health records, and scientific documents. There are thousands of distinct well-studied constructs psychologists
and social scientists might wish to quantify in text including emotions, moods, symptoms, attitudes, states, traits, and
abilities, often through different dimensions such as valence, arousal, and severity. The goals of these assessments
could be to screen for a health outcome, summarize a person’s experience, trigger an intervention, or use measurements
as variables in subsequent models to further understand a psychological theory (Demszky et al., 2023; Kjell, Kjell, &
Schwartz, 2023). This can be used to make individual-level predictions, such as a suicide risk screening (Zuromski et al.,
2024), or to do large-scale public-health monitoring such as understanding how a pandemic might be affecting different
psychiatric populations over time (Low et al., 2020). The traditional approach Psychometrics has taken to measure
psychological constructs has been through the use of rating scales (Figure 1). Rating scales ask a rater to convert a
subjective observation into a specific value, usually on an ordinal scale. Compared to rating scales, systematically
measuring constructs from natural language itself can seem more complex or inexact. However, language has been

Natural language processing (NLP) offers many tools to quantify these constructs, and the recent growth in abilities of

(Rust, Kosinski, & Stillwell, 2020) are not normally carried out when evaluating NLP models for psychological
measurement. Researchers would never publish scales without assessing these psychometric properties, but they do
so for NLP and other behavioral or biological markers, which are also susceptible to poor psychometrics, a double
standard that has been called the “psychometric bias" (Shankman, Kaiser, & Shenberger, 2020).

2.1

Why text psychometrics?

61

We consider that Text Psychometrics is a concise term for the subfield of psychometrics that studies how to assess
psychological constructs in text or natural language, especially when focusing on validity, reliability and standardization
properties. Focusing on this subfield could help concentrate discussion on broadening the validation of NLP models
for psychological assessment and showing limitations of the most common approaches. A related field uses NLP
to improve the development and validation of rating-scale assessments, such as generating questionnaire items (e.g.,
Laverghetta Jr and Licato (2023)), which could also fall under Text Psychometrics, and under the more established
’computational psychometrics’ (von Davier, Mislevy, & Hao, 2022), which is much more broad in scope. A second
related area of research is assessing the artificial psychological profiles of artificial intelligence (AI) models (Shu et al.,
2023a), which has been referred to as AI Psychometrics (Pellert, Lechner, Wagner, Rammstedt, & Strohmaier, 2023).
We can distinguish this from Text Psychometrics in that the latter measures psychological properties in humans from
their natural language or uses NLP to improve rating-scale assessments in humans. AI psychometrics would not be
a fitting label for these applications given many NLP methods (e.g., lexicons, TF-IDF, sentence embeddings) should
probably not be considered intelligent, at least orders of magnitude less so than current LLMs. Text Psychometrics is
simply performing psychometrics from text and natural language instead of rating scales.

It is important to note that human language has been at the center of psychometrics’ history. For a brief overview of
psychometrics and related methods including item-response theory, network analysis, and causal inference, see Appendix
B. In Figure 2 we highlight key developments in psychometrics, NLP, and AI throughout history. Psychometrics has
discovered key attributes humans have (e.g., states, traits, attitudes) often by collecting large lists of words associated
with psychological traits. Since early in psychometric history, it was clear that just because we have different words for
traits does not mean these words refer to different attributes; therefore, there was a need to discover the underlying
independent and general traits from all possible trait names (Allport & Odbert, 1936). Thurstone’s (1934) “The Vectors
of Mind" (Thurstone, 1934) suggested we can discover the key, non-redundant psychological traits by having individuals
rate if they would describe someone with adjectives such as “friendly" or “cheerful." Using factor analysis, he grouped
words with similar ratings and was surprised to discover there were only five factors underlying personality. Factor
analysis would return how much each word is associated with each factor (i.e., factor loadings). He proposed plotting
each adjective as a vector of points in space in five dimensions whereby similar traits would point in the same direction;
if they are anticorrelated they would be pointing in opposite directions; and if they are independent, then they would
be orthogonal (p. 15). One could measure this using cosine angles from the center of the space. Allport, Odbert, and
Cattell between the 1930s and 1950s took this approach and gathered such ratings on thousands of words, an approach
that ultimately led to the Big five factors of personality (Goldberg, 1981), which includes openness to experience,
conscientiousness, extraversion, agreeableness, and neuroticism.

Bridging natural language processing and psychometrics, two fields with a shared history

Lexicons (i.e., lists of words and phrases associate with constructs) have also been used to measure psychological
constructs in text. The General Inquirer gathered thousands of words related to different constructs, but used the first
commercial computers from the 1960s to automatically count the appearance of different words in corpora (Stone,
Dunphy, Smith, & Ogilvie, 1962) which later inspired the widely used Linguistic Inquiry and Word Count (LIWC)
(Boyd, Ashokkumar, Seraj, & Pennebaker, 2022) and many other lexicons (Low et al., 2024).
Not only was language key in developing psychometrics, psychometrics has also influenced NLP, although this is
relatively unknown. About three decades after Thurstone’s (1934) address, vector space models by Salton and others
in the 1960s and 70s took this factor analysis approach (Dubin, 2004), (without acknowledging Thurstone as far as
we can tell) but vectorized words by counting how many times a word co-occurs with other words (dimensions of the
vector) within small windows of text across different corpora (Dubin, 2004; Salton, Wong, & Yang, 1975). This was
used to find documents that were similar to a query in information retrieval systems (e.g., finding articles about a topic
in libraries).
This approach, along with advances in deep learning and hardware capabilities, contributed to the explosion of
vector semantics that came with the creation of the word2vec word embedding algorithm in 2013, which was able
to better quantify aspects of the meaning of words in vectors (i.e., ordered lists of numbers that represent positions
in a multidimensional space). Improving upon word embeddings led to current state-of-the-art transformer-based
embeddings, which can encode aspects of sentence and document meaning. These embeddings from transformer models
have a wide variety of uses including information retrieval, paraphrase detection, categorizing text, summarization,
and keyword extraction (Muennighoff et al., 2022). The origins of vector semantics is often credited to hypotheses in
linguistics from the 1950s from Harris (“If A and B have almost identical environments we say that they are synonyms"
Harris (2013)) and Firth (“You shall know a word by the company it keeps" Firth (1957)): vector semantics captured a
word’s meaning by counting which words it co-occurs with. However, we find factor analysis as previously described
also had a large influence as well. We can add the following quote by Thurstone (1934) to the history of vector
semantics: “each trait can be represented as a point on the surface of a ball. If two traits A and B tend to coexist, the
two points will be close together on the surface of the ball." (Thurstone (1934) p. 14).

3.1

Brief overview of text classification methods for psychological assessments

138

Here we provide different ways NLP tackles the task of assigning a score to a text document for a given construct (e.g.,
does the text mention loneliness?), which is called “text classification" in NLP. This is analogous to assessments with
rating scales, which generally assign a number to a construct for a given individual 2 . We focus on describing prior
work achieving text classification through multiple methods displayed in Figure 3 3 .

Lexicons or dictionaries. Using lexicons is a traditional way of performing text classification (Stone et al., 1962).
Lexicons contain one or many constructs, each with an associated list of tokens (words and phrases) – which can be
validated by domain experts – that are counted in a given document (Low et al., 2024). Higher counts indicate the
construct is more likely to be present. Then these counts can be used as features in a statistical or predictive model,
which would be highly interpretable and would avoid missing obvious, prototypical tokens (e.g., “anorexia" for eating
concerns) that might be missed by a high-performing deep learning model (Low et al., 2024). Our construct-tracker
packages allows for using generative AI to automatically create the initial lexicon, which can then be validated by
human experts (Low et al., 2024).
Term Frequency–Inverse Document Frequency (TF-IDF) is a common technique for identifying words and phrases
in documents that characterize a dependent variable based on how frequently they appear in a given text relative to
a larger corpus (Jones, 1972). These scores can be used as features in predictive models (e.g., see Low et al. (2020)
for words that characterize different mental health support groups on Reddit). Unlike lexicons, TF-IDF requires
no expert input, making it data-driven, scalable, and domain-agnostic. However, as with lexicons, TF-IDF treats
words independently and lacks semantic understanding. Furthermore, this data-driven approach that finds words that
characterize a variable is likely to be dependent on the specific dataset used and might not generalize to other datasets.

Other methods presented here (including lexicons and LLMs) have the advantage of detecting a broad coverage of
words that are prototypical of a construct –if designed to do so– and not just associated in some way in a specific dataset
(Low et al., 2024).
The remaining methods are generally carried out using deep learning methods. Rarely is training done from scratch on
the target task (e.g., loneliness detection), given they generally require large datasets coded for the target task; rather,
pretrained models like BERT (Devlin, Chang, Lee, & Toutanova, 2018) are trained on a very large corpus (the entire
English Wikipedia and other sources) in a self-supervised manner (e.g., predicting masked words within the input) to
generate rich, internal representations that quantify aspects of meaning of words and phrases. Then this model can
be fine-tuned (i.e., adapted) for a specific task (e.g., detecting loneliness in text) on a smaller, annotated dataset (e.g.,
documents coded for loneliness or other constructs) by attaching a feedforward neural network (Sun, Qiu, Xu, & Huang,
2019). Pretraining can also be done in a supervised manner by training to predict labels in large, publicly-available
datasets (e.g., for named-entity recognition). Then these pretrained models can be fine-tuned for the target task, which
should improve performance in comparison to using the target dataset to train the model from scratch.
Named-entity recognition classifies each token as a possible entity belonging to a category or ontology (Ashok &
Lipton, 2023). The presence or sum of entity types in a document would return similar results as text classification. The
general approach is to train a model to output a probability for each entity type.
Natural language inference and textual entailment. This method can be used for text classification as well (Gretz et
al., 2023; Schopf, Braun, & Matthes, 2022; Yin, Hay, & Roth, 2019). Entailment is inferring whether two sentences
are a contradiction or an entailment (e.g., “Met my first girlfriend that way." is a contradiction of “I didn’t meet my
first girlfriend until later."; “At 8:34, the Boston Center controller received a third transmission from American 11" is
an entailment of “The Boston Center controller got a third transmission from American 11."; Williams, Nangia, and
Bowman (2018)). In a classification setup, the document to be classified can be treated as the premise for which one
can build a positive hypothesis using the template “this text is about <construct>" as well as a negative hypothesis using
other labels; (Yin et al., 2019). Gretz et al (2023) Gretz et al. (2023) fine-tune natural language inference models which
output entailment and contradiction scores that serve for text classification.
Fine-tuning deep learning models. Whereas traditional methods involve some preprocessing (e.g., lemmatization,
data cleaning, and feature extraction), pretrained deep learning models like BERT allows for end-to-end prediction,
taking raw data, skipping most preprocessing, and outputting a prediction (Devlin et al., 2018). However, this generally
requires a substantial amount of labeled training data, at least in the thousands of samples (although new advances such
as LoRA are improving this (Hu et al., 2021)).
Construct-text similarity. Since lexicons can miss important tokens that they do not have (e.g., a lexicon may have
suicide attempt but miss “attempted suicide" if not in the lexicon), there is a method to find semantically similar tokens
to the ones in lexicon using deep learning using relatively interpretable method called similarity-based zero-shot text
classification (Schopf et al., 2022) or sentecon (Lin & Morency, 2023). We introduce a more generalized version that
has more parameters to chose from for each step, which we call construct-text similarity (which we expect will be
a more transparent term for psychologists), as shown in Appendix Figure 8. This method works as follows: it first
computes the similarity between a target construct representation (e.g., text embedding for the phrase "eating disorders"
or for many tokens related to eating disorders or the average of all of these) and each token in a given document (e.g.,
text embeddings for phrases). It then takes a summary statistic as the final output (e.g., maximum between all construct
and document embeddings). This approach produces a zero-shot output, meaning the method requires no training data.
The construct can be the average of the text embeddings for a lexicon’s category, which means that it can capture much
of what the lexicon captures and also similar words while a lexicon can only capture exact matches. The process of
being able to capture similar words has been demonstrated to outperform lexicons (Lin & Morency, 2023). It is a middle
ground between interpretable lexicons which will miss similar words if not in the lexicon and standard supervised deep
learning which is a black box and hard to explain. It can also combine counts (0 or N with semantic similarity if there
is not an exact match). However, it does not learn the contextual cues or reasoning abilities found in the previously
described deep learning models. The resulting score can be used as the output for zero-shot text classification. It was
found to outperform the textual entailment approach in a zero-shot setting (Schopf et al., 2022), but not when textual
entailment models were fine-tuned (Gretz et al., 2023). It is also more lightweight than deep learning methods because it
can run more easily on standard CPUs, unlike deep learning, which often requires specialized GPUs. This is especially
beneficial for psychology researchers who may not have the expertise to work with GPUs.4

Desirable natural language processing model characteristics that can address key constraints in
psychological research

Now that we have introduced several NLP methods, we highlight desirable NLP model characteristics as shown in
Table 1. These tackle pragmatic constraints that psychology researchers often face. One is the limited availability of big
training datasets, especially in clinical settings. This poses a challenge for fine-tuning standard supervised learning
models such as BERT that require large datasets. A second challenge is the need for interpretable models to understand
how a model is making predictions to increase trust that it is not biased (Kaur, Uslu, Rittichier, & Durresi, 2022).
Explainability methods also allow researchers to rank which variables are most predictive of a target variable, which
can be used to advance scientific understanding of the variables or discover novel risk factors (Molnar, 2020). A third
challenge is the need to keep sensitive data private, which currently limits the use of submitting data to cloud services to
use existing proprietary generative AI models, although cloud-based data security may improve in the near future.

Desirable psychometric properties of psychological assessments and how to evaluate these
properties in text

There are many desirable psychometric properties that we may want a psychological assessment tool to fulfill. Here we
provide a concise outline of these properties with examples of how to evaluate them with text-based models. This list of
properties is helpful for several reasons: first, to adapt the definitions of classical and modern psychometric concepts to
fit the needs of current NLP and AI goals. And second, to inspire new types of analyses that might be less frequent
among NLP researchers so they can validate their models more broadly and help link their intuitive analyses to concepts
that already have a vast literature in psychometrics.
Criterion validity. An instrument score is valid if it is associated with a criterion assumed or demonstrated to be valid
Rust et al. (2020). The criterion is often a human or expert judgment but can also be a certain behavior or biological
variable. Psychometrics has generally focused on within-sample associations, while NLP and machine learning focus
on out-of-sample prediction. There are three types of criterion validity, depending on when the criterion and the
instrument were assessed:

(a) Concurrent validity: The instrument’s score is associated with a more standard or previously validated
questionnaire total score or questionnaire item that at face value is valid, assessed at the same time point, either through
correlations or out-of-sample prediction. NLP example: Correctly classifying crisis counseling conversations as being
about physical abuse given the counselor’s annotation (i.e., the criterion; demonstrated in section 5).
(b) Prospective validity: Rating scales should be associated with future scores or future behaviors within the same person, which may require repeated measures. This will also depend on whether the construct is an episodically-changing
trait or a persistent trait. Higher scores on a suicide risk assessment questionnaire should have a positive correlation
with future suicide attempts. This is related to the study of dataset shift in machine learning (see Table 1). This has been
traditionally referred to as “predictive validity" (Rust et al., 2020); however, given correlation is not prediction (see
Prediction entry below), we use “prospective validity" instead. NLP examples: (1) Demonstrate baseline language
patterns are associated with future PTSD symptoms (Son et al., 2023). (2) Predict whether a conversation will be
annotated as an imminent risk by the counselor (better assessed towards the end of the session) from the initial 25% of
the conversation (demonstrated in section 6).
(c) Retrospective validity: Whether an instrument reflects past conditions or behaviors. For example, the score of a
suicide risk assessment should correlate with lifetime suicidal thoughts and behaviors. NLP example: Train a model
to detect language about depression and correctly identify which individuals had been diagnosed with a depressive
disorder in the past, independent of current diagnosis.

Ecological validity. The assessment resembles real-world conditions vs. tightly controlled laboratory conditions.
NLP example: Have participants journal by randomly prompting them during the day (i.e., ecological momentary
assessment); social media data (Low et al., 2020).
Face validity. Whether a test appears to be valid for members of the target population based on a subjective judgment.
For instance, individuals may not support the claim that the way they draw a person –a common projective assessment–
clearly reflects their personality or mental health. If a math exam is clearly too hard for test-takers, then it will have
low face validity. A scale assessing honesty may purposely try to have low face validity to avoid a social desirability
bias. NLP example: The individuals being assessed could compare the construct being assessed (e.g., anxiety) with the
question they were asked (e.g., “Describe what has been worrying you the most today", which would have high face
validity versus “Describe this picture of a forest" which may have low face validity). If the model is explainable (see
Table 1), they could assess the appropriateness of the explanation. For instance, if a model uses words from text like
“bulimia" and “eating" to indicate eating and body image concerns but ignores “anorexia", it may indicate low face
validity (Low et al., 2024)).
Construct validity. Some constructs such as pain, mood, attitudes, disability, and quality of life are not directly
measurable and therefore do not have a clear gold-standard method of measurement (Davidson, 2014). Construct
validation is thought to provide support that the measurement is measuring a given construct. A measurement score for
a construct should not be highly associated with scores for unrelated constructs (discriminant validity) and associated
scores for similar constructs (convergent validity). It can be demonstrated though a pairwise correlation matrix of related
and unrelated scores or through prediction. Discriminant validity has a variety of methods (Rönkkö & Cho, 2022). If
one of the two variables being associated is considered true (e.g., a gold standard), then the association would be a
demonstration of criterion validity; when neither is, it is a demonstration of construct validity. A test can be similar to
another, but they can both be invalid; therefore construct validity can be seen as an inferior form of validity than criterion
validity with a gold standard, and there is good reason to suggest it does not assess validity properly (Borsboom, Cramer,
Kievit, Scholten, & Franić, 2009). NLP example: Among individuals at clinical high-risk for psychosis, the NLP metric
of semantic coherence was correlated with positive thought disorder severity (e.g. tangentiality and derailment) and the
NLP metrics of complexity (sentence length and complementizer use) were correlated with negative thought disorder
severity (poverty of speech and content) (Bilgrami et al., 2022). A type of construct validity is known-groups validity:
When a test score discriminates between two groups known to differ (Davidson, 2014). NLP example: A valid measure
of quality of life from natural language should likely differ between groups with and without chronic pain.
Content validity. The systematic examination of the test content by an expert to determine whether it covers a
representative sample of the behavior to be measured (Anastasi & Urbina, 1997). A scale assessing depression should
cover most common symptoms; however, depression scales perhaps should be expected to have low content validity
given the dozens of diverse symptoms that are considered part of depression (i.e., heterogeneity, (Fried, Flake, &
Robinaugh, 2022)). NLP example: Even though a classifier has relatively high performance on a test set, this does
not guarantee it covers a representative sample of the behaviors of the construct being measured. A classifier can
achieve higher performance detecting eating or body image concerns while missing “anorexia" if this token is relatively
infrequent in the test set (Low et al., 2024). LIWC has low content validity for most mental health symptoms such as
hopelessness or suicidal ideation. Content validity test sets compute the sensitivity of a given trained model on its ability
to predict as positive a list of tokens that cover most types of instances of the construct (proposed in the current study).

Epistemic validity and clinical utility. Validity is generally seen as a demonstration that a test measures what it
intends to measure (Borsboom, Mellenbergh, & Van Heerden, 2004; Kelley, 1927), independent of how it is being used
(Borsboom, 2006). However, a test may be used to inform a diagnosis or treatment plan or to determine the efficacy
of a therapeutic or educational intervention. Aspects of how a score is interpreted and used in practice ethically or
unethically are related to its epistemic validity (Truijens, Cornelis, Desmet, De Smet, & Meganck, 2019) (see also,
Jacobs and Wallach (2021)). Relatedly, determining whether a certain sensitivity and precision of a test is appropriate
depends not on only their values but on how decisions will be made as a consequence of their values, their clinical
utility. This can be quantified through a net benefit analysis which weighs potential benefits versus harms of a test,
considering its true positive and false positive rates (Kennedy et al., 2021; Kessler, Bossarte, Luedtke, Zaslavsky, &
Zubizarreta, 2020; Vickers, Van Calster, & Steyerberg, 2016). NLP example: Consider an NLP model that detects
suicide ideation in the last clinical visit to determine whether their should be a follow-up; if the model has low precision,
it might still lead to improved outcomes compared to not deploying it, a net benefit.
Fairness. Related to the concept of equivalence in psychometrics, a test should not unintentionally function differently
for different groups (e.g., age, disability, gender, race, ethnicity, education level, sexual orientation) Rust et al. (2020),
particularly if it is unknown to the test recipient. More broadly, fairness relates to evaluating whether discoveries (e.g.,
five dimensions of personality) generalize as universals beyond the socioculturally-narrow populations where they were
tested (Gurven, Von Rueden, Massenkoff, Kaplan, & Lero Vie, 2013). NLP example: A model underpredicts the need
for an intervention for a protected group. Metrics and algorithms exist to identify and mitigate bias (Hort, Chen, Zhang,
Harman, & Sarro, 2023). Discovering social bias in LLMs is another active field of research given the data used to train
models, while large in scale, may also be narrow socioculturally (Kotek, Dockum, & Sun, 2023).
Measures of uncertainty. Uncertainty can be considered as doubt about the validity of a measurement result and a
dispersion of the values that could reasonably be attributed to the attribute being measured (Jcgm et al., 2008). While
standard deviations and confidence intervals can be estimated as average effects, individual predictions should also
convey level of confidence as well as a lack of confidence when a model has not been trained on similar samples.
Uncertainty methods for individual predictions include prediction intervals, conformal sets, Monte Carlo dropout,
ensembling, and Bayesian methods (Kompa, Snoek, & Beam, 2021). NLP examples: (1) If model predicts high risk
with high uncertainty, do not deploy planned intervention and have expert inspect. (2) Generative AI models could
provide confidence estimates and evidence that supports their opinions or inferences.
Standardization. Method by which a score’s test is interpreted by comparing a score to a representative sample
(populational norms) on standardized scores (e.g., stanines, T scores; Rust et al. (2020)). Lack of replication across
studies can occur when different measurements of the same construct are done differently due to either different
unstandardized research design or operationalization. NLP examples: (1) Leaderboards are ways of standardizing
models’ criterion validity, public tokenizers can help standardize preprocessing. (2) Standards for feature extraction
could be developed to rule out results across studies are not different due to differences in pipeline implementations or
choices in parameters.
Unidimensionality. Whether the target construct to be measured is homogenous, that is, indicators measure the
same thing (e.g., a specific type of insomnia) or whether it is heterogeneous and thus measures multiple things (e.g.,
depression, Fried et al. (2022)). The amount of dimensions can be assessed through exploratory factor analysis (EFA),
categorical principal component analysis loading plots, and item response theory (Mair et al., 2018). If items or more
specific constructs have strong associations to certain latent variables and not to others, then each latent variable can
be seen as a homogeneous construct. These could be grouped into a multidimensional scale (e.g., personality, job
performance). A network approach does not need to assume latent variables and can assess dimensionality through
community detection of observed variables. NLP example: The dimensionality of a lexicon can be assessed by encoding
the tokens with embeddings and clustering, where the amount of clusters (i.e., construct dimensions) would have the
highest average silhouette score. Embeddings could also be used in factor analysis. Features of a trained model could
also be evaluated similarly.
Reliability. The consistency of a measure depends on the condition under which it is evaluated 7 :
(a) Parallel-forms reliability and intra-method reliability. Consistency of responses of a given individual to
two versions of the same questionnaire that use different items (Van der Wal et al., 2022). NLP example: If we
consider trained models as individuals, slightly changing prompts to a generative AI LLM can result in very different
performance (Sanh et al., 2021; Shu et al., 2023b; Van der Wal et al., 2022). Testing sensitivity of a model to
reinitializing random weights or parameters (Hoyle, Goel, Sarkar, & Resnik, 2022; Van der Wal et al., 2022).
(b) Test-retest (across time). Measuring same individuals using same test across time points. When this is done by
a same rater over time, it is called intra-rater reliability. As with prospective validity, this will depend on whether
construct is expected to change (i.e., trait) or remain stable over time (i.e., state). NLP example: An NLP model

Which of these properties should be evaluated will depend on the goals of the study. Ideally, a measurement is
demonstrated to be valid when a measurement outcome (e.g., change in thermometer) is caused by an intervention on
the target attribute (e.g., temperature) (Borsboom et al., 2004). This causal effect can be estimated through randomized
controlled trials or causal inference methods for observational data (Hernán & Robins, 2020). This causal perspective
on validity could discard many types of so-called validity approaches listed here as necessary to prove validity; in
particular, construct validity (i.e., the degree to which a test correlates with what it should and does not correlate with
what it should not) could be discarded Borsboom et al. (2009). Some of the properties we present may only demonstrate
some expected property of a valid assessment, but not prove validity themselves (e.g., construct validity). An NLP
example of this type of causal validity could be demonstrated by evaluating the validity of an NLP model that measures
state anxiety from text data: participants could be randomly assigned to a relaxation- or anxiety-inducing task and asked
immediately after to write about their affect –a valid score from this NLP model would be lower after the relaxation
task than the anxiety task. 8 Having said this, we expect researchers would want to evaluate most of these different
properties depending on their application, but might only evaluate some of them (e.g., standardization) after more basic
evaluation (criterion validity) is assessed. Our goal is to describe most properties that be of interest, especially for NLP
and psychology researchers who are less familiar with psychometrics or psychometricians who are less familiar with
NLP examples.

Study 1: assessing multiple types of mental health issues to evaluate concurrent criterion
validity and content validity

Here, we present a use case to demonstrate how to train and validate these different types of NLP methods to detect
a diverse set of psychological constructs in two datasets: (a) issues discussed during de-identified Crisis Text Line
conversations, as annotated by the crisis counselor immediately following the conversation ; and (b) similar types of
issues from Reddit (described in section 5.1). The semantic variety of issues –from suicide to bullying to gender/sexual
identity– should allow us to evaluate the sensitivity of the models to these different types of constructs. Demonstrating
that an NLP model can detect what counselors coded as the conversation happened is a form of concurrent criterion

Models

LIWC-22 lexicon: LIWC is a lexicon that counts the proportion of tokens related to 117 categories including semantic
constructs (family, anger) and nonsemantic linguistic features (1st person pronouns, unique words).
Suicide Risk Lexicon (SRL): this lexicon contains tokens (words and phrases) associated with 49 different risk factors
for suicidal thoughts and behaviors (STBs) including direct-self injury, hopelessness, and discrimination (Low et al.,
2024). It has been shown to surpass LIWC and offers higher content validity (i.e., more related constructs) than LIWC
for language around psychological issues and imminent risk. Initial tokens were built using GPT-4 turbo and later
validated by clinicians. Counts are normalized by document word count.
LIWC-22 and SRL were trained using logistic regression. Hyperparameter tuning was done using Bayesian optimization
using the scikit-optimize package on a 5-fold cross-validation of the training set. Hyperparameter tuning of the Ridge
alpha parameter included values [0.001, 0.01, 0.1, 1, 10, 100].
Construct-Text similarity (CTS): We apply this method, as described in section 3.1, twice: once by building the
construct representation using only one prototypical token and again by using all prototypical tokens from the suicide
risk lexicon as construct’s tokens; prototypical tokens are those that scored 3 out of 3 by all clinicians in how much
they represent a given construct (e.g., "anorexia" is very prototypical of eating disorders while "didn’t eat much" is less
so). We tokenize the document into independent clauses (with subject and predicate) to capture more minimal units
of meaning than sentences separated by punctuation. We compute the maximum similarity to capture the document
token with the highest similarity to any prototypical lexicon token. We then min-max scale the output of the cosine
similarities between 0 and 1 to match the scale of other models.
Open-source LLM Gemma instruct models: Gemma 7B is an open-source generative LLM that was high-performing
on multiple tasks involving reasoning, question-answering, math and science, and coding at the time of analysis (Team
et al., 2024). Gemma 2B is a smaller version that is expected to underperform but can run efficiently on a personal
CPU unlike its larger counterpart. These models were downloaded onto our private and secure computer cluster. We
provided the prompt displayed in Appendix Figure 9. We did inference using Gemma 2B-it on a single NVIDIA
GeForceGTX1080Ti GPU for 10 hours and Gemma 7B-it on a single QUADRORTX6000 for 16 hours.

Proprietary LLM Google Gemini 2.0 flash & OpenAI GPT-4o models. These models are among the highest
performing models at the time of analysis 9 . Therefore, we used these for public Reddit data but not Crisis Text Line
data. They also allow for a prompt that includes the entire codebook which reduces API requests considerably (see
Figure 10). We randomized the order of the constructs in the prompt for every submission to avoid an ordering bias.
To encourage the model to select the most highly predicted tokens, the temperature —a parameter controlling output

To evaluate content validity quantitatively, we introduce content validity test sets: lists of tokens judged by experts to be
highly prototypical of the construct of interest. Prototypical tokens are those that first come to mind when thinking of
a construct (“I’m a burden” or “I’m a nuisance” for burdensomeness) –given a specific definition– and have fewest
features in common with other constructs (Low et al., 2024). Prototypical tokens should not lead to many false positives
where the the person is not expressing the construct (e.g., “I’m anxious” for burdensomeness). While these tokens
are likely to appear in a large enough test set, their relative frequency will be different, as depicted in Figure 5. A
random test set will evaluate a model’s ability to generalize to real, unseen data; while a content validity test set will
answer the question: how many of the obviously prototypical tokens can the model detect? These lists can be obtained
from publicly available lists made by experts (e.g. symptoms in DSM-5) or more rigorously by creating a lexicon and
validating it with clinicians, as done with the Suicide Risk Lexicon (SRL; Low et al. (2024)) 10 . We would expect
high-performing models to capture all prototypical tokens, especially those scored 3 out of 3 in clinician evaluations
of prototypicality as we test here. Therefore, the metric of choice is sensitivity: out of the prototypical tokens how
many can the model correctly predict? While traditional test sets include non-obvious positive cases making a perfect
prediction of 1 very difficult, we expect all models to have a perfect sensitivity close to 1 in detecting obvious tokens,
especially those built using SRL (SRL and CTS). These content validity test sets provide a model-agnostic method
of evaluating content validity, which can also serve as an explainability tool (i.e., what information does the model
capture?). Fang and cols. (2022) Fang et al. (2022) evaluated content validity of text embeddings through probing
experiments: text is encoded with a given embeddings model x (e.g., BERT) and with an interpretable feature f (e.g.,
sentence length); the embedding is assumed to contain the interpretable information if it can predict it, a common
approach in explainable machine learning. However, this does not answer whether a model captures a representative set
of prototypical expressions, which may be closer to the definition of content validity, and we try to demonstrate through
our complementary approach, which unlike probing is dataset-independent and works for evaluating models and not
just embeddings.

Study 2: Predicting whether to deploy an emergency service intervention for imminent

532

The goal of this second use case is to present a more challenging NLP task: predicting a low-base rate phenomenon:
whether the outcome of the conversation resulted in requesting an emergency services intervention, resulting in an
imbalanced dataset. Furthermore, we will predict this outcome using earlier parts of the conversation, which is a form
of prospective criterion validity. We show how different classification metrics improve as more messages are included.
We finish by providing an algorithmic explanation (i.e., feature importance), that is, revealing which risk factors are
most predictive of the outcome which can help inform theory.

During Crisis Text Line conversations analyzed, counselors assessed for suicidal thoughts, suicidal plans, access to
means, and imminent 48-hour timeframe, in that order (see Low et al. (2024) for more details). If a texter is determined
to be at a high risk by the crisis counselor, the counselor will involve a supervisor. The supervisor evaluates whether the
texter is in immediate danger and unable to create a safety plan or deescalate, in which case the supervisor may contact
local emergency services. To better dissociate the likely highest risk group in the dataset, we created two groups: (a)
suicidal desire without plans, means, or timeframe, and (b) cases where an emergency service intervention was initiated,
which has all symptoms present and cannot be fully deescalated. Such models could speed up risk assessments (e.g.,
alert the supervisor earlier), give a second opinion to the counselor and supervisor, or, after appropriate validation, be
used in a different setting where there is no counselor (e.g., social media). We balanced the training set by downsampling
suicidal desire cases to the imminent risk sample size to avoid the model over-predicting values closer to the majority
value, resulting in a training set of 3500 (half in each group). The test set included a total of 2358 cases, with 2152
suicidal desire cases without plans, means, or timeframe cases (91% of test set) and 206 emergency services intervention
cases (9% of test set).

Discussion

583

We have introduced Text Psychometrics as an important area of research to improve the validity and reliability of
NLP methods being developed for psychological assessments in text data. We reviewed how to validate models that
use NLP to assess psychological constructs in text through examples and through two original studies. In particular,
we have provided a novel method for evaluating content validity using lexicons and expanded a similarity-based text
classification method which we call construct-text similarity which is interpretable and lightweight. Next we help guide
researchers in choosing the optimal NLP method given their research and dataset constraints.

7.1

In Figure 7 we summarize the tradeoffs of different NLP methods as a function of different, key desirable properties.
This is a qualitative analysis based on our results and prior studies. The first point to make is that using LIWC or an
existing lexicon is rarely a good choice given it generally has lower predictive performance compared to other methods.
We also argued it lacks content validity at face value since it does not generally capture the main target constructs of
interest in any given study (Low et al., 2024); furthermore, when used to detect a given construct, the resulting model
misses many prototypical tokens of the construct as shown with the content validity test sets (Table 2). An alternative is
building one’s own lexicon, and one can have an LLMs generate a draft lexicon as a starting point (Low et al., 2024).
This is likely to achieve improved performance (criterion validity) and higher content validity. Custom lexicons can
be used directly without training a model (zero-shot) in the sense that one can count whether their tokens appear in a
document without training a model, although it is unlikely to perform very well. It is better to train a model to learn
how much to weigh these counts to detect the dependent variable. The main limitaitons with lexicons is they cannot
match tokens that are similar to the ones they have ("suicide attempt" will not match "attempted suicide") and they lack
awareness of how a word’s context might be associated with the target variable (’not sad’, ’very sad’, ’sort of sad’),

Recent LLMs perform well zero-shot, can be fine-tuned for improved performance, and can be requested to provide

scenarios. Other approaches (BERT-type models) can be used in these settings for deterministic results as an alternative
or used alongside generative LLMs.
Overall, lexicons or construct-text similarity might be prioritized over other deep learning approaches when (a) an
interpretable is preferred for high-stakes decisions or scientific inquiry (perhaps used in tandem with LLMs to guarantee
capturing tokens an LLM might miss); and (b) when models that might result in higher predictive performance are
challenging to train or use due to lack of labeled data, computational resources, or data-privacy requirements that forbid
sharing data with a LLM API or cost (e.g., running LLMs for millions of electronic health records). LLMs are already
dominating other methods given their performance is extremely high. LLMs will gain even more traction once their size
decrease in order to run more easily on personal computers and their self-explanations are deemed not only plausible,
but also faithful (i.e., true) (Agarwal et al., 2024). More mechanistic interpretability methods and understanding for
LLMs are also being developed (Lieberum et al., 2024; Lindsey et al., 2025).

Are text-based measurements objective if they predict a subjective criterion? Moving beyond the
convergence with rating scales.

It is commonly thought that a biomarker (digital speech pattern, neural circuit, genetic marker) or behavioral marker
(language sentiment) are objective because they can measure a variables automatically, that is, they are more objective
than any one human’s subjective opinion. However, can these markers be considered objective if they are predicting
subjective variables such as a self-report questionnaire? A first consideration is that the target labels may come not
from self-report but from the opinion of experts who provided the labels, who may use more objective criteria than
nonexperts. The label may be a gold-standard that is relatively objective, such as a diagnosis of vocal-fold paralysis
through video endoscopy Low, Rao, Randolph, Song, and Ghosh (2023). A model that accurately predicts expert
judgements could be more objective than most non-expert humans. Second, if a model is biased or unfair, such as one
19

Low et al. (2024). Text Psychometrics.

632
633
634
635
636
637
638
639

that unjustly uses proxies for race to grant or deny parole to incarcerated individuals (Mayson, 2018), it could in theory
be improved or corrected through bias mitigation methods (Hort et al., 2023). This model could end up being more
objective than the average judge. Therefore, by increasing the amount of training datasets and correcting over time,
a model might become more objective than the humans who provided annotations in any one of its training datasets.
Self-supervised models such as GPT models achieve this even without an external criterion: it uses next-word prediction
of the own training data on vast amounts of diverse corpora to allow models to gain rich representations useful in having
better predictions than many humans in a variety of downstream tasks, especially when combined with reinforcement
learning with human feedback (Bai et al., 2022).

660

Nevertheless, given text-based measures often predict subjective target variables, it might be important to evaluate
the validity of a text-based measurement beyond assessing its convergence to a subjective rating scale (Kjell et al.,
2023). Several studies have compared text models to rating scales without having to train the text model to optimize the
prediction of a self-report rating scale variable. One study (Kjell, Kjell, Garcia, & Sikström, 2019) took the pictures
of facial expressions of different emotions done by actors (happy, sad, or contemptuous, taken as the ground-truth)
and randomly assigned participants to either describe each expression with words or rate each stimulus using rating
scales for the corresponding expression (e.g., 1 = low to 5 = high happiness). Then both types of responses (i.e., text
versus rating scale) were used to predict the ground-truth emotion category and the text-based model showed higher
performance. Therefore, these types of analyses allow text-based models to independently be compared to rating scales
using criterion validity of a ground truth. Another study (Sikström, Nicolai, Ahrendt, Nevanlinna, & Stille, 2024)
first had one set of individuals write about a series of internal states (depression, anxiety); these narratives are safely
assumed to be about those states (ground-truth). Then they had a second set of individuals use relevant rating scales
(PHQ-9, GAD-7) to assign scores to those narratives. Then models were trained to predict the states from either the
features extracted from the narratives or from the rating-scale scores and the text models outperformed the rating scales
in detecting emotional states. However, going forward, comparing the utility of natural language and rating scales
should be based on multiple desirable psychometric properties, consider the trade-offs in the time it takes to produce
scores with rating scales and narratives, and how well a researcher can measure a specific construct of interest using
both methods; for instance, perhaps heterogeneous constructs such as depression could be better assessed through
natural language in order to capture nuanced and diverse symptoms, but a more specific construct like hopelessness
is better assessed through a quick ordinal rating scales. Because if the person does not mention hopelessness in their
response, this does not mean they are not experiencing it, which could quickly be assessed with a rating scale.

As NLP-based assessments improve, it might be possible for users to assess individuals without their consent. This
could lead to spreading private information or rejecting individuals during jobs or insurance applications based on model
predictions. European legislation has proposed individuals have a right to an explanation on high-stakes algorithmic
decisions, which would make these malicious practices illegal (European Commission, 2023). As models become
better, users may not doubt models’ outputs, which is especially problematic if we trust AI to evaluate AI (section
7.5.3). If measurement is done via AI chatbots then all the known risks of generative AI hold: hallucinations; suicide
encouragement; non-evidence-based medical recommendations; disinformation; illegal, toxic, and discriminatory
content; leakage of personal information or copyrighted material from the training set; and plagiarism (Weidinger et al.,
2021). Standardized AI safety benchmarks are being used to assess LLMS (Liang et al., 2022). Specific questions could
be posed to a model to determine whether it will output harmful content such as suicide methods. Furthermore, training,
fine-tuning, and particularly running inference with LLMs can produce a substantial impact on the environment, and
reducing this impact is an active area of research (Chien et al., 2023).

In Appendix B we briefly reviewed factor analysis, item response theory, networks, and causal inference. Other

Multiple methods could be applied to improve performance including few-shot or exemplar prompting (i.e., including

provide a step-by-step explanation in the prompt before providing the answer), ensemble refinement (e.g., include
multiple chain-of-thought responses through temperature sampling in the prompt for the final answer), sampling
different hyperparameters (temperature, top-k, and nucleus), and fine-tuning using efficient methods such as QLoRA
(Dettmers et al., 2024; Singhal et al., 2023).

Future directions: exploring other models, other NLP tasks, and AI evaluating AI

Other psychometric methods

Other large language model methods.

Other NLP tasks.

RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation) is an approach to improve generative AI giving them access to external
databases (Gao et al., 2023). Even though LLMs are originally trained on large corpora, they will likely not have
information that is proprietary or novel and, therefore, can benefit from searching through new data using semantic
textual similarity and other techniques. There are many metrics and benchmarks that evaluate different properties of the
retrieved information (Gao et al., 2023). Applying item-response theory (IRT) could help evaluate whether the system
adapts appropriately to varying levels of question difficulty or ambiguity.
Chatbots. Evaluating the quality of a chatbot will require similar metrics as we have used, but also ethical considerations
as well as metrics related to user engagement and conversation length. Fully automated chatbots are likely pose serious
risks in high-stakes scenarios such as crisis counseling. A desirable property for a chatbot could be long-term memory
of the interactions with the user, which may help the system retrieve details of the user over time and over longer
documents. Another may be to have a reliable personality (Shu et al., 2023b).
Agentic AI. AI agents can take human input via prompts in order to interact with a device and take actions. Examples
include virtual assitants that can set an alarm; AI research assistants that can search for articles, analyze data, and
draft manuscripts; and code assistants that can generate and test scripts (Acharya, Kuppan, & Divya, 2025). They
are typically evaluated using task completion metrics, benchmark suites (e.g., BabyAGI, AutoGPT), and qualitative
human feedback on their goal achievement and adaptability. Incorporating psychometrics could enhance evaluation by
modeling latent behavioral traits and tracking their evolution over time, task type, complexity, and novelty.
LLM-as-a-judge: AI evaluating AI for automated psychometrics. If AI is similar or better than humans at a given
task, then AI could be the ground truth criterion we try to predict, perhaps to validate smaller, weaker, faster, or cheaper
models. This is known as LLM-as-a-judge (Zheng et al., 2024). The gemma models we used incorporate this during
training by having a larger, higher capability model express choose between responses it produces (Team et al., 2024).
Models could choose the best hyperparameters of another model for training, suggest adjustments during training,
suggest how to adapt trained models to new data, generate synthetic training data to improve measurement, continually
21

Low et al. (2024). Text Psychometrics.

monitor predictions once deployed, and run tests to improve predictions. It is feasible that AI could one day help
demonstrate all the desirable properties we outlined in section 4 on its own. However, generative AI has been shown
to carry different types of racial and gender biases (Zack et al., 2024) as well as methodological biases (Zheng et al.,
2024), which should be carefully evaluated and mitigated.

Conclusion

751

We have tried to promote Text Psychometrics as a field that studies how to assess psychological constructs in text. We
first presented different types of validity and desirable properties that could be evaluated for text-based assessments.
We then systematically compared a diverse set of models including lexicons, zero-shot construct-text similarity, and
zero-shot generative AI on different desirable properties in their ability to predict differents types of mental health
issues and imminent suicide risk. We argue there is a hyperfocus on criterion validity (e.g., out-of-sample prediction
of a gold-truth label). We introduced content validity test sets as a way to demonstrate whether models miss any key
prototypical expressions of the target variable. This helps us demonstrate that some models can have good criterion
validity (e.g., ROC >0.75) but miss many prototypical tokens (e.g., content validity sensitivity < 0.80). In a second
use case, we predicted the need for a future emergency service intervention (i.e., prospective criterion validity) and
uncovered which risk factors most predicted the need for an emergency service intervention (e.g., lethal means for
suicide more than depressed mood or hopelessness). We finish by providing a comparison of desirable properties
to select models depending on the constraints different researchers may have (Figure 7.1). We hope this study will
encourage broader validation of these promising NLP methods in psychology, medicine, and the social sciences.

Correlation does not imply prediction

780

A key methodological distinction between psychology and NLP is in the definition of prediction and association. While
it is common knowledge that correlation (or more generally, association) does not imply causation, it is less known in
psychology that correlation does not imply prediction. In machine learning, prediction entails training a model from a
set of samples and labels, and then testing the model on new samples the model did not observe. It is common in
psychology to refer to within-sample associations (e.g., correlations, within-sample regressions) as predictions;
however, a model fit using the entire dataset may not generalize well when predicting new individual samples (Yarkoni
& Westfall, 2017). Therefore, it is useful to distinguish associations or explanations (i.e., within-sample testing) from
predictions (i.e., out-of-sample testing) as defined in machine learning and natural language processing. Moreover,
many have suggested to place more focus on prediction in psychology and related fields (Poldrack, Huckins, &
Varoquaux, 2020; Shmueli, 2010; Yarkoni & Westfall, 2017). Prediction is a better test of generalizability, that is the
ability for an instrument to be reliable across different demographics, measurement instruments, models, hardware, and
time (related to the concepts of external validity and transferability).

Here we review how psychometrics has dealt with modeling psychological assessments throughout its history. From a
classical test theory perspective, X = T + E (the true score model) where X is the observed score, T is the true
unknown score and E is the unknown error between the two (Mair et al., 2018). A good quality measurement should
show consistency among repeated attempts to measure the same thing, which implies observations X match true scores
T (i.e., reliable scores, free from error across observations). Differences in repeated attempts could be due to
measurement error; seasonal effects; the episodic, oscillating nature of the construct; carryover effects (i.e., people
remember their answers if the time-lapse is short). Since true scores and errors are unknown, many different attempts at
estimating reliability have been developed which involve either correlating multiple test administrations (i.e., parallel
forms reliability, test-retest reliability) or calculating reliability across items of a single test administration (internal
consistency) (McNeish, 2018). Instead of assuming there is one source of error as in the classical true score model,
Cronbach (1972) (Cronbach, 1972) proposed generalizability theory to account for multiple sources of error (i.e.,
facets) including items, raters, and measurement instances (Mair et al., 2018). A test can reliably return the same score,
but be measuring the wrong attribute, that is, not be valid; whereas, a test cannot logically be both valid and unreliable.
Item response theory (IRT, a.k.a., modern test theory) was later developed to account for the fact that different items
have different difficulty and discrimination (i.e., how well an item can differentiate between individuals with different
levels of the trait) (De Ayala, 2013). IRT achieves this by modeling the probability of a person’s response to an item as
a function of the person’s ability and the item characteristics. This can provide more flexible, efficient, and informative
testing than classical test theory approaches. When applied to biomarkers or behavioral makers (e.g., allostatic load
from blood biomarkers, a psychological construct from natural language), IRT can help identify individual variables
that are most informative and least informative for developing a risk assessment score, and determine if individual
variables provide information along the entire risk score continuum, or if they provide information for only a portion of
the continuum (Liu, Juster, Dams-O’Connor, & Spicer, 2021).
A limitation of IRT is that it assumes a specific causal structure where observed items (e.g., sadness, anhedonia, fatigue)
are caused by an underlying latent variable (e.g., depression), that is, a reflective model (Edwards & Bagozzi, 2000).
However, as Fried (2017) (Fried, 2017) points out, psychological constructs do not seem to be natural kinds (e.g., gold,
water) that are discovered, unchanging, and exist independently of human measurement; rather they are constructed
(human-defined), mutable, and have no sharp boundaries. Once constructed, certain psychological constructs seem to
co-occur not necessarily because there is an underlying common cause (e.g., depression as a brain disorder causing
changes in passive indicators), but because they cause each other (e.g., each symptom having unique brain circuits that
affect each other without the need to consider a depression construct). Instead of a natural kind, this can be better
thought of a homeostatic property cluster and statistically modeled through networks (i.e., a graph of variables and
multivariate associations between them) (Borsboom, 2008; Borsboom et al., 2021; Cramer, Waldorp, Van Der Maas, &
Borsboom, 2010; Fried, 2017) instead of through latent variable models like IRT. Unlike traditional models that view
symptoms as passive outcomes of an underlying illness, the network approach sees symptoms as active contributors
that causally trigger and reinforce each other: symptoms do not merely signal the presence of a disorder—they are the
disorder (Roefs et al., 2022). For more on the history of psychometrics, see Jones and Thissen (2006).
A limitation of prior approaches is they do not prove causality as rigorously as structural causal models that use
directed acyclic graphs (Pearl, 2010) or as the potential outcomes framework Hernán and Robins (2020). Having said

this, the use of directed acyclic graphs is challenging in psychology given acyclicality would rarely hold if constructs
are causing each other, but recent advances that drop this assumption have been proposed (Park, Ryan, & Waldorp,
2023). Another approach is to use dynamical systems to obtain closed-formed equations or computational models of
how constructs interact in a system Haslbeck, Ryan, Robinaugh, Waldorp, and Borsboom (2019); Robinaugh et al.
(2024); Ryan, Haslbeck, and Robinaugh (2023). This approach is promising because, as with networks, it better reflects

inference on the effect of CBT (which is practiced differently by different clinicians and has many active ingredients)
on panic disorder may be an ill-posed problem, which complex systems may model better (Ryan et al., 2023). From
this perspective, mental health disorders are systems that are stuck in negative stable states reinforced over time by
harmful causal interactions (anxiety > insomnia > fatigue > anxiety).

How to define measurement and validity?

849

A common view of measurement in psychology is that of assigning any type of numeral to a construct through some
function or rule (Krantz, Luce, Suppes, & Tversky, 1971). This definition broadly applies to many types of
relationships. For instance, the degree to which SAT scores is associated with future college success. So what
constitutes a valid measurement? A classic definition for validity states that a test is valid if it measures what it purports
to measure (Kelley, 1927). Borsboom et al. (2004) (Borsboom et al., 2004) argues that a test is valid for measuring an
attribute if the attribute exists and if variations in the attribute causally produce variations in the outcomes of the
measurement; if they do not, then perhaps one is measuring something different or nothing at all (Borsboom et al.,
2004). Markus and Borsboom (2013, p.54) (Markus & Borsboom, 2013) suggest it might therefore be more accurate to
think of measurement as assigning quantities related to a real attribute, which should be on at least an interval scale. If
the difference between a score at the 10th percentile and a score 1 point above is not the same as the difference between
a score at the 90th percentile and a score 1 point above (i.e., it is not interval), then changes in the real attribute assessed
would not create precise changes in the score assigned as occurs in more natural attributes such as temperature and
well-validated measurement tools such as thermometers. Furthermore, measurement is prototypically asymmetric: a
thermometer measures temperature, but temperature does not measure a thermometer’s value given the logical causal
direction between the two. In this sense, finding that SAT scores correlate with GPA in college is statistically
symmetric, so perhaps should not be considered a case of measurement but of prediction, association, or testing.
Therefore, Markus and Borsboom (2013) (Markus & Borsboom, 2013) definition of measurement would not apply to
most psychological assessments.

Formal description of text classification

As described in (Riezler & Hagmann, 2022), first, a label y for a raw text input x is provided by a human annotator or
algorithm. Then a machine learning model pθ is trained (a function is learned) to map labels and inputs and predict
output ŷ where θ is the learned parameters. This can be formalized as ŷ = pθ (x) for a regression setting. In
classification settings, we use the maximum a posteriori prediction ŷ = argmaxy pθ (y|x) where the category yi with the
maximum value is taken among possible categories y. Measuring psychological constructs in text can be done for
binary or multi-class (i.e., multinomial) classification (i.e., categorical output variable) to answer questions such as "Is
this text document about construct c?"; through multi-label classification to answer questions such as "Which of these
constructs c ∈ C are present?"; as well as through regression (which in machine learning refers to predicting ordinal or
metric output variables) to answer questions such as "Can I detect the severity or degree that construct c is present in a
person from a sample of their text?".
