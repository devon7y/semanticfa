Effective educational measurement relies heavily on
the curation of well-­designed item pools. However,
item calibration is time consuming and costly,
requiring a sufficient number of respondents to
estimate the psychometric properties of items. In this
study, we explore the potential of six different large
language models (LLMs; GPT-­3.5, GPT-­4, Llama 2,
Llama 3, Gemini-­Pro and Cohere Command R Plus)
to generate responses with psychometric properties
comparable to those of human respondents. Results
indicate that some LLMs exhibit proficiency in College
Algebra that is similar to or exceeds that of college
students. However, we find the LLMs used in this
study to have narrow proficiency distributions, limiting
their ability to fully mimic the variability observed
in human respondents, but that an ensemble of
LLMs can better approximate the broader ability
distribution typical of college students. Utilizing item
response theory, the item parameters calibrated by
LLM respondents have high correlations (eg, >0.8 for
GPT-­3.5) with their human calibrated counterparts.
Several augmentation strategies are evaluated
for their relative performance, with resampling
methods proving most effective, enhancing the
Spearman correlation from 0.89 (human only) to 0.93
(augmented human).
KEYWORDS
data augmentation, item response theory, large language
models, psychometric analysis, simulation

Practitioner notes
What is already known about this topic
• The collection of human responses to candidate test items is common practice in
educational measurement when designing an assessment tool.
• Large language models (LLMs) have been found to rival human abilities in a
variety of subject areas, making them a low-­cost option for testing the efficacy of
educational assessment items.
• Data augmentation using AI has been an effective strategy for enhancing machine
learning model performance.
What this paper adds
• This paper provides the first psychometric analysis of the ability distribution of a
variety of open-­source and proprietary LLMs as compared to humans.
• The study finds that item parameters similar to those produced by 50 undergraduate
respondents.
• Using LLM respondents to augment human response data yields mixed results.
Implications for practice and/or policy
• The moderate performance of LLM respondents by themselves suggests that they
could provide a low-­cost option for curating quality items for low-­stakes formative
or summative assessments.
• This methodology offers a scalable way to evaluate vast amounts of generative
AI-­produced items.

I NTRO DUCTI O N
Generating sets of well-­functioning items for mathematics assessments often requires multiple iterations of calibration, involving extensive human participation. One of the most common techniques used in building an item pool is item response theory (IRT), where ample
response-­level data are typically required for accurate item calibration and scaling (Kim &
Kolen, 2019; König et al., 2021). This process is time consuming and costly and significantly
limits the rapid adaptation of educational assessments to different sets of students. For
example, the Program for International Student Assessment (PISA) main survey requires
between N = 250 and N = 750 respondents per item per country (Mazzeo & Davier, 2014).
Thus, the time and cost involved in obtaining responses from human respondents remain a
significant area of resource expenditure.
With the advent of advanced AI technologies, novel ways to address these challenges
have arisen. Recent developments in large language models (LLMs) are achieving
near-­human performance (Liu et al. 2023; Chang et al., 2024; Katz et al., 2024; OpenAI
et al., 2024), leading to speculation about whether they can competently generate high-­
fidelity synthetic data without the traditional need for full data collection (Ding et al., 2024;
Whitehouse et al., 2023; Ye et al., 2024). In our domain, we explore whether the capabilities of LLMs can be leveraged to provide responses resulting in psychometric properties
similar to those derived from human respondents' answers. Since a single LLM might not
have the full potential to resemble humans, as revealed from previous work in the literature (He-­Yueya et al., 2024), we additionally explore what the most cost-­effective strategy

to gather partial human data and augment it with synthetic data is. This research is guided
by three critical research questions:
• RQ1: Which language model or configuration of language models best mimic human respondent abilities in mathematics, as measured by IRT?
• RQ2: How do the psychometric properties of items fit to human respondents compare to
those fit to LLM respondents?
• RQ3: Can the augmentation of human respondent data with LLM respondent contributions yield item parameters comparable to those obtained from solely increasing human
data collection?
If we are able to successfully utilize LLM respondents to simulate or augment human
data collection, it would mean that questions, including those produced via generative AI
(Bhandari et al., 2024), could be tested and evaluated en masse nearly instantly for use in a
variety of educational contexts, such as computer tutoring systems and other formative and
summative assessment scenarios.
In this study, we investigate the capabilities of various LLMs, including GPT-­4 (OpenAI
et al., 2024), GPT-­3.5 (Brown et al., 2020), Llama 3 (Dubey et al., 2024), Llama 2 (Touvron
et al., 2023), Gemini-­
Pro (Gemini Team Google, 2024) and Cohere Command R Plus
(Cohere, 2023), to generate assessment responses. Specifically, we prompt each model
with 20 items sourced from the OpenStax Creative Commons textbook for College Algebra,
producing 150 responses per model. Our analysis focuses on assessing whether these models can effectively replicate the response characteristics of our target population, which consists of undergraduate students in the United States, and on comparing the performance of
these models against each other. To this end, we compare the model-­generated responses
to those obtained from U.S. undergraduates on the popular crowdsourcing platform, Prolific.
We also evaluate whether an ensemble of these LLMs can better fit the response characteristics of human respondents utilizing an augmentation procedure. Specifically, we use data
from 50 human respondents and incorporate the closest LLM-­generated respondent to each
human in our dataset. Based on the proportions of each LLM in this dataset, we experiment
with randomly selecting 50 synthetic respondents according to these proportions and then
expand this selection to 100 LLM respondents.

R EL ATED WO R K
This section introduces data simulation and augmentation, both of which involve generating
synthetic data. Section “Simulated data in educational measurement and educational data
mining” discusses fully synthetic data generation for scenarios without human data, while
section “Data augmentation” addresses augmentation, where synthetic data supplements
partially collected human data for cost-­effectiveness. Finally, we explore the relationship
between automatic item generation and simulated/augmented data.

Simulated data in educational measurement and educational
data mining
Analyzing examinee responses to test questions is indispensable in the field of measurement. While gathering real data can be time consuming, costly and often incomplete,
simulation is a useful and economical technique since it can usually be done on a laptop without additional costs. Therefore, researchers commonly use simulation to validate

models (Swaminathan et al., 2006), compare different models (Harwell et al., 1996) and
evaluate estimation methods (Wang & Vispoel, 1998). In fact, among a random sample of
publications in the field, 60% of the studies use simulation, while the ratio for real data is just
41% (Feinberg & Rubright, 2016). Typically, a respondent proficiency distribution is specified (usually normal distribution), and then the item response-­level data (ie, dichotomous or
polytomous response) are simulated according to the distribution. The authentic student response data (ie, students' open-­ended answers for constructed response items or students'
choice for selected response items) are ignored and not generated during the process. To
sum up, the primary reason for conducting simulation studies in the pre-­GenAI era was to
allow researchers to compare estimated parameters against their respective true parameters, which are unknown in real data applications (Feinberg & Rubright, 2016).
Now, thanks to advancements in generative AI, we can simulate response-­level data
and potentially gain insights into the cognitive structures underlying the response process.
While most simulation work in educational settings is based on dialogue (Markel et al., 2023;
Shaikh et al., 2024), some researchers are focusing on item response-­level data simulation. For example, Xu and Zhang (2023) demonstrated the possibility of simulating student
behaviour based on assessment history. He-­Yueya et al. (2024) showed how fully synthetic
responses generated by GPT could help calibrate items. Lu and Wang (2024) used insights
from teachers to create generative student profiles and then used the outcomes of these
generative students to guide item development.
In the realm of educational data mining, gathering real learner data can pose privacy
concerns (Hutt et al., 2023) and present challenges with the costs of managing logged data
(Jacob et al., 2015). To address these challenges and evaluate the fidelity of student models, researchers have at times leveraged simulated data (Badrinath et al., 2021; Beheshti
et al., 2012; Desmarais, 2012; Desmarais & Pelczer, 2010; Ding et al., 2024; Hu et al., 2022;
Ndukwe et al., 2018). LLMs are often used to generate the training datasets needed to train
and test other models. For example, by using pseudocode to generate synthetic datasets,
researchers have been able to develop test cases of teaching activities to inform the development of a teaching outcome model (TOM) (Ndukwe et al., 2018). Similarly, researchers
have proposed using LLMs as data annotators to create synthetic data that can be used to
train other models (Ding et al., 2024), mimicking the framework of teacher–student learning
(TSL) (Hu et al., 2022). Simulated data have also been used within the educational data mining community to evaluate latent trait models (Badrinath et al., 2021; Beheshti et al., 2012).

Data augmentation
Data augmentation is a method often employed to increase the volume and diversity of
data, and it can also be applied to enlarge the training data and improve model performance
for various tasks (Cochran et al., 2022; Fang et al., 2023). Having a limited number of data
points often leads to weaker generalization capabilities, which can act as an obstacle to
the effectiveness of studies (Kieser et al., 2023). Thus, data augmentation is used to enrich
datasets and enhance their suitability for training models. For example, to augment image
data, techniques such as resizing, rotating and shifting images are frequently used (Shorten
& Khoshgoftaar, 2019; Xie et al., 2017). Researchers have also explored introducing noise
in LLM training data (Xie et al., 2017), adding audio tracks or temporal shifts in speech
recognition (Deng et al., 2000; Hannun et al., 2014), and leveraging Generative Adversarial
Networks (GANs) to generate training data for medical imaging (Frid-­Adar et al., 2018),
ultimately creating datasets that are more generalizable and effective.
Researchers often view data augmentation as a ‘task-­agnostic’ technique (ie, derived
from the training data itself) to reduce overfitting and thus facilitate the process of building

called Easy Data Augmentation (EDA) in which data are augmented in a text-­based dataset
through synonym replacement, random insertion, random swap and random deletion (Wei &

et al., 2019), making neural networks more adaptable to a variety of architectures and datasets, compared to widely known regularization methods such as weight decay and dropout
(Hernández-­García & König, 2018).

Automatic item generation
With the significant growth of open educational resources (OERs), researchers are
secondary beneficiaries, being provided with a corpus of educational resources at no cost
and open materials that can be freely distributed, remixed and adapted to create item pools
(Henderson & Ostashewski, 2018). In the classroom, teachers are able to utilize items from
OERs as homework or assessment problems (Wiley & Green, 2012). With the rise of LLMs,
many researchers have begun exploring utilizing the capabilities of LLMs to generate math
questions (Bhandari et al., 2024; CH & Saha, 2023; Keller, 2021; Lee et al., 2024; Onal &
Kulavuz- ­Onal, 2024; Shridhar et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2023), which can ultimately contribute
to the adaptability and scalability of OERs. Through these methods, while the speed of
item generation has greatly accelerated, the speed of evaluating the appropriateness and
effectiveness of these questions has not kept pace due to the manual process of gathering
responses and refining the items. Therefore, this paper aims to study the feasibility and
effectiveness of leveraging LLMs to simulate responses to items. This would allow for nimble
testing of LLM-­generated questions, expediting the creation of OERs for both classroom,
testing, and research purposes.

M ETHO DS
Model selection
We selected six LLMs to generate responses that simulate answers from undergraduate
college students in the U.S. to assessment questions. Our selection included GPT-­4, GPT-­
3.5, Llama 3, Llama 2, Gemini-­Pro and Cohere Command R Plus. These models were chosen for their varying levels of sophistication, reported accuracy on mathematics items and
widespread popularity, allowing us to simulate a broad spectrum of student abilities, from
lower to higher academic proficiency. For implementation, we utilized APIs for each model.
As Llama does not offer a direct API, we accessed Llama 2 and Llama 3 via the Replicate
API.1 We used the default sampling parameters for each model, which included non-­zero
temperature values.

Selection of items and prompt engineering
College Algebra was chosen as the subject because pre-­authored questions were available under a CC BY licence from an open textbook publisher, OpenStax.2 Additionally, we
used a dataset from an earlier study that calibrated its item pool and had already collected
responses from human participants via Prolific for 20 of the OpenStax College Algebra
questions in Lesson 2.2: Linear Equations in One Variable (Bhandari et al., 2024) using the
OATutor system (Pardos et al., 2023). This prior data collection contained some missingness
in the data, so we were able to effectively use N ≥ 99 for all items.
We distinctively formatted the questions, each prefixed with a label in the following format: ‘Q1: <question 1>’ followed by a double newline, then ‘Q2: <question 2>’, and continued this pattern for all 20 questions. The full prompt is shown in Appendix A. Specifically, the
questions were structured as follows:
Q1: Given m = 4, find the equation of the line in slope-­intercept form passing through the
point (2, 5).
Q2: Find the slope of a line that passes through the points (2, − 1) and (−5, 3).
Q20: For the following exercises, solve the equation for x. State all x-­values that are excluded from the solution set. 2 − 3 ∕ (x + 4) = (x + 2) ∕ (x + 4). Answer choices: Excluded
values: −4 and x = −3; Excluded values: 4 and x = −3.
We simulated 150 respondents for each LLM, as this number is equivalent to the effective number of human participants originally used to calibrate the item pool. The first
response produced by each model is provided in Appendix B. To assess the accuracy of
the responses, the second author of the study, who has extensive prior tutoring experience in College Algebra, manually graded them and noted the accuracy for each one. In
particular, a single response by an LLM provided answers to all 20 questions. The second
author of the paper scored each answer in the response using the OpenStax answer key,
preparing the dataset for IRT calibration. Answers were considered correct if it either
exactly matched the answer provided in the key or was an equivalent response (ie, can
be simplified or rearranged to the provided answer). This grading matched the method
used by the OATutor system (Pardos et al., 2023) and thus aligns with how the human
respondents were graded.

Augmentation procedure
In a real-­world case, sometimes only partial data are gathered. To explore the possibility
of augmenting the data, we treated each human respondent in our dataset as a centroid, using only 50 human responses to represent the partial data. We then identified
the nearest synthetic respondent from our pool of synthetically generated answers for
each human centroid, allowing us to map the AI responses directly onto the characteristics of individual human responses. To do this, we first created binary vectors representing the pattern of answers for each LLM respondent and human respondent across
all the questions (1 for correct and 0 for incorrect). Then, we computed the Euclidean
distances between each human respondent and LLM respondent using these binary
vectors.
Next, we conducted a resampling procedure to create a dataset from an ensemble of LLMs.
First, we identified the proportion of each LLM that was closest to the human respondents in the
initial set of 50. Then, using these proportions, we randomly selected 50 synthetic respondents,
ensuring that the proportions of each LLM model in this new subset reflected the percentages

IRT analysis
Contrary to sum-­score analysis or percentage correct metrics, we plan to use IRT to estimate
the latent ability of human and LLM respondents. This method has several advantages over
sum-­score analysis (DeMars, 2010). First, IRT assumes a latent trait theta, transforming
all estimations onto a logit scale instead of the sum-­score scale, which greatly improves
measurement precision. Second, IRT provides person-­level fit data, which can be done
independently of other respondents. Lastly, and most relevant to our purpose, IRT maps
both persons and items onto the same scales, enabling equating to be carried out without
assuming population score distributions. In fact, IRT equating may be the best method when
tests of differing difficulties are given to non-­random groups of examinees who differ in
ability (Cook & Eignor, 1991).
The simplest IRT model is often called the Rasch model or the one-­parameter logistic
model (1PL). Packages like TAM (Robitzsch et al., 2020) and mirt (Chalmers, 2012) in the R
environment can help carry out the analysis. The probability of individuals responding to a
binary item (ie, True/False) is determined by the individual's trait level and the difficulty of the
item, which is often presented as:

A significant merit of using the Rasch model is that the estimates of latent trait and difficulty are mapped onto the same logit scale. The logits are interval units, which make the
Wright Map (also known as the item-­person map) a useful tool for presenting both item difficulties and person abilities arranged along the same logit scale. The location of item difficulty denotes the ability level at which the individual has approximately a 50% probability
of answering the item correctly. The results from human calibration using Rasch analysis
are shown in Figure 1. Items were ranked by their difficulties in ascending order from top
to bottom (Wilson, 2023).
IRT analysis was carried out for two purposes: First, we used the item parameters calibrated by human respondents as a standard so that the parameters for LLM respondents
can be estimated. By treating item parameters as fixed, we were able to compare the proficiency distribution of college students and LLM respondents (Kim, 2006). Second, separate
calibrations were carried out on human and AI respondent groups, informing us of the practicality of item calibration using AI-­generated data.

R ESULTS
LLM-­respondent simulation (RQ1 and RQ2)
The initial item parameters for the item pool were calibrated on a group of current college
students in the United States. Since multiple forms were used, there was missingness at
random in the data, effectively resulting in N ≥ 99 for all items, satisfying the basic requirement
for a Rasch analysis (Wang & Chen, 2005). The results are shown in Figure 1. We then fixed
the item parameters estimated from the model to obtain the proficiency estimates for the
six LLMs used in this study. Results showed that most LLM proficiency distributions had a
significant overlap with the human respondents. In particular, Llama 3 and GPT-­3.5 had the
highest mean proficiency distribution, which was higher than the human mean, indicating
these AI models' greater proficiency in College Algebra compared to the sampled college
students. The mean proficiency of GPT-­4 was comparable to the mean proficiency of human
respondents, while Cohere and Gemini were lower than the humans. Llama 2 was the worst
among all six models.
Besides the findings on the average proficiency of each model, an interesting observation was that the variability in proficiency distribution for LLM respondents (standard deviation ranging from 0.29 to 0.58) was greatly reduced compared to the human distribution
(SD = 0.98). Most of the studied GenAI distributions appear to be sharper than their human
counterparts (kurtosis larger than 3). Detailed distributional information can be found in
Table 1 and Figure 2. Further analysis might explore ways to increase the variability in proficiency and response patterns.
To increase generalizability and account for the fact that all the OpenStax items used
in this study have publicly available solutions that might appear in the training data of the
LLMs, we compared model performance on these items with a set of newly generated items
which do not have publicly available solutions in OpenStax. We find similarity in accuracies
and thus conclude that the presence of answers in the LLMs' training data does not alter the
results. Detailed results of this analysis are included in Appendix C, Table C1.
We also performed item calibration on fully simulated data, comparing it against human-­
calibrated data and AI-­calibrated data. Notably, the IRT estimated item difficulties showed
a significant correlation between AI-­generated and human-­generated data, especially for
GPT-­3.5 and GPT-­4 (Pearson 𝜌 > 0.7), as shown in Table 2. Rank correlation (ie, Spearman
correlation) is also reported since not all distributions had the same mean, and the difficulty

The resampling, LLM ensemble analysis resulted in a set with significant variation in the
proportions of each model used. GPT-­3.5 was the most prevalent, comprising 36% of the

To evaluate the relative performance of these three strategies, we conducted four experiments to test the effectiveness of different strategies on the item calibration process. The
benchmark performance of the calibration was set by the human respondents; namely, we
use all available data from human respondents to calibrate the item pool and gather item
parameter estimates. Each experiment was designed to explore a different augmentation

Experiment 1 We use only half the number of the respondent pool and do the calibration

only part of the intended respondents were collected.
Experiment 2 In addition to Experiment 1, we enlarge the data size by twice using augmentation strategies (N = 100).
Experiment 3 We use a mixture of human respondents and resampled ensemble data
in a 1:1 ratio (N = 100).
Experiment 4 We use resampled ensemble data, with a size equal to the number of the
benchmark dataset (N = 100).

D I SCUSS I O N A ND CO NCLUS I O NS
In this study, we explored six different LLMs (GPT-­3.5, GPT-­4, Llama 2, Llama 3, Gemini-­Pro
and Cohere Command R Plus) and various combinations using sampling methods to arrive
at psychometric properties similar to those from human respondents' answers. Our findings are structured around three key conclusions: the proficiency of LLMs in approximating
ability distributions (RQ1), item parameter correlation (RQ2) and the effectiveness of data
augmentation (RQ3). First, when comparing the proficiency between LLM respondents and
human respondents, the results showed that although some LLMs have comparable or even

opposed to the point estimates or percent-­correct scores reported in other studies. Second,
we compared the item parameters calibrated by AI respondents and human respondents,
finding a relatively high Spearman correlation of 0.87 for GPT-­3.5 and a lower 0.78 for GPT-­
4. Notably, GPT-­3.5 emerged as the most human-­like AI respondent, exhibiting Spearman
correlations within 0.02 of those from 50 human respondents. On the contrary, Llama 2
yielded a null correlation (r = −0.01) with the human counterpart. This is primarily due to
Llama 2's extremely poor performance on the 20 college algebra problems (accuracy less
than 13%), which provided little to no information for statistical inference on item parameters.
Finally, since no single LLM currently had the capability to represent humans by itself, we
explored ensembling approaches using three strategies. Among these, a mixture of human
respondents and fully resampled data in a 1:1 ratio (Experiment 3) provided the best result,
raising the Spearman correlation from 0.89 to 0.93. However, these augmentation results
were mixed; while Spearman and Pearson correlations improved by 0.04 and 0.01 respectively, this approach substantially increased the RMSE.
To summarize, when solely using LLM respondents, we suggest using GPT-­3.5 since
it provides the best approximation of ability distribution. If gathering a subset of human respondents is feasible, then an augmented approach with a mixture of human respondents
and fully resampled data in a 1:1 ratio (Experiment 3) would provide the best results.
Our findings hold much promise for the automatic curation of items for tutoring systems.
Simply put, it seems plausible to leverage AI respondents to curate an item pool that has
a desirable spread of difficulty. Given the performance of 150 AI respondents from GPT-­
3.5 closely mirroring that of 50 humans (Experiment 1), AI respondents could be used as
an initial filtering phase to reliably narrow down a larger item pool, and then have human
respondents further refine the selection using the more manageable subset of items. This
would significantly help optimize human resources, saving production time and money. For
classroom environments, this research may allow for efficient auditing of newly generated
questions, created by humans or AI, enabling a first-­pass quality check for assessments to
present in the classroom.

LI M ITATI O NS A N D FUTUR E WO R K
There were several limitations to our study. First, we only utilized a single College Algebra
lesson, which makes it difficult to generalize the results to other lessons within the same
subject or to different subject areas. Additionally, our OpenStax item pool consisted only of
questions without images, figures or tables because not all the LLMs support multimodal
capabilities. Furthermore, in our experiment, the original partial human dataset displayed
bimodality (Experiment 1), atypical of a respondent pool. Therefore, when augmenting the
data without resampling (Experiment 2), bimodality was also exhibited. While Experiment
3 mitigated this impact by using an effective resampling strategy, it would be beneficial to
utilize a pool of human respondents that portray a normal distribution from the start. Due to
leveraging prior data collection, we used the respondent sample size from their study, rather
than calculating what the effective size should actually be. We also did not examine how the
quality of partial data collection impacts the augmentation results. A natural extension of
this work would be to formalize the effectiveness of augmentation strategy across varying
levels of data quality and systematically quantify its performance while accounting for the
randomness in data collection. This may require multiple experiment replications to analyze
the distribution of performance improvements.

In the future, analyses should be conducted to determine how valid the estimated proficiencies are when derived from a measurement tool calibrated by augmentation experiments versus those from benchmark data. To address this, we should investigate whether
the structure of the tool with augmented respondents is the same as it is with the original
human population (Ahmad et al., 2016). Typically, this is done by investigating the reliability
of the measurement tool and conducting statistical analyses such as explanatory factor
analysis (EFA) or confirmatory factor analysis (CFA) (Arafat et al., 2016). Moreover, future
studies should examine the performance of the proposed method on item pools other than
College Algebra, despite the fact that algebra is considered a hard subject for GenAI. In
particular, most of our current GenAI models/configurations yield satisfactory results (with
the exception of Llama 2) in terms of creating synthetic data for algebraic items. Whether
the good performance in the simulation stage can still hold for other subject areas, and if
not, whether the data augmentation procedure can mitigate the issue by ensembling several
unsatisfactory GenAI responses together, remains to be answered.
The field should also aim to refine and expand methodologies to significantly improve
the accuracy of responses generated by the models. In our study, we presented all 20
questions in a single prompt. However, it may be beneficial to question the model independently for each item. Although our initial trials with this technique for GPT-­3.5 resulted
in degenerate outcomes, this method could be extended to other models to assess its effectiveness more comprehensively. Additionally, more sophisticated prompt engineering
techniques should be explored. Our prompt was simple and uniform across all models.
However, it may be advantageous to customize prompts based on the model to better suit
the specific strengths and design of each model. Incorporating few-­shot learning by including one or two example responses in the prompt may also be effective. While we used
the default sampling parameters for each model, further exploration should be conducted
on varying these sampling parameters (ie, setting the temperature to an unusually high
value). Finally, employing hallucination mitigation techniques such as self-­c onsistency
(Wang et al., 2022), in which the model is prompted multiple times for the same question and the modal response is selected, may help minimize erroneous outputs and thus
increase accuracy. This technique has proven effective in reducing hallucination rates
to near zero for answering mathematics questions (Pardos & Bhandari, 2024). Thus, expanding this technique, along with exploring other hallucination mitigation methods, may
prove beneficial.
