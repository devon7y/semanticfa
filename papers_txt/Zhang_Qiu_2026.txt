Abstract
Large language model embeddings show promise for modeling psychometric properties directly from
item text. This study provides a rigorous, condition-sensitive validation by testing whether the cosine
similarity of sentence embeddings predicts empirical inter-item correlations, and whether this
relationship is uniform across different psychological constructs. With a sample of 1010 participants, we
analyzed 463 item pairs nested within nine dimensions across four scales (assessing internet gaming
disorder, dark triad traits, aggression, and life satisfaction). A multilevel model was fitted with empirical
correlation as the outcome, LLM cosine similarity as a fixed effect, and random slopes and intercepts
across dimensions. Results confirmed a significant positive fixed effect, indicating that greater semantic
similarity predicts stronger empirical correlations. Crucially, significant random slope variance revealed
that the strength of this predictive relationship differed substantially across dimensions. Furthermore, a
strong negative correlation was found between the random intercepts and slopes, suggesting that for
constructs with higher baseline inter-item cohesion, semantic similarity plays a proportionally smaller
role. The findings offer conditional support for the theory that semantic overlap is a systematic source of
item covariance while highlighting the moderating role of the psychological construct itself.
Keywords: sentence embeddings, item correlations, multilevel modeling, computational
psychology

Can Large Language Model Embeddings Predict Test Item Correlations? A Multilevel Analysis
The covariance among items within a psychological scale is a fundamental psychometric
property that both defines and is defined by the underlying construct. Traditionally, this covariance is
assumed to arise primarily because all items share a relationship with the latent construct they aim to
measure (Streiner, 2003). Empirical and theoretical work suggests that a portion of this inter-item
covariance can be attributed to the semantic similarity between the items themselves (Ravenda et al.,
2025). A recent review (Arnulf et al., 2024) has also concluded that the relationship between variables is
often semantic instead of empirical. While traditionally estimated from large samples of human
responses, the emerging computational psychology paradigm posits that this covariance may, in part, be
derivable from the semantic representation of the items themselves.
Large Language Models (LLMs) offer a powerful, untapped resource for this endeavor,
generating dense semantic embeddings that quantitatively capture the meaning of items. These models
introduce new capabilities to follow instructions and be trained for diverse semantic matching tasks
based on user demands (Zhu et al., 2026). Specifically, LLMs map inputs to high-dimensional vectors
where points close in the embedding space correspond to similar meanings (Martínez et al., 2024).
Empirical research has demonstrated a significant correlation between the cosine similarities of these
embeddings and actual empirical correlations among items (Feraco & Toffalini, 2025). However, existing
research has yet to rigorously test whether this predictive relationship holds consistently across scales
with different internal structures (e.g., item pairs nested into dimensions).
Our study extends previous works by modeling the predictive relationship within a multilevel
framework. We analyzed item pairs from four scales, nesting them within their respective
subdimensions in a linear mixed model. This allows us to partition variance and assess whether
prediction strength differs significantly between a scale’s dimensions. Our aim is not merely to

Method
Participants
Human participants were recruited from local universities. After our screening process through
the lie detector questions, the final sample consisted of 1,010 valid questionnaires, including 695 male
students (68.81%; Mean age: 21.14 years, SD: 1.98, Range: 17-37) and 315 female students (31.19%;
Mean age: 21.44 years, SD: 2.21, Range: 18-32). Informed consent forms, detailing the study’s purpose,
the voluntary basis of participation, and the commitment to data privacy, were received from
participants.
Measures
Each participant completed the short-form Internet gaming disorder scale, the dirty dozen, the
aggression questionnaire, and the Life Satisfaction Scale. These scales encompassed both
unidimensional and multidimensional constructs, positive and negative traits, personality and behavior,
as well as diverse scoring methodologies.
The short-form Internet gaming disorder scale: This 9-item unidimensional scale (Pontes &
Griffiths, 2015) was developed directly from the DSM-5 diagnostic criteria for Internet gaming disorder.
Respondents indicate their agreement with statements describing gaming-related behaviors and
psychological consequences on a 5-point scale (1 = strongly disagree, 5 = strongly agree). The
Cronbach’s alpha of the unidimensional scale in the current study is .928.
The dirty dozen: The dirty dozen scale (Jonason & Webster, 2010) was employed to assess the
dark triad traits. This 12-item scale comprises three 4-item subscales measuring Machiavellianism,
Psychopathy, and Narcissism. Respondents rated their agreement with each statement on a 7-point

scale (1 = strongly disagree, 7 = strongly agree). The Cronbach’s alpha of the Machiavellianism,
Psychopathy, and Narcissism were .911, .912, and .829, respectively.
The aggression questionnaire: The Buss-Perry Aggression Questionnaire (Buss & Perry, 1992)
was used to measure aggressive tendencies. This 29-item scale consists of four subdimensions: Physical
aggression, Verbal aggression, Anger, and Hostility. Participants rated their agreement on a 5-point scale
(1 = strongly disagree, 5 = strongly agree). The Cronbach’s alpha of the Physical aggression, Verbal
aggression, Anger, and Hostility subdimensions were .795, .777, .867, and .845, respectively.
The satisfaction with life scale: The Satisfaction With Life Scale (Diener, 1984) was used to assess
life satisfaction. This unidimensional scale consisted of five items, which required respondents to
indicate their agreement on a 7-point scale (1 = strongly disagree, 7 = strongly agree). The Cronbach’s
alpha of the unidimension in the current study is .905.
Analysis
To derive semantic representations of the scale items, we extracted dense embedding vectors
for each item’s textual content using the BGE-M3 model (Chen et al., 2025). This model generates a
1024-dimensional dense vector for each input sentence, capturing its semantic information in a
continuous space. For every possible pair of items within a scale, we computed the cosine similarity (i.e.,
the cosine of the angle) between their corresponding embedding vectors. This cosine value, ranging
from -1 to 1, served as the primary predictor, quantifying the computed semantic similarity for each
item pair. The final dataset for analysis consisted of these item pairs, nested within their respective scale
dimensions, with each pair containing its LLM-predicted cosine similarity and the corresponding
empirical correlation coefficient.
Based on the constructed dataset of item pairs, we employed a multilevel modeling approach
using the Linear Mixed Models module in JASP (JASP Team, 2025) to account for the hierarchical nesting
of the item pairs. The empirical correlation coefficient for each item pair served as the dependent

variable. The fixed effect structure included the LLM-derived cosine similarity as the primary predictor.
To model the nesting of item pairs within specific uni- or sub-dimensions of their respective scales, we
specified dimensions as a random factor, allowing both the intercept and the slope of the cosine
similarity predictor to vary randomly across these dimensions. This random components structure also
estimated the potential correlation between the random intercepts and random slopes. For hypothesis
testing, we specified Type III sum of squares and used likelihood ratio tests to evaluate the significance
of the fixed effect. Factor contrasts were set to sum coding for the random factor.
Results
The final dataset for analysis comprised 463 item pairs nested within nine theoretical
dimensions. For the linear mixed model, the likelihood ratio test indicated that the fixed effect of cosine
similarity was statistically significant, χ²(1) = 4.07, p = .044. The fixed effect estimate was b = 0.41, SE =
0.17, t(7.58) = 2.40, p = .045. This confirms that, on average across all dimensions, a higher cosine
similarity between item embeddings predicts a stronger empirical correlation between those items. See
also Table 1 for the results of the linear mixed model.
Crucially, the random effects structure revealed significant variance between dimensions. The
estimated SD for the random intercept was 0.31 (variance = 0.09), and for the random slope of cosine
similarity was 0.43 (variance = 0.18). This indicates substantial variability in both the baseline correlation
level and the strength of the predictive relationship across different psychological constructs.
Furthermore, the correlation between the random intercept and slope was strongly negative (r = -.93),
suggesting that within dimensions where the average empirical correlation is higher, the predictive
utility of the LLM cosine similarity tends to be weaker.
Discussion
The results provide a nuanced answer to our research question: while LLM-derived semantic
similarity significantly predicts empirical item correlations on average, this predictive relationship is not

uniform but is substantially moderated by the psychological dimension. The significant fixed effect
confirms the fundamental proposition that shared semantic content, as captured by modern embedding
models, is a systematic source of covariation among scale items. However, the considerable variance
associated with the random slope indicates that the strength of this relationship differs meaningfully
across the constructs measured in this study.
The significant random slope variance indicates that the predictive strength of semantic
embeddings varies systematically across constructs. This finding aligns with the psychometric
understanding that items are not pure indicators of a latent trait but are also influenced by constructirrelevant item attributes, such as perspective or phrasing, which systematically affect responses
(Widhiarso et al., 2025). It can be inferred that LLM embeddings, which primarily capture semantic
content, are more effective at predicting covariance for constructs where the shared meaning is the
dominant construct-irrelevant item attributes. Conversely, for constructs where other constructirrelevant item attributes (e.g., social desirability, temporal frame) or non-semantic method factors
heavily influence responses, the embeddings’ predictive power diminishes.
The strong negative correlation between the random intercepts and slopes suggests a potential
compensatory mechanism. Dimensions with higher average empirical correlations (intercepts)
demonstrated a weaker link between semantic similarity and those correlations (slopes). One plausible
interpretation is that in dimensions where items already show high intrinsic covariance (potentially due
to a strong, unified latent trait or other shared method factors), the additional explanatory power
provided by semantic overlap is relatively diminished. Conversely, for dimensions with lower baseline
inter-item correlation, semantic similarity emerges as a more salient and distinguishable contributor to
their observed covariance. This finding implies that in well-defined, high-cohesion constructs, semantic
similarity may play a relatively smaller role in determining empirical covariance compared to other
shared factors.

Methodologically, this study moves beyond asking if LLM embeddings predict correlations to
rigorously quantify how much this effect varies across different psychometric contexts. This directly
addresses the core question raised in our introduction regarding the uniformity of this predictive
relationship. The framework partitions variance, distinguishing the general predictive signal from the
substantial dimension-specific heterogeneity, thereby offering a more precise and conditional
assessment of model validity than a single-level analysis would allow. It provides a template for future
research to test other computational features or compare different LLMs under the same hierarchical
structure. Practically, this suggests their potential utility lies in auxiliary roles, such as in the initial stages
of scale development to generate hypotheses about item relationships or to flag items with potentially
problematic semantic overlap, rather than as a standalone substitute for empirical validation.
These contributions must be evaluated within the context of several limitations. First, the LLM
embeddings used are static, general-purpose semantic representations that may not capture the
dynamic, context-dependent meaning or the subjective response processes of actual human
respondents. Second, the analysis was confined to four scales; while they varied in structure, they may
not fully represent all types of psychological constructs, particularly those more reliant on situational
context or cultural nuance. Third, our analysis focused on predicting pairwise correlations rather than
more complex, higher-order factor structures. Finally, we did not explore other textual features of the
items (e.g., concreteness, emotional valence) that might interact with or enhance the predictive power
of semantic embeddings. Future work should incorporate these elements to build more comprehensive
computational models of psychometric structures.
