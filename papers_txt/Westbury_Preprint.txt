Abstract:

The widespread critique that generative pre-trained transformer (GPT) models are
“stochastic parrots” rests on a misunderstanding about how such models process and
represent their training input. GPT models are not mere statistical mimics of linguistic
form but are better understood as inductive inference engines that operate over rich,
high-dimensional semantic spaces. Modern word embedding models, such as LSA and
word2vec, reveal that the structure of meaning can be learned through statistical
relationships between word contexts. GPT models extend this approach, learning to
generate vectors not only from tracking word order but from dynamic attention-based
transformations across contexts. These models navigate semantic space in a manner
that parallels human inductive reasoning, often producing original and meaningful
sentences that neither replicate nor resemble their training data. I show that such
capabilities arise not despite, but because of, the non-semantic nature of their training
objectives. Furthermore, I explain that GPT models do not represent words internally.
They rather construct contextually appropriate vectors that usually do not correspond
to any labeled word at all. Semantic generalization arises because the geometry of the
model encodes a latent structure far richer than any discrete lexicon. The claim that
GPT models merely repeat what they have seen collapses once we recognize that
they were trained not to recognize words, but to predict contextually meaningful
representations. These models are not stochastic parrots. They are semantic
composers operating within a vast, unlabeled, meaning-saturated space.

Significance Statement [197 words]

Generative pre-trained transformer (GPT) models such as ChatGPT have created the first widely
accessible form of general artificial intelligence. In only a few years, they have launched a multibillion-dollar industry and begun to reshape education, medicine, science, and work itself.
Popular accounts often misrepresent how these systems function, repeating some form of the
claim that they are merely “stochastic parrots”, elaborate echo machines that regurgitate text
drawn from their training data. This misunderstanding has led many to dismiss their scientific
and social significance. This paper explains why GPT models are not parrots. Drawing on the
long scientific history of word-embedding research, it shows that these models do not memorize
words but learn to represent meanings as patterns in a high-dimensional semantic space. Their
power lies in discovering and manipulating relationships among meanings, allowing them to
generalize to situations and sentences never encountered before. Properly understood, GPT
models are not mimics but semantic engines: tools that translate between the continuous
geometry of meaning and the discrete symbols of language. What we are witnessing is not a new
trick of imitation, but the emergence of a new kind of cognitive instrument, one capable of
something closely analogous to understanding.

The idea that categories can be recognizable without being strictly definable has been
explored for decades, from Wittgenstein’s (1953) notion of family resemblance to Polanyi’s
(1966) extensive discussion of tacit knowledge to Rosch’s (1973; Rosch & Mervis, 1975)
prototype theory and its extension into metaphor by Lakoff (1987). The human experience of
recognizing a quality without being able to define it applies to the problem of recognizing what
is meaningful. An oft-repeated claim about generative pre-trained transformer (GPT) models is
that they do not operate with meaning at all but are rather “stochastic parrots”1 (Bender, Gebru,
McMillan-Major, & Shmitchell, 2021) or “money laundering for copyrighted data” (Willison,
2023) or “a blurry jpeg of the web” (Chiang, 2023) or “copy/paste on steroids” (Lee, 2023). In
this paper I present evidence that and explain why these frequently expressed opinions are
unfounded. Claims that GPT models can do no more than parrot their input text are based on at
least two related errors, both of which may seem too obvious to doubt: that word meanings are
simple computational units and that GPT models are designed to predict the next word.

meaning. In the third section I outline in more detail how word embedding models work and
what they can do. In the fourth section I explain how GPT models work, with an emphasis on

1. The Nature of Word Meaning
Despite its central place in psychology and the fact that all of us do know it when we see
it, it is difficult to define what meaning is. Although it would be difficult to state unambiguously
where to draw the line, somewhere between the detection of a single bit of information (an
amoeba oozing along a temperature gradient or a tick detecting the presence of butyric acid) and
the synthesis of thousands or millions of bits of information (a holy book or a hockey game),
meaning arises. Even the seasoned pros at the Oxford English Dictionary threw up their hands,
falling nearly into tautology by defining the word meaning broadly as the “significance, purpose,
underlying truth, etc., of something” (Oxford English Dictionary, 2000).
This paper focuses on one form of meaning, lexical semantics. The term semantics was
first coined by the philologist Michel Bréal in his (1883) article Les lois intellectuelles du
langage. Fragment de sémantique. He suggested the term semantics (sémantique) for the study
of linguistic meaning, from the Greek verb σημαντικός [simantikós], meaning ‘to signify’. Many
theories have since been proposed to explain how words can signify something other than
themselves.
Perhaps the oldest theory of lexical semantics, with roots going back to Socrates, is the
mentalist theory of meaning, which argues that the meaning of a term is the mental image to
which it refers. The mentalist theory has the major problem that it makes meaning subjective,
although Socrates tried to get around that by improbably assuming the existence of universal
forms in a perfect reality adjacent to the sad vale of tears we inhabit. If we forego that mystical

The mentalist theory of meaning has found contemporary extension in conceptual
semantics (e.g., Wierzbicka, 1992; Jackendoff, 2002). The essential idea of conceptual semantics
was stated by Chomsky (2000): “However surprising the conclusion may be that nature has
provided us with an innate stock of concepts, and that the child’s task is to discover their labels,
the empirical facts appear to leave open few other possibilities” (p. 64-66). This bold but dubious
claim recalls Dennett’s (1991) definition of Philosopher’s Syndrome: “mistaking a failure of the
imagination for an insight into necessity” (p. 401). Philosopher’s Syndrome impacts the entire
history of academic thought about lexical semantics, including the way that most people think
about GPT models. It is easy and common to mistakenly believe that our intuitions about a thing
give us direct insight into the explanation of that thing. Conceptual semantics assumes that
meaning is fixed by “a finite set of mental primitives and a finite set of principles of mental
combination” (Jackendoff, 1990, p. 9), an assumption that makes semantic structures and
principles analogous to the unconscious structures and principles posited by Chomskian
syntacticians to underlie syntax. Conceptual semantics proposes that the semantic meaning of
word is combined with a non-linguistic conceptual representation into a lexical concept, blurring
the line between linguistic and non-linguistic meaning. Semantic structures are assumed to be
composed of primitives (e.g., events, states, places, amounts, experiences, things, properties,
emotions) that combine in rule-governed ways.

A looser version of the mentalist theory was outlined by several cognitive linguists
beginning in the 1980s (e.g. Lakoff & Johnson, 1980, 1999, Lakoff, 1987) in highlighting the
central role of metaphorical extension in lexical semantics. These cognitive linguists emphasized
that many linguistic distinctions (e.g., front/back, inside/outside, over/under, up/down,
part/whole) are rooted in sensory-motor and affective experience and that these embodied
categories offer a fruitful path for metaphorical extension both in language and in our own
private meaning-making.
The referential theory of meaning suggests that the meaning of a word is whatever it
refers to, its denotation. The referential theory of meaning is problematic for several reasons.
One is that it fails to capture the fact that words do not only have denotative meaning, but also
connotative meaning, a set of affective, pragmatic, and conceptual associations. A second
problem with referential theories of meaning was famously discussed by the philosopher Gottlob
Frege (1892). He noted that the planet Venus has been referred to as both the morning star and
the evening star. It seems clearly wrong to say that the tautological sentence The morning star is
the morning star has the same meaning as sentence The morning star is the evening star,
although the referential theory of meaning implies that the sentences have identical meaning.
Frege proposed that we distinguish between a word’s reference (denotative meaning) and its
sense (our subjective understanding of the meaning). Senses are much more complex and
numerous than denotative meanings.

usually attributed to the linguist Leonard Bloomfield. Bloomfield (1933) defined the meaning of
a word as “the situation in which the speaker utters it and the response which it calls forth in the
hearer” (p. 139). This idea was picked up by Skinner in his (1957) book Verbal Behavior (see

The use theory of meaning is related theory of meaning that posits that “the meaning of a
word is its use in language” (Wittgenstein, 1953; see also de Saussure, 1916). The use theory
addresses the problem of identifying the relevant inputs for a behavioral theory of word meaning,
by narrowing the scope to accepted, publicly-observable uses of language. Under the use theory
of word meaning, word meaning is not a function of the observable inputs of the speaker. It is
rather a function of a speaker’s interactive history with other language speakers who share what
Wittgenstein referred to as the same ‘form of life’ (roughly, the same subculture). The meaning
of a word is wholly contained in the historically-contextualized way that it is used.
The referential, behavioral, and use theories of word meaning are all broadly externalist.
Externalists (e.g., Putnam, 1975; Burge, 1986) hold that word meaning depends largely on how
words are anchored to the external world rather than on purely linguistic or inferential relations.
In emphasizing word-to-world connections, they deemphasized the role of mental
representations that had been central to earlier empiricist accounts of meaning. The position
advanced in this paper can be viewed as statistical externalism, since transformer models (and

The view I will outline below of GPT models as inductive inference engines navigating a
structured semantic space intersects with several recent traditions in the philosophy of language
and cognitive science, especially conceptual-role semantics and inferentialist theories of
meaning. Conceptual-role semantics (Block, 1998; Harman, 1973) holds that a representation’s
meaning depends on its functional role in thought and inference, how it interacts with other
representations, beliefs, and actions. It arose from efforts to explain meaning in terms of use and
internal cognitive relations, drawing on Sellars’ (1956) rejection of the given (knowledge
grounded in raw, preconceptual experience) and on Wittgenstein’s (1953) claim that meaning is
grounded in linguistic practice.
Inferentialism (Brandom, 1994) develops this insight in a normative, social direction by
locating meaning in the web of inferences that governs our shared commitments and
entitlements, the pattern of reasons we use to justify what we say within discourse. Inferentialism
thus systematizes Wittgenstein’s more open-ended idea of “meaning as use.” Conceptual-role
semantics treats meaning as a property of mental architecture, whereas inferentialism treats it as
a property of participation in shared reasoning practices. Both approaches replace earlier
reference-based semantics with relational and functional accounts of meaning.
Conceptual-role semantics and inferentialist theories of meaning are consistent with
connectionist and transformer models, which encode norms and patterns of reasoning to the
extent that they can be induced from patterns of language use alone. Connectionist and
transformer models also align with more recent proposals by Piantadosi and Hill (2022), who
argue that “the search for meaning in learning models—or brains—should focus on

The same geometric structure that allows GPT models to generalize meaningfully from

emerging evidence that linguistic brains and language models share a common informational
architecture rather than a merely superficial resemblance.

2. Word-Embedding Models
In all the theories of lexical semantics considered so far, word meaning is abstract and
difficult to assess empirically. However, the use theory of meaning is closely related to a modern
theory of lexical semantics that makes the use theory concrete and amenable to empirical
enquiry: the distributional theory of meaning. Distributional theories narrow the wide scope of
use theories in a way that makes them directly amenable to computational modeling and testing.

The precursor to the defining idea of distributional theories of word meaning was first
specifically stated by Joos (1950): “[…] the ‘meaning’ of a morpheme (or of a combination
thereof, of any complexity up to a complete utterance, which might even be a whole book or
poem) is by definition the set of conditional probabilities of its occurrence in context with all
other morphemes–of course, without enquiry into the outside, practical, or sociologist’s meaning
of any of them” (p. 708). Harris (1954) made a very similar point. Distributional theories of
meaning have become popular recently, in large part precisely because they are defined
specifically enough to be modeled computationally. The first computational models based on
word distribution began by simply counting how often any two words co-occurred within the
same document (Latent Semantic Analysis [LSA]; Landauer & Dumais, 1997) or within the
same small moving window of text (Hyper-linguistic Analog to Language [HAL]; Lund &
Burgess, 1996).
2.1 Latent Semantic Analysis [LSA]
Although the models are conceptually nearly identical, I will focus on explaining how
LSA works, for two reasons. One is because LSA is deals with language in a slightly more
concrete way than HAL, since it focuses on whole documents rather than an arbitrary
computationally-defined context. The more important reason is that Landauer & Dumais’ (1997)
paper was explicitly focused on explaining how word-embedding models are related to
induction, the ability to reason from observed particulars (say, all the cats you have ever met) to
induced generalities (your concept of a cat). Understanding how induction is related to wordembedding is important to understanding why GPT models are not simply stochastic parrots.

LSA is not simply summing up how often two words co-occur in the same document,
which would provide us with one measure for each pair of words. That direct co-occurrence is
known as first-order co-occurrence. We can use first-order co-occurrence to get a little bit of
information about word meanings. For example, if we found the word dog occurred in the same
document much more often with the word cat than with the word ambiguity, we might induce
that cats and dogs are more similar than dogs and ambiguity. However, LSA uses the simplicity
of counting first-order co-occurrence only as a step towards computing the similarity between
the contexts in which two words occur, as represented by their vectors. We call this second-order
co-occurrence because is it not about whether any two words ever occur close together (firstorder co-occurrence), but rather about whether any two words ever occur in similar contexts
(second-order co-occurrence).
The context similarity measured by second-order co-occurrence underlies our
understanding of how LSA uses induction. Words can have similar (theoretically, even identical)
contexts without ever appearing in the same document. Similarity of context means that the
words co-occurred with many of the same words, rather than that they occurred with each other.
LSA can therefore induce that two words are similar even if those two words never appear in the
same document.

observations (how often words occur together) to make conclusions about general truths (how
similar words are in meaning).

Word-embedding models make clear that language use exists on a continuum, from passive

The question we need to be considering when we ask to what degree a language model is
‘a true language user’ is not whether a language model really understands language in some
metaphysical sense. The question is rather: To what degree can the model participate in the
practice of language? By any behavioral, inferential, or functional standard, a person or a
machine that has access to a word-embedding matrix of a language they do not speak can
participate more in the practice of that language than that same person or machine could without
such a matrix.

through induction from contextual similarity. The model’s representation of a word changes each
time it encounters a semantically related word, thereby updating its estimate of meaning even for
words never directly observed.

any two representations depends not only on direct experience with them, but with everything
else ever experienced” (Landauer & Dumais, 1997, p. 217). In a word-embedding model, this
conclusion follows directly from the fact that the similarity between the vectors of two words
will change every time we encounter either word. Since the similarity between any two words in
a vector model is well-defined, a change in the vector representation of one word must change
that vector’s similarity relationship to every other vector in the matrix. In a high dimensional
space, every time we learn something about one word, we learn a little more about every word.

language use. This has implications for the most famous expression of Plato’s problem in

If we tried to make the ‘stochastic parrot’ criticism of LSA, it would be “All that LSA
can do is tell you what it was trained on: how often words co-occur in the same documents”.
This is clearly untrue. An LSA matrix was never intended to be used to estimate how often word
pairs appeared in the same document. The power of the model arises from the fact that the
vectors represent an aggregate description of the context of every word. Counting how often
words occur in the same document was just a way to get those aggregate descriptions.
It is not the only way to get them. The development of HAL and LSA paved the way for
many other word-embedding models to be implemented (e.g. BEAGLE, Jones, Kintsch, &
Mewhort, 2006; word2vec, Mikolov, Chen, Corrado, & Dean., 2013; GloVe, Pennington,
Socher, & Manning, 2014; FastText, Bojanowski, Grave, Joulin, & Mikolov, 2017; BERT,
Devlin, Chang, Lee, & Toutanova, 2019). Although these differ in various ways from the early
models and from each other, they are all conceptual cousins of those models. The main advance
in the more recent models is how the context vectors are derived.

3. Generative pre-trained transformer models
The exact same principle holds for GPT models, to which we can now turn our attention.
As we shall see, the fact that GPT models are trained on predicting the next word does not mean
that all that they can do after training is to predict the next word. The transformer architecture
was first introduced in a short paper out of Google that was published in 2017 (Vaswani,
Shazeer, Parmar, et al., 2017), which proposed the architecture for translating between two
languages. OpenAI later adapted the transformer architecture for autoregressive, generatively
pre-trained models, introducing the term Generative Pretrained Transformer when it released
GPT-1 in 2018 (Radford et al., 2018).
A GPT model is a neural network composed of multiple computational elements called
heads that are each composed of three pre-trained parameter matrices: a query matrix, a key
matrix, and a value matrix. Each matrix is a linear transformer that projects an input matrix down
to a lower dimensionality, for reasons I will explain below. As a soundbite mnemonic (repurposing Crain & Steedman’s (1985) comment about syntax and semantics), we can say that the
query matrix proposes, the key matrix disposes, and the value matrix composes. This mnemonic
is purely metaphorical, of course. The query, key, and value matrices contain no explicit
propositional content. They are just linear projections that allow the model to compute weighted
similarities between contextual embeddings. The “propose/dispose/compose” phrasing is
intended here to capture their respective mathematical roles in allocating and transforming the
relevance of words in the context. I will begin by describing each of the three matrices in broad

3.1 The Query Matrix Proposes
We can think of a query matrix as sending propositions to its head’s key matrix: Here’s
what I propose we should pay attention to. Do you agree? Both the query and key matrices
consist of vectors that are associated with words in the current input context (in chat: the current
conversation). When the models are in productive operation, the query vectors for all words in
the current input context are compared with the key vectors for all words in the current input
context, by calculating their dot product similarity. This comparison, the query, functions like a
search for relevant information about words in the context, helping the model determine which
tokens in are most relevant in the current context. Of course, the model isn’t explicitly searching
for or formulating questions about aspects of the words. Rather, the similarity between the query
and key vectors is used as a weight on ‘attention’3. The query vector gets ‘an answer’ to its query
about what is relevant by computing how well its vectors align with the analogous key vectors,
allowing the model to identify and weight contextually relevant words.

Many interpretations of transformer function have been proposed, notably including
Shalizi’s (2023) mathematically formal explanation of attention (a term he despises) as learned
adaptive smoothing over context. Shalizi came up with this idea independently but admits that
priority is due to Tsai et al, 2019. Shalizi notes that the ‘attentional weighting’ operation
undertaken by a GPT model is mathematically close to the kernel smoothing operation invented
by Nadaraya (1964) and Watson (1964) many years ago. Kernel smoothing estimates a smooth
curve in noisy, nonlinear data by locally averaging nearby observations, an idea that was later
used in generalized additive models (Hastie & Tibshirani, 1987). In transformers, the same logic
applies. The dot-product similarity between a query vector and a set of key vectors acts as a
learned kernel, since those similarity measures quantify how relevant each key is to the query. A
softmax function converts those similarities into normalized weights that sum to one, so the
model is effectively computing a weighted average of the corresponding value (query/key
similarity) vectors. Each token’s new representation is a smooth interpolation among its
contextual neighbors in word embedding space, with closer (more similar) contexts exerting
greater influence. Compositional meaning then arises through local smoothing in a learned
semantic geometry rather than through discrete symbolic substitution. This account complements
and clarifies, rather than contradicts, the more accessible but less precise geometric interpretation
I have offered of attention as context-sensitive semantic weighting.

in large part from the fact that they use many heads. For example, consider GPT-3, a nowobsolete early model which I will discuss here because its structure is publicly known, which is
not the case for more recent models. GPT-3 consists of 96 independent columns of heads. Each
column in GPT-3 is composed of 96 heads. The model therefore has 96 x 96 = 9216 heads.

4. What’s in a vector?
We have so far reviewed some historical conceptual models of semantics and laid out the
general principles by which a second-order vector can be created in different semantic models. In
this section we will look more deeply into what information is contained in those second-order
vectors.
The relationship that a word embedding vector has to its label’s referent or connotations
is shown in cartoon form in Figure 3, an imaginary representation of the vector for the word dog.
The illustrative features are drawn from the closest vector neighbours to the word2vec vector for
the word dog (using the Google News Corpus matrix available from
https://code.google.com/archive/p/word2vec/, which I will use for all vector computations
discussed in this paper). These neighbours include, for example, the words cat, canine, puppy,
doggy, and veterinarian. Note that these are not just near-synonyms for the word dog. They
represent a range of semantically-related words.
There are three points worth focusing on in Figure 3.
4.1 Vector Values Are Not Semantic Dimensions

rather by a set of vector values. When we think about the semantic dimensionality of a wordembedding model, we must think in terms of combinations of vector values. A word vector is

because the overall pattern is unique, stable, and richly informative. No one bit tells you it’s a
dog; the whole constellation does. We cannot say that vector value 17 means ‘animal-ness’ and
vector value 103 means ‘attracts fleas’. But the combination of all 300 vector values lets us

with a ChatGPT-3 vector. This number, vast beyond human comprehension, can be put into
perspective by noting that it is thousands of orders of magnitude larger than Eddington’s (1939)
estimated number of atoms in the universe (10^80). If each of our possible semantic dimensions
were represented by a single atom, we would need kajillions of universes to store them all.

time, which would give us just 12,288 semantic dimensions. This is absurdly low and based on
the absurd assumption that every vector value encodes exactly one semantic dimension. If there
were ten vector values per semantic dimension (surely also far too low) then we would have
2.05*10^34 semantic dimensions. If there were one hundred vector values per semantic
dimension (almost certainly still too low) then we would have a semantic dimensionality of
3.88*10^250, nearly two hundred orders of magnitude larger than the estimated number of atoms
in the universe. We could keep doing the math (the numbers climb rapidly), but the point is clear.

4.2 Meaning Is Compositional
This discussion is closely related to the second point that is apparent from Figure 3,
which is that we can think of vectors with labels (i.e. word vectors) as the centroid of a set of
other points that have labels, i.e. word meanings are compositional. The vector for (for example)
the word cat can be defined as that point that lies at the centroid of the vectors for the set of all
cattish things.
To illustrate how meaning is compositional in word embeddings, I implemented a greedy
reconstruction algorithm that approximates the word2vec vector of a target word by summing a
series of other word vectors. The Python code is available at https://osf.io/6khqm/. At each step,
the algorithm selects a word from a randomly sampled subset of the vocabulary in Google’s New
Corpus word2vec matrix whose addition most increases the cosine similarity between the
running sum and the target vector, ensuring monotonic convergence toward the target in
semantic space. I settled on a tiny subset of 100 words to make the examples interestingly weird.
With such a small sample, the meanings of the sampled words often bear little resemblance of
the meaning of the target word. Nevertheless, their cumulative contribution reveals how even
distantly related vectors can combine to reconstruct very specific meanings.

4.3 Do GPT Models Know What a Word Is?
Although it is not directly apparent in Figure 2, the diagram also provides us with a way
to think about what aspects of words are accessible to a GPT model. When every point in
semantic space is equally accessible, the demonstration that any point’s dimensions can be
constructed by averaging labeled vectors is irrelevant. Inside the GPT model, vectors that have
labels in the word embedding matrix have no special status. If a GPT models needs a vector, it
constructs it directly. It doesn’t need to solve any puzzles of labeled vector combinations because
the models are doing vector math rather than relying on the relatively tiny set of vectors that
represent human words. Mapping a constructed vector back onto a labeled vector is only a
requirement for interacting with humans. It is not a requirement for tracking what point(s) in
vector space to use during a computation or how to use vectors in those computations.
To state the issue in the bluntest terms: critics who think that GPT models are stochastic
parrots stringing words together have failed to understand that GPT models do not even know
what a word is. This is true not in the trivial sense that ‘they don’t know anything because they
are machines’ or in any sense for which we might need a philosopher. It is true in the simple
factual sense that to a GPT model a vector that has a label in the word-embedding matrix is not
different to a GPT model than any other vector that doesn’t have a label. Since unlabeled vectors
are very much more common than labeled vectors and since GPT models are designed to alter
word vectors to fit the context, GPT models spend almost all their computing time ignoring
words. Inside a GPT model, computation occurs entirely in continuous vector space. The model
never manipulates words as discrete symbols. It only manipulates unlabeled vectors that
correspond to locations in semantic space. During generation, the model traverses this space to
construct a new contextual vector. It only consults the embedding matrix at the end, when it must

5. Do GPT Models Recognize Meaning?
Along with the mistakes of conflating training task with production function and
assuming that GPT models compute across vector labels, another reason that some people
believe that GPT models are only able to parrot their input is because they believe that GPT
models can’t have any concept of meaning. This claim is usually presented as an unsupported
assertion. However, the question is not purely philosophical because it admits of empirical
investigation. In this section I outline such an investigation.
The key principle motivating the current study is that meaningfulness is not a binary
concept (meaningful/nonsense) but rather is reliably graded. It is generally very difficult, and
perhaps impossible, to control for the different possible causes that might contribute to the
human experience of meaning, which is one reason why semantics has remained such a mystery.
However, large language models do provide us with a controlled situation, since they are closed
systems that are only influenced by word distributions and the current context of the discussion.
This makes it possible to put bounds on how much of the variance in meaning can be extracted
from those factors alone. We do that by having a large language model make meaningfulness
judgments about a large set of sentences that have previously been rated for meaningfulness by
human beings.

5.1.5 Discussion
Treating meaning as continuous enables us to quantify the degree to which a GPT model
can account for variance in meaning. The highly reliable correlation between the human and
GPT ratings suggests that the criticism that GPT models are unable to detect meaning is
incorrect. Over half the reliable variance in human meaningfulness ratings can be accessed by
GPT models that only have access to information about language use. This experiment is not
intended to expose the internal computations of GPT models or to suggest that they possess
metacognitive access to their own processes. Rather, it demonstrates that their judgments over
novel sentences share a graded structure with human judgments, suggesting that both systems
encode similar semantic regularities. This alignment constrains what “stochastic parrotry” might
mean. A model that merely recombined surface statistics would not yield such stable, human-like
patterns of semantic grading.
This alignment between human and GPT judgments of meaningfulness does not prove
that GPT models “understand” meaning in any human sense. It does, however, show that their
behavior cannot simply be reduced to stochastic recombination of surface forms. If a stochastic
parrot were truly stochastic, its judgments about meaning would vary with random seeds or
corpus frequencies. GPT models, however, make stable, graded, and context-sensitive meaning
judgments that correlate with human intuitions across wholly novel combinations. That pattern is
not just a stochastic echo of the past but a deterministic projection from a learned semantic
manifold. The observed correspondence implies that the model has internalized a high-

6. General Discussion
The claim that GPT models are stochastic parrots rests on four misunderstandings:
i.) That a neural network’s only possible function is the task it was trained on.
ii.) That a GPT is model is predicting words.
iii.) That a GPT model works with words internally.
iv.) That the full possibility of lexical semantics is contained in the tiny set of
locations in semantic space in which we humans have planted a flag.
The claim that a neural network’s only possible function is the task it was trained on rests
on a category mistake (Ryle, 1949) by conflating a model’s training objective with the limits of
its productive capability. The fact that GPT models are trained to predict the next word does not
mean that they are only capable of next-word prediction. The internal computations that achieve
the training goal are not word-level in any meaningful sense. During training, GPT models
predict a probability distribution over all possible next tokens and evaluate their predictions
using a cross-entropy loss function, which quantifies how far the model’s predicted distribution
is from the true one. This provides supervision at the level of discrete tokens, but the
transformations that minimize that loss take place in a continuous, high-dimensional semantic
space. What is optimized is not simply the identity of the next word, but the geometry of
contextual relationships that makes certain words more appropriate than others. In this sense,
GPT models do not so much predict words as they predict meanings.
The real objective of the training is to shape the internal geometry of the model’s
representational space in a way that its designers find useful for achieving some identified

Critics who reduce GPT models to their training objective misunderstand this. Arguing
that a machine cannot do semantic reasoning because it was trained to predict the next word is
like arguing that a violin cannot produce music because it was designed to vibrate, or, to make
the absurdity even clearer, like arguing that a luthier’s wood carving tools could not make
something that is not itself a wood carving tool. The main original insight about word embedding
models in Landauer & Dumais’s LSA paper was that a tool built from a mindless tally of first
order word co-occurrence can do a remarkable amount of inductive reasoning about many
aspects of both language and the world(s) it refers to.
The next two specific misunderstanding are related. They are the erroneous
understandings that a GPT model is in fact engaged in predicting words and/or that it works with
words internally. As I noted above, a GPT model doesn’t even ‘know what a word is’. The
GPT’s input is not the word that we use to label a vector that encodes latent structure. The model
has no mechanism to ‘see’ those labels. The GPT’s input is just the latent structure. When a GPT
constructs (not chooses or predicts) the next vector in sequence, it does so by compiling
information in very fluid way through a continuous space of semantic possibility conditioned on
context. The models are not limited by the quantization of semantics imposed by an insistence on
using words. GPT models can and do compose meanings that were never explicitly paired in

The last misunderstanding about GPT models as parrots has to do with whether lexical
semantics is wholly captured by the tiny subset of locations in semantic space that we humans

their observed first-order cooccurrence probabilities ever since Andrey Markov introduced the
concept of state-dependent probabilities in his (1906) paper. Claude Shannon used Markov’s
idea to model dependencies between English words in the (1948) paper in which he introduced
information theory. However, despite decades of perfect understanding of these very simple
models and the ease with which they can be implemented computationally, they did not lead to
models that could interact with human beings in any interesting way. Chaining together words
statistically definitely does not dredge up enough semantics to support chat. What was needed to
get to chat capability is what has been introduced by word-embedding models: large sets of finegrained, sub-semantic information. Modern GPT models have their amazing language abilities
not because they predict words. Their abilities arise because they have exploded each word into
trillions of dimensions of relevant difference. The work that is done by GPT models is done by
relying on the resonant semantics that arises from the vector representation of a word’s context.
When the decoder in a GPT model processes the sequence of contextual vectors that are the
decoder’s input, it could not possibly be trying to predict the next word. It is rather trying to
construct the most appropriate semantic representation of the current context. That vector
representation need not (indeed, certainly will never) correspond exactly to the vector of any
word in the vocabulary because it is not even aiming to be a word. It is aiming to be a meaning.

Once it has figured out a meaning, a GPT looks for the closest human word to capture that

Since the most appropriate vector at any time is influenced by the current linguistic
context, it is possible that that output vector from the decoder will correspond to a word that
never appeared after the word that it appears after in the model’s output. A GPT model is
therefore perfectly capable of producing output that was not in its training corpus. An
appropriate way to think about this is that the model’s current linguistic context ‘programs’ the
model. Of course, this is also true for humans engaged in language behavior. We ‘program’ each
other’s language systems insofar as we require (or, at least, learn to confidently expect) contextrelevant responses. If we talk to someone about a topic she has never discussed before, it is quite
possible that the person will produce word sequences that she has never encountered. As a trivial
example, a discussion about an animal so obscure that one person had never heard of it (say, a
tenrec or a kakapo) could easily result in the production of a sentence unique in the speaker’s
linguistic history, such as What does the tenrec eat? or Is the kakapo an endangered animal?
The accusation that GPT models are stochastic parrots reveals more about the limitations
of the metaphors we use to discuss these models than it reveals about the genuine capacities of
the models being criticized. GPT models are not echo chambers of linguistic surface forms. They
are rather intricate engines of generalization, grounded not in memorizing letter string sequences
but in navigating through structured semantic geometry in an extraordinarily complex space that
we ourselves do not and probably will never fully understand. GPT models do not simply
regurgitate words. They navigate meaning in a highly-structured space in which what humans
call words are only waypoints, not destinations. To view the models as parrots is to mistake a
map for the pen that drew it. What we are witnessing with the appearance of GPT models is not

Appendix A: Instructions for humans (above) and GPT models (below) for rating
meaningfulness of sentences
Humans
“We are going to ask you to rate a series of statements for how meaningful they are. The
questions are presented as statements, with a scale beneath that is marked 'Definitely
meaningless' on the left and 'Definitely meaningful' on the right. Please use the computer mouse
to mark the position on the scale that indicates your own opinion of how meaningful the
statement. When you let go of the mouse button, you will move to the next question so do not let
go until you have positioned the marker.
There is no right or wrong answer, and this is not a test. We are only interested in your
opinion.
We will begin now with two example questions just to show you how it works.”
GPT models
“I have collected meaningfulness ratings for 200 sentences on a scale of 1 (definitely
meaningless) to 100 (definitely meaningful). I'm interested in your opinion, which might
help me understand the human ratings. Are you able to provide your own ratings from 1
to 100? I have appended the sentences, in random order, below.”
