18                                              Abstract

19   The Generalized Total Entropy Fit Index (GenTEFI) is introduced as a new fit index for
20   comparing the dimensionality of correlated traits and hierarchical/bifactor structures in
21   Structural Equation Modeling (SEM) and Network Psychometrics. This study addresses
22   limitations in recent simulation studies that focus solely on discrepancy due to estimation, by
23   incorporating discrepancy due to approximation and population error. Three Monte Carlo
24   simulations were conducted to assess the accuracy of GenTEFI and traditional fit indices
25   (CFI, RMSEA, SRMR, BIC, AIC) in identifying correct model structures at both sample
26   and population levels (correlated traits vs. bifactor, bifactor vs. correlated traits, bifactor
27   vs. bifactor). Results show that GenTEFI is the only fit index that adequately differentiates
28   between correlated traits and bifactor structures in both sample and population contexts,
29   regardless of the true data generation mechanism. GenTEFI demonstrated high balanced
30   accuracy (94-95%) across all conditions tested, outperforming traditional fit indices like BIC,
31   AIC, RMSEA, and SRMR, when the data comes from a correlated traits model. When the
32   data comes from a bifactor model, or when comparing different bifactor structures, GenTEFI
33   performed comparably to traditional indices. Two empirical examples illustrate the practical
34   application of GenTEFI in model selection for attachment styles and misinformation
35   susceptibility. This study highlights the potential of GenTEFI to enhance the validity and
36   reliability of measurement instruments in psychology, offering researchers a powerful new
37   tool for understanding the structure of psychological constructs.

42                                          Introduction

43          Psychometric research has increasingly focused on understanding the complex
44   structure of psychological constructs using advanced modeling techniques. The bifactor
45   model, which allows for the estimation of both general and group factors, has gained
46   popularity among researchers due to its ability to provide a more nuanced representation of
47   the underlying structure of the data. However, recent simulation studies have raised concerns
48   about the potential bias of fit indices when comparing correlated traits and bifactor models,
49   highlighting the need for caution in model selection (Greene et al., 2019; Morgan, Hodge,
50   Wells, & Watkins, 2015; Murray & Johnson, 2013).

51          Fit indices, such as the comparative fit index (CFI : Bentler, 1990) and the root mean
52   square error of approximation (RMSEA: Steiger, 1980), are commonly used to assess the
53   adequacy of competing models and determine which model best fits the data. These indices
54   are widely relied upon to justify the selection of one model over another, particularly in the
55   context of comparing correlated traits and bifactor models (Morgan et al., 2015). However,
56   simulation studies have revealed that fit indices may be biased in favor of bifactor models,
57   even when the data generating mechanism is a correlated traits model (Greene et al., 2019;
58   Morgan et al., 2015) or when the data follow random patterns (Bonifay & Cai, 2017). This
59   bias is particularly concerning because researchers rely on model fit to determine the most
60   appropriate model.

61          Cucina and Byle (2017a), for example, showed the bifactor model was the model
62   presenting better fit across 166 comparisons (90% of the comparisons), leading to the
63   recommendation for researchers to consider using bifactor models in intelligence batteries.
64   Another representative example is the work of Snyder, Young, and Hankin (2017) that
65   showed the bifactor structure presented a better fit than the unidimensional and the two
     GENERALIZED TOTAL ENTROPY FIT INDEX                                                                                                                                                                     4


66   correlated traits model, therefore recommending the former as the the best representation of
67   psychopathology symptoms (the p-factor of psychopathology). The tendency of bifactor
68   models to overfit the data, especially in the presence of model misspecifications or
69   complexities in the true data-generating process, further complicates the interpretation and
70   generalizability of the findings of research investigating the structural organization of
71   variables in psychology (Bonifay, Lane, & Reise, 2017).

80            In a recent study, Kan, Psychogyiopoulos, Groot, Jonge, and Ten Hove (2024)
81    cautioned against using approximate fit indices (such as CFI, RMSEA) as relative measures
82    when comparing models, as they may be biased towards favoring the more parameterized

84    information criterion (BIC) and the Akaike information criterion (AIC) for model selection
85    between bifactor other types of models (Kan et al., 2024).

86            To address the potential bias of bifactor models, studies have emphasized the need for
87    a more comprehensive approach to model evaluation and selection. Rather than relying
88    solely on fit indices, researchers have been urged to consider a range of factors, including
89    substantive theory, factor strength indices, and the interpretability of the resulting factor
90    scores (Reise, Scheines, Widaman, & Haviland, 2013; Rodriguez, Reise, & Haviland, 2016).
91    The usual argument is that by adopting a more nuanced and critical approach to the
92    application and interpretation of bifactor models, researchers can mitigate the potential bias
93    and ensure that their conclusions are grounded in both statistical and substantive
94    considerations.

95            Despite the significant practical and theoretical implications of overfitting bifactor
96    models, especially in contrast to correlated traits structures, it is remarkable that the
97    primary concern—the efficacy of fit indices—has often been overlooked. Many studies
98    emphasize the need for parsimony in model comparison, cautioning against exclusive
99    dependence on fit indices. This dependence raises a critical question: if fit indices are not
100   reliable for assessing model-data congruence, what purpose do they serve? The prevalent
101   reliance on these indices without addressing their limitations underlines a significant gap in
102   the field.

103           Instead of tackling the problem of traditional fit indices being biased toward bifactor
      GENERALIZED TOTAL ENTROPY FIT INDEX                                                             6


104   models, suggesting that new fit indices are necessary, the field has adopted either an
105   “evasion” approach (e.g., “researchers shouldn’t rely solely on fit index, but use theory or a
106   parsimonious approach”) or a “bifactor avoidance” position (e.g., “bifactor models should not
107   be used because they overfit the data”). Neither positions addresses the problem, but both
108   lead to a lack of engagement with critical or challenging aspects of the subject, potentially
109   hindering scientific progress. The field has historically prioritized tradition, and may have
110   overlooked or underestimated the need for new fit indices that are not based on the distance,
111   difference, or equivalence between a model-implied covariance matrix and the covariance
112   matrix of observed variables (like the traditional fit indices CFI, RMSEA, and SRMR).

113   Limitations of Recent Simulation Studies

114          Recent simulation studies investigating the performance of fit indices when comparing
115   correlated traits and bifactor models have adopted strategies that limit their generalizability:
116   they focus solely on discrepancy due to estimation, which quantifies sampling error or the
117   discrepancy between population and sample estimates (Cudeck & Henly, 1991; Preacher,
118   Zhang, Kim, & Mels, 2013). Cudeck and Henly (1991) defined four types of discrepancy,
119   described below and represented in Figure 2:


120      1. Discrepancy due to Approximation (DA): This represents the difference between the
121         population covariance matrix (Σ0 ) and the model’s implied covariance matrix in the
122         population (Σ̃0 ). DA is a measure of the model’s lack of fit in the population,
123         independent of sample size and sampling variability. It quantifies the degree of model
124         misspecification or the extent to which the model deviates from the true
125         data-generating process. Minimizing DA is equivalent to maximizing the model’s
126         verisimilitude or proximity to the truth. It quantifies population error or model
127         misspecification, independent of sampling variability.

128      2. Discrepancy due to Estimation (DE): This represents the sampling variability or the
129         difference between the model’s implied covariance matrix in the population (Σ̃0 ) and
      GENERALIZED TOTAL ENTROPY FIT INDEX                                                           7


130        the model’s implied covariance matrix in the sample (or model prediction: Σ̂). DE
131        arises because model parameters are estimated from a finite sample rather than the
132        population. As sample size increases, DE decreases, and Σ̂ approaches Σ̃0 . It
133        quantifies sampling error or the discrepancy between population and sample estimates.

134     3. Sample Discrepancy (SD): Refers to the discrepancy between observed data (S) and a
135        model’s predictions (Σ̂).

136     4. Overall Discrepancy (OD): This is the sum of DA and DE (plus a negligible term that
137        disappears as sample size increases). It represents the overall difference between the
138        population covariance matrix (Σ0 ) and the model’s implied covariance matrix in the
139        sample (Σ̂). OD takes into account both the model’s lack of fit in the population (DA)
140        and the sampling variability (DE). Minimizing OD is equivalent to maximizing the
141        model’s generalizability or its ability to fit new data arising from the same population.
142        It quantifies the overall discrepancy between the population covariance matrix and the
143        model-implied covariance matrix estimated from a sample.


144         At small sample sizes, DA and DE can have opposite effects on OD. Adding
145   parameters may reduce DA but increase DE. As sample size increases, DE decreases, and
146   OD approaches DA. Therefore, minimizing DA and OD are asymptotically equivalent, but at
147   realistic sample sizes, the model with the lowest DA may not have the lowest OD.

148          Greene et al. (2019), Morgan et al. (2015), and Bonifay and Cai (2017) showed that
149   traditional fit indices are biased in favor of bifactor models. Similarly, Kan et al. (2024)
150   argued that approximate fit indices (like CFI and RMSEA), which reflect sample discrepancy
151   between the sample covariance matrix and the model-implied covariance matrix, should not
152   be used for comparing models. Instead, Kan et al. (2024) propose that relative fit metrics
153   like AIC and BIC, which approximate overall discrepancy between the population covariance
154   matrix (Σ0 ) and Σ, are more accurate in differentiating bifactor models from other types of
155   models. However, these studies share a common limitation: they focus solely on the accuracy
156   of fit indices in selecting the model with the highest generalizability (i.e., minimizing OD),
      GENERALIZED TOTAL ENTROPY FIT INDEX                                                           9


157   failing to consider the equally important goal of selecting the model with the highest
158   verisimilitude (i.e., minimizing DA). As Cudeck and Henly (1991) and Preacher et al. (2013)
159   argue, these two goals are distinct and may lead to different model choices, especially when
160   different sample sizes are investigated. Therefore, a comprehensive evaluation of fit indices in
161   the context of bifactor modeling should consider their performance in relation to both
162   generalizability and verisimilitude.

163          When the focus is on one type of evidence because the data generation mechanism
164   can capture only model divergence due to estimation (i.e., the data is generated from a
165   specific model-implied covariance matrix that is a perfect representation of the population
166   covariance matrix), nothing can be said about the fit indices’ capacity to identify divergence
167   due to approximation. Divergence due to approximation is only possible when the data
168   generation mechanism has a model-implied covariance matrix that is not a perfect
169   representation of the population covariance matrix. In other words, it is necessary to add
170   error to the population to be able to capture more realistic evidence about divergence due to
171   estimation (sample-level) and to capture divergence due to approximation (population level).
172   Greene et al. (2019), Morgan et al. (2015), Bonifay and Cai (2017), and Kan et al. (2024)
173   did not include error in the population, nor did they investigate how different fit indices
174   perform at the population level.

175   Present Study

176          Golino et al. (2021) recently developed a new family of fit indices for dimensionality
177   analysis called entropy fit indices. These indices, based on information and quantum
178   information theory, provide an objective function that can be optimized for dimensionality
179   tasks. In their simulation study, the total entropy fit index (TEFI ) demonstrated the highest
180   power in detecting the correct (population) structure compared to other entropy fit indices
181   and traditional fit measures used in structural equation modeling, such as the CFI and
182   RMSEA.
      GENERALIZED TOTAL ENTROPY FIT INDEX                                                            10


183          TEFI computes the distance between the mean Von Neumann (VN ) entropy for the
184   estimated dimensions (factors) and the total VN entropy of the system of variables, with a
185   penalization for the number of dimensions. This penalization ensures that TEFI only
186   decreases with additional dimensions if each dimension reduces the uncertainty or disorder of
187   the system of variables. TEFI requires only a vector representing the structure (i.e., how
188   variables are grouped into dimensions) and a scaled correlation matrix. It measures the
189   entropy reduction by partitioning a multidimensional space into several groups of variables,
190   aiming to minimize the uncertainty or disorder due to the correct identification of underlying
191   dimensions (Golino et al., 2021).

192          Although TEFI overcomes some limitations of traditional fit indices, it is only
193   suitable for single-level structures, such as those estimated using exploratory graph analysis
194   (Christensen & Golino, 2021; EGA; Golino & Epskamp, 2017; Golino et al., 2020), reflecting
195   a correlated traits structure. In this paper, we introduce the generalized total entropy fit
196   index (GenTEFI ), which expands TEFI to accommodate more complex two-level structures
197   represented by a bifactor organization with multiple general factors, such as those estimated
198   using hierarchical EGA (Jiménez, Abad, Garcia-Garzon, Golino, et al., 2023).

199          Our simulation studies address the limitations of recent simulations by generating
200   data from a population model with error, enabling the quantification of fit indices’ accuracy
201   in capturing discrepancy due to sampling and approximation. We check the accuracy of fit
202   indices in three simulations:


203      1. True data generation mechanism: correlated traits model with population error.

204      2. True data generation mechanism: bifactor model with population error.

205      3. True data generation mechanism: bifactor model with population error, comparing the
206        correct structure to over- or under-factored structures.


207          In all simulations, we compute the accuracy of fit indices at both the sample and
      GENERALIZED TOTAL ENTROPY FIT INDEX                                                         11


208   population levels. Trustworthy fit indices should demonstrate high accuracy at both levels,
209   indicating a high capacity to select models with generalizability and verisimilitude. Fit
210   indices that perform well in samples but poorly in the population (or vice versa) are biased
211   and should be used with caution.

212          We show that GenTEFI is the only fit index that can adequately differentiate
213   between correlated traits and bifactor structures when the data generation mechanism is
214   either a correlated traits model or a bifactor model, both at the sample and population
215   levels. Additionally, when the data generation mechanism is a bifactor model, GenTEFI
216   performs as well as traditional fit indices but with higher accuracy in identifying the correct
217   structure when the general factor is overfactored.

218          The paper is organized as follows. First, we present TEFI and its expansion,
219   GenTEFI. Then, we discuss traditional fit indices used in dimensionality assessment and
220   briefly introduce bifactor models with multiple correlated general factors. We describe three
221   Monte Carlo simulations, present the results, and provide an empirical example. Finally, we
222   discuss the findings, limitations, and future directions.

223      The Total Entropy Fit Index and its generalization to two-level structures

224   Entropy, Total Correlation, and the K-Function

225          Golino et al. (2021) introduced a new a family of fit indices based on the
226   information-theoretic concept of entropy, that can be used to check the fit of the
227   dimensionality structures estimated via EGA (Christensen & Golino, 2021; Golino &
228   Epskamp, 2017; Golino et al., 2020) and factor-analytic methods. In information theory,
229   entropy is a measure of the uncertainty associated with a random variable. It is a way of
230   quantifying the amount of randomness or unpredictability in a system, with its historical
231   roots in thermodynamics with the work of Boltzmann and others (Watanabe, 1960).

232          The entropy of a discrete random variable X depicts the average amount of
233   unpredictability that is removed when the outcome of X is known (Yeung, 2008),
      GENERALIZED TOTAL ENTROPY FIT INDEX                                                          12


234   characterizing the uncertainty associated with X or the degree of disorganization of X.
235   Watanabe (1939) proposed an entropy-based index termed total correlation to measure the
236   uncertainty of random variables and the strength of their correlation beyond their average
237   interaction (Watanabe, 1960). The total correlation of a set of variables increases as the
238   entropy of individual variables increases or as the entropy of the set of variables decreases
239   (Watanabe, 1960). Replacing metrics of distance by total correlation, Watanabe (Watanabe,
240   1969) developed a cluster-identification technique termed interdependence analysis. The
241   general idea of interdependence analysis is to partition a set into subsets until
242   interdependence is minimized (Watanabe, 2000).

243          Hiroshi Watanabe (Watanabe, 2000) showed that constructing clusters using total
244   correlation could lead to undesirable outcomes in partitioning a multidimensional space of
245   variables. As total correlation disregards the size of the subsets, small subsets with low
246   entropy values might be removed, regardless of their relationship to other subsets. To
247   address this issue, Hiroshi Watanabe (Watanabe, 2000, 2001) introduced the K-function, a
248   modified total correlation index calculated as the difference between the average entropy of
249   Xυ and Xω from the entropy of the super-set X , (Xυ , Xω ) ∈ X .

250          The K-function can be used as a measure of entropy reduction by partitioning, where
251   minimization reduces the uncertainty or instability in the partition of a multidimensional
252   space, and can also be interpreted as the maximization of information gain (Watanabe, 2000,
253   2001). However, despite its interesting properties, Golino et al. (2021) showed that the
254   K-function is not suitable as a fit index for dimensionality assessment because it decreases as
255   the true number of subgroups of variables (i.e., dimensions, clusters, factors, or communities)
256   increases.

257   Total Entropy Fit Index

258          Golino et al. (2021) developed a family of fit indices to overcome the limitations of
259   the K-function proposed by Watanabe (2000) and Watanabe (2001). One of these new fit
      GENERALIZED TOTAL ENTROPY FIT INDEX                                                         13


260   indices, the total entropy fit index (TEFI ), presented the highest power in a Monte Carlo
261   simulation. When TEFI was used to compare the correct (population) structure with
262   incorrect structures (with more or less factors than the population model used to generate
263   the data, or with a different composition of items per factor), it presented a mean percentage
264   correct score (i.e., the mean number of times it selected the correct dimensionality structure
265   when compared to incorrect structures) of 92%. RMSEA and CFI presented a mean
266   percentage correct score of 78% and 74% when used as relative measures of fit, and 14% and
267   35% when their traditional cut-off criteria (≤ .05 and ≥ .95, respectively) was used.

268          The TEFI index computes the distance between the mean Von Neumann entropy
269   (Von Neumann, 1927) of the dimensions (factors or communities) and the total entropy of
270   the system of variables, and adds a penalization to the number of dimensions estimated. Von
271   Neumann entropy (Von Neumann, 1927) is an index that was developed to quantify the
272   amount of disorder in a system. It’s also been used to quantify the entanglement between
273   two subsystems in quantum physics (Preskill, 2018), which occurs when two (or more)
274   particles become inextricably linked. As Golino et al. (2021) points out, the entanglement of
275   two or more systems can also be expressed in terms of a density matrix, a type of matrix
276   used in quantum mechanics that is akin to the probability distribution of position and
277   momentum (i.e., phase-state probability) in classical statistical mechanics (Hall, 2013). Any
278   density matrix has three main characteristics: (1) it is real symmetric, (2) positive
279   semi-definite, and (3) has trace equal to one. Preskill (2018) shows that for any density
280   matrix ρ, Von Neumann entropy is:



                                          S(ρ) = −tr(ρlogρ).                                      (1)


281          Golino et al. (2021) shows that any correlation matrix can be transformed into a
282   density-like matrix by using the number of variables as a scaling factor that will make the
283   trace of the matrix equal to one (Anderson, 1963). By dividing the elements of the matrix by
      GENERALIZED TOTAL ENTROPY FIT INDEX                                                            14


284   the number of variables, the correlation matrix will hold the same properties as any density
285   matrix, and can be used to describe or quantify properties of a system of variables (Golino et
286   al., 2021).

287            Wihler, Bessire, and Stefanov (2014) shows that Von Neumann entropy can also be
288   estimated using the eigenvalues of a density matrix. Given a density matrix ρ with
289   eigenvalues λ1 , λ2 , · · · , λm ≥ 0, Von Neumann entropy is:



                                                      S(ρ) = −tr(L(D)).                              (2)


290   in which D is a density matrix, and L is the vector eigenvalues of the density matrix. In
291   other words, the Von Neumann entropy of a density matrix is the Shannon entropy of the
292   vector of eigenvalues (Golino et al., 2021; Preskill, 2018). Additionally, the joint Von
293   Neumann entropy of two (or more) density matrices, A, B, can be calculated as products of
294   their individual eigenvalues, since the eigenvalues of A ⊗ B are
295   λ1 µ1 , · · · , λ1 µm , · · · , λ2 µ1 , · · · , λ2 µm , · · · , λn µm (Laub, 2005).

321   factor would be conditional on the number of factors already being estimated in the model,
322   showing a decreasing effect as the number of factors increases. In summary, TEFI computes
323   the distance between the mean Von Neumann entropy of the dimensions (factors or
324   communities) and the total entropy of the system of variables, and adds a penalization to
325   the number of dimensions estimated.

328   (e.g., those reflecting first-order factors or communities). At present, there’s no way for TEFI

330   structures with multiple correlated general factors. Expanding TEFI to accommodate
331   dimensionality structures in two levels requires adding two new components to equation 3.

348   Interesting Properties of The Generalized Total Entropy Fit Index

349          To demonstrate the applicability of the GenTEFI in comparing structures with varied
350   organizations—such as correlated traits versus bifactor models—data were generated under
351   two distinct conditions. In the first scenario, we generated data using a correlated traits
352   structure, characterized by factor loadings between 0.45 and 0.75, correlations between
353   factors ranging from 0.00 (orthogonal) to 0.70, and four variables per factor in a total of four
354   factors. In the second scenario, the data was generated using a bifactor model comprising
      GENERALIZED TOTAL ENTROPY FIT INDEX                                                          18


355   four group factors and one general factor, with four variables per group factor. Here, the
356   loadings on the group factors varied from 0.45 to 0.55, while those on the general factors
357   ranged from 0.45 to 0.70. For each model, we generated 100 datasets, each consisting of
358   5,000 observations, to robustly assess the characteristics of the GenTEFI (more specifically,
359   the lower-order or first-order TEFI and the high-order or second-order TEFI values) across
360   these different structural configurations.

361          In Figure 3, a hexagonal binning plot shows the relationship between factor loadings
362   and the first and second-order TEFI under varying conditions of a correlated traits model.
363   The use of hexagonal cells enables a clear depiction of data point density, with color
364   gradients indicating the concentration of points—brighter yellow hues signifying greater
365   densities. The plot’s facets correspond to different levels of interfactor correlation, ranging
366   from orthogonal (zero correlation) to highly correlated factors, enabling a comparative
367   analysis across the spectrum of loading magnitudes. The hexagons’ borders are color-coded

      GENERALIZED TOTAL ENTROPY FIT INDEX                                                                                                                                                                                                                           19


382   the factors, which makes them more mixed or presents a higher disorganization and higher

386   variables has a higher entropy or uncertainty than the average entropy of its individual
387   factors. This suggests that the global interactions of the variables contribute more to its

389   hypothetical second-order factor decreases. In other words, as the correlations between
390   factors increase, a second-order structure emerges.

436   Ponsoda, 2016; Golino et al., 2021). Clark and Bowles (2018) and Beierl, Bühner, and Heene
437   (2018), for example, showed that none of the conventional cut-off values previously cited
      GENERALIZED TOTAL ENTROPY FIT INDEX                                                          22


438   appeared to perform well enough to be recommended, a finding replicated by Golino et al.
439   (2021). Clark and Bowles (2018), Beierl et al. (2018), and Garrido et al. (2016) found that
440   the RMSEA index combined with the typical cut-off value of 0.05 was insensitive to latent
441   misspecification, frequently accepting models with fewer major factors than those in the
442   population. Similarly, although CFI performed more reliably using a 0.95 cut-off value, its
443   accuracy was contingent on the size of the factor loadings, factor correlations, and number of
444   items (Beierl et al., 2018; Clark & Bowles, 2018). Again, the SRMR index performed very
445   poorly, generally accepting underfactored models when using a cut-off value of 0.08.

446          Golino et al. (2021) found that despite not performing well when using the cut-off
447   values previously cited, CFI and RMSEA performed very well when used as relative
448   measures of fit. The use of traditional fit indices as relative measures of fit means that two
449   or more dimensionality structures are being compared, and the one presenting the highest
450   CFI value, or the lowest RMSEA/SRMR fits the data better. The values of the fit indices
451   are affected by parameters not related to the size of the misfit, such as the size of the factor
452   loadings, the number of response categories, and the sample size (Chen et al., 2008; Golino
453   et al., 2021; Heene, Hilbert, Draxler, Ziegler, & Bühner, 2011; Hu & Bentler, 1999; McNeish,
454   An, & Hancock, 2018; Savalei, 2012; Xia & Yang, 2018).

455          Golino et al. (2021), however, did not investigate the accuracy of relative measures of
456   fit, such as Akaike’s information criterion (AIC; Akaike, 1973) and the Bayesian information
457   criterion (BIC; Schwarz, 1978). Both fit indices are part of a broader set of fit indices based
458   on overall discrepancy (OD), designed to select the simplest model from a pool of competing
459   models that most describes observed data more accurately (Preacher et al., 2013), and can
460   be generally represented with the following equation:



                                          OD = −2fk + α × qk ,                                     (9)


461          where fk is the log-likelihood of model k, qk is the number of free parameters in model
      GENERALIZED TOTAL ENTROPY FIT INDEX                                                         23


462   k, and α is a function of sample size: in AIC α = 2 and in BIC α = ln(N ), N being sample
463   size.

464           AIC and BIC are two model selection criteria that impose different complexity
465   penalties, with BIC having a stiffer penalty than AIC for sample sizes greater than or equal
466   to 8 (Preacher et al., 2013). Although AIC has been shown to perform well in selecting the
467   true number of factors at small sample sizes, it tends to select more complex models as the
468   sample size increases (Song & Belin, 2008). This tendency has raised questions about the
469   appropriateness of AIC for model selection, as it was designed with generalizability in mind
470   (ability to cross-validate well to data arising from the same underlying process) rather than
471   verisimilitude (proximity to the objective truth; Preacher et al., 2013). BIC, on the other
472   hand, has been found to outperform AIC in recovering the true number of factors, but
473   suffered as N increased to large sample sizes (Preacher et al., 2013).

474           To our knowledge, the current paper is the first to evaluate how well traditional fit
475   indices used in factor analysis (CFI, RMSEA, SRMR, AIC, and BIC) perform as tools of
476   dimensionality assessment in bifactor structures, computing accuracy at the sample and
477   population levels, and using population covariance matrices with error. Therefore, there are
478   no previous Monte Carlo simulations to base our expectations on or compare our results to.
479   Nonetheless, we expect to replicate—to some extent—the results of the previous simulations
480   using the more common correlated first-order factor structures.

481                       Bifactor models with multiple general factors

482           Bifactor analysis (BIFA) is a popular approach to decompose item variance in group
483   factors that affect a subset of items and one orthogonal general factor that is common to all
484   the items (Reise, 2012). This modeling framework has been used in a wide variety of fields,
485   like personality (Abad, Sorrel, Garcia, & Aluja, 2018) and intelligence (Cucina & Byle, 2017b;
486   Eid, Krumm, Koch, & Schulze, 2018), to deal with the simultaneous presence of narrow and
487   broad dimensions, giving a comprehensive view of the psychological traits that are measured.
      GENERALIZED TOTAL ENTROPY FIT INDEX                                                           24


488          However, BIFA is restricted to a single general factor, whereas psychometric
489   instruments are often created with items that encompass many different domains. For this
490   reason, with the prolific development of BIFA methods in the last decade (Abad,
491   Garcia-Garzon, Garrido, & Barrada, 2017a; Eid, Geiser, Koch, & Heene, 2017;
492   Garcia-Garzon, Abad, & Garrido, 2019; Garcia-Garzon, Nieto, Garrido, & Abad, 2020;
493   Jennrich & Bentler, 2011, 2012; Lorenzo-Seva & Ferrando, 2019; Waller, 2018), there has
494   also been an increasing interest to address more than one general factor in a single model
495   (Cai, 2010; Jiménez, Abad, Garcia-Garzon, & Garrido, 2023a; Tian & Liu, 2021). In the
496   context of bi-factor analysis with multiple general factors (BIFA-MGF: Jiménez, Abad,
497   Garcia-Garzon, & Garrido, 2023b), a general factor is defined as a dimension whose
498   indicators are themselves indicators of at least two group factors, giving a nested-looking
499   structure in which the variance shared by the group factors is contained in the general
500   factors. This generalization of BIFA takes into account the dependencies between the
501   domains (i.e., correlations between the general factors and interstitial cross-loadings),
502   removing possible biases that may appear when conducting independent BIFA analyses for
503   each domain of the data. Furthermore, BIFA-MGF can be used in a completely exploratory
504   framework (Jiménez, Abad, Garcia-Garzon, & Garrido, 2023a), facilitating the discovery of
505   theoretical misspecifications (e.g., items loading on a different general factor than expected
506   by the theory) that will promote the content expansion or redefinition of broad traits.
507   Despite the appealing features of this bi-factor extension, estimating these models require
508   the correct specification of the number of group and general factors, which can be
509   challenging in many situations (but see Jiménez, Abad, Garcia-Garzon, Golino, et al., 2023).

584   Population Error

585            To simulate more realistic data, the model correlation matrix of each condition was
586   contaminated with error, creating some misspecification. Model misspecification is
587   ubiquitous in psychological modeling. The current consensus admits that it is impossible to
588   accommodate the presence of the many nuances and minor factors that explain common
589   item variance beyond the major factors that are specified in practice (MacCallum, 2003;
590   Mõttus et al., 2020). At best, factor models can capture the broad traits underlying the data.
591   According to this perspective, the population correlation matrix can be expressed as



                                          R = ΛΦΛ⊤ + Ψ + E,                                      (10)


592   where Λ is the item loading matrix on the major factors, Φ is the matrix of major factors
593   correlations, Ψ is the diagonal matrix with unique item variances, and E is a symmetric,
594   off-diagonal matrix of covariances for which the minor factors are responsible (i.e, an error
595   term).

596            A consequence of the inevitable misspecification carried in the E term is that the
597   model parameter estimates, even when they are unbiased, cannot reproduce the population
598   correlation matrix. Therefore, to face this situation, applied researches usually evaluate the
599   difference between the population and model correlation matrices with fit indices to decide
600   whether the amount of misspecification is small enough as to render the model an acceptable
601   or good simplification of the reality (Maydeu-Olivares, Shi, & Rosseel, 2018; Shi,
602   Maydeu-Olivares, & Rosseel, 2020).

603            Such model assessment with fit indices has motivated the development of several
604   methods intended to generate data (i.e., the E term) with varying levels of misspecification
605   according to different values of the fit indices, so that the datasets created in simulation
606   research resemble better the messy data encountered in practice (Cudeck & Browne, 1992;
      GENERALIZED TOTAL ENTROPY FIT INDEX                                                          30


607   Lai, 2019; Tucker, Koopman, & Linn, 1969; Yuan & Hayashi, 2003). In fact, many studies
608   have already uncovered the fallibility of many dimensionality methods under the presence of
609   model misspecification (Jiménez, Abad, Garcia-Garzon, Golino, et al., 2023; Lim & Jahng,
610   2019; Xia, 2021), strengthening the view that simulation studies in dimensionality research
611   should include a condition involving population error to avoid erroneous conclusions.

612          For this simulation, we decided to generate data with population error following the
613   method of Cudeck and Browne (1992) using the sim_factor function from the bifactor
614   package. This approach has two features that make it more appealing than other competing
615   methods. First, the objective value of the discrepancy function or a given fit index can be
616   fixed, giving a precise control of the desired amount of misspecification. Second, the global
617   minimum at the original model parameters is preserved after adding the E term, so that the
618   nominal conditions involving the magnitude of item loadings, cross-loadings, and factor
619   correlations are not contaminated.

620          The amount of population error was specified according to the CFI index estimated
621   via maximum likelihood, with levels of CFI = 0.95 chosen to reflect an excellent fit to the
622   data, and CFI = 0.90 to represent a good level of fit (Montoya & Edwards, 2021). Even
623   though all commonly used factor-analytic fit indices are impacted by incidental parameters
624   not related to the size of the misfit, the CFI has proven to be the most robust to latent
625   misspecification in comparison to absolute fit indices such as RMSEA or SRMR (Garrido et
626   al., 2016). The largest residual correlation for a model was a second criteria used to control
627   the amount of population error. The largest population residual correlation for models with
628   a CFI = 0.95 was ≤ 0.10, while for models with a CFI = 0.90 it was ≤ 0.15 (Shi,
629   Maydeu-Olivares, & DiStefano, 2018). This ensured that the amount of population error
630   introduced was within the intended levels for all models.

631          Importantly, Cudeck and Browne (1992) warned that their method works only when
632   the generated error is not too large. Thereby, to confirm that we achieved a close-fitting
      GENERALIZED TOTAL ENTROPY FIT INDEX                                                            31


633   population structure for each condition, we fitted a CFA with maximum likelihood using the
634   correct specification of the model, and the resulting CFI was compared with the intended
635   CFI at a tolerance value of 1e-05. Similarly, we also checked whether the estimated
636   parameters were equal to the population parameters and that the largest absolute residuals
637   for each structure was within the intend boundary. In the end, the sim_factor function was
638   iterated until a positive definite correlation matrix with error was obtained and satisfied the
639   aforementioned requirements. Finally, for each conditions we extracted 500 samples from a
640   multivariate normal distribution with unit variances and zero means.

915   Example Two: The Misinformation Susceptibility Test

916          Recently, a series of three studies aimed at developing and validating a comprehensive
917   measurement tool for misinformation susceptibility called the Misinformation Susceptibility
918   Test (MIST) was published (Maertens et al., 2023). The researchers created a set of fake
919   news headlines (using *GPT-3*) and real news headlines from trusted sources. They
920   employed several psychometric analyses, including EGA (Golino & Epskamp, 2017; Golino et
921   al., 2020) and hierarchical EGA (Jiménez, Abad, Garcia-Garzon, Golino, et al., 2023), to
922   select the items and investigate the structural validity of the instrument.

923          The studies collectively demonstrate the feasibility of developing a psychometrically
924   validated measurement instrument for misinformation susceptibility. The MIST showed
925   evidence of superiority in predicting outcomes compared to other measures. Overall, the
926   Misinformation Susceptibility Test (MIST) provides a reliable and standardized tool for
927   assessing individuals’ susceptibility to misinformation, allowing researchers to gain insights
928   into different facets of misinformation discernment and judgment biases.

929          In the current paper, Sample 2E from the MIST paper (Maertens et al., 2023) is used.
930   The reason to use this sample is to contrast and replicate the hierarchical EGA of the
931   original paper (used in Sample 2E) with the analysis implemented in this section. Sample 2E
932   was collected in November 2022 using Respondi/Bilendi, using the 16 items version of the
933   MIST instrument. The sample is a nationally representative quota sample (N = 1213) of

977   Discussion

978          In this study, we introduced the Generalized Total Entropy Fit Index (GenTEFI), a
979   novel tool to assess the fit of competing dimensionality structures in psychometric research.
980   GenTEFI extends the recently developed Total Entropy Fit Index (Golino et al., 2021) to
981   accommodate more complex, two-level structures such as those represented by bifactor
       GENERALIZED TOTAL ENTROPY FIT INDEX                                                           55


 982   models with multiple correlated general factors. Our simulation studies demonstrate that
 983   GenTEFI is the only fit index that can adequately differentiate between correlated traits and
 984   bifactor structures in both the sample and population level (simulations one and two),
 985   addressing a critical limitation in the field.

 986          Fit indices are crucial in psychometrics as they allow researchers to evaluate how well
 987   a statistical model fits the empirical data, thereby aiding the interpretation of the underlying
 988   structure of the variables and making predictions about their behavior (Golino et al., 2021;
 989   Savalei & Rhemtulla, 2013; Shi, DiStefano, McDaniel, & Jiang, 2018). However, traditional
 990   fit indices have limitations, such as assumptions about the data and model, and the use of
 991   cut-off values with low power to detect population structure in simulation studies.
 992   Additionally, recent simulation studies have pointed to the strong bias of traditional fit
 993   indices when comparing correlated traits and bifactor structures, usually suggesting the
 994   latter as fitting data better (Bonifay & Cai, 2017; Greene et al., 2019; Morgan et al., 2015;
 995   Murray & Johnson, 2013).

 996          Examining the limitations of the traditional fit indices in this context, the field has
 997   adopted two general approaches that we call evasion and bifactor avoidance. The evasive
 998   approach argues that other elements should be used when comparing different structures in
 999   psychological research, such as theory and reliability, and that reliance on fit indices is
1000   inadequate. The bifactor avoidance approach, on the other hand, argues that bifactor
1001   structures should not be used because they overfit the data. In this paper, we argue that
1002   both positions overlook the main issue: A new fit index that can be used to compare the fit
1003   of competing dimensionality structures, such as a correlated traits model and a bifactor
1004   model, is necessary. In response to the limitations of the traditional fit indices, our study
1005   introduced the GenTEFI.

1006          Our study also advances previous simulation studies investigating the accuracy of
1007   traditional fit indices to differentiate between bifactor and other competing models. These
       GENERALIZED TOTAL ENTROPY FIT INDEX                                                        56


1008   previous simulations focused solely on DE, which quantifies sampling error or the
1009   discrepancy between population and sample estimates (Cudeck & Henly, 1991; Preacher et
1010   al., 2013). In our simulations, we use (Cudeck & Henly, 1991) different types of discrepancy.
1011   DA represents the difference between the population covariance matrix and the model’s
1012   implied covariance matrix in the population. DA is a measure of the model’s lack of fit in
1013   the population, independent of sample size and sampling variability, and was implemented in
1014   our study by computing the accuracy of different fit indices at the population level, with two
1015   magnitudes of errors: lower and higher population error. It quantifies the degree of model
1016   misspecification or the extent to which the model deviates from the true data-generating
1017   process. Minimizing DA is equivalent to maximizing the model’s verisimilitude or proximity
1018   to the truth.

1019          Previous research showed that traditional fit indices are biased in favor of bifactor
1020   models (Bonifay & Cai, 2017; Greene et al., 2019; Morgan et al., 2015). Similarly, Kan et al.
1021   (2024) argued that approximate fit indices (like CFI and RMSEA), which reflect sample
1022   discrepancy between the sample covariance matrix and the model-implied covariance matrix,
1023   should not be used for comparing models. Instead, they proposed that relative fit metrics
1024   like AIC and BIC, which approximate the OD, are more accurate in differentiating bifactor
1025   models from other models. However, as stated above, these studies share a common
1026   limitation: they focus solely on the accuracy of fit indices in selecting the model with the
1027   highest generalizability (i.e., minimizing OD), failing to consider the equally important goal
1028   of selecting the model with the highest verisimilitude (i.e., minimizing DA).

1029          When the emphasis is placed on a single type of evidence, due to the data generation
1030   mechanism only capturing model divergence resulting from estimation (i.e., the data is
1031   generated from a model-implied covariance matrix that perfectly represents the population
1032   covariance matrix), it becomes impossible to evaluate the fit indices’ ability to detect
1033   divergence caused by approximation. Divergence due to approximation can only occur when
1034   the data generation mechanism’s model-implied covariance matrix does not perfectly match
       GENERALIZED TOTAL ENTROPY FIT INDEX                                                          57


1035   the population covariance matrix. In other words, introducing error into the population is
1036   necessary to capture more realistic evidence of divergence caused by estimation (at the
1037   sample level) and to capture divergence due to approximation (at the population level).
1038   Previous studies did not include errors in the population and did not explore how various fit
1039   indices perform at the population level (Bonifay & Cai, 2017; Greene et al., 2019; Kan et al.,
1040   2024; Morgan et al., 2015).

1041          The result of the present study shows that GenTEFI is the only fit index that can
1042   adequately differentiate a correlated traits structure from a bifactor structure in terms of fit
1043   both at the population (DA) and sample levels (DE), irrespective of the true data generation
1044   mechanism. Considering all conditions tested in the first and second simulation of our study,
1045   GenTEFI presented a balanced accuracy of 94% at the sample level and 95% at the
1046   population level for both lower and higher population error. GenTEFI is a very good fit
1047   index to capture both discrepancy due to estimation and discrepancy due to approximation,
1048   and it was not impacted by the magnitude of population error. BIC and AIC, pointed out by
1049   Kan et al. (2024) as the go-to fit indices for comparing bifactor models with competing
1050   models, presented a very high and moderately high balanced accuracy, respectively, at the
1051   sample level. However, both fit indices performed poorly at the population level, indicating
1052   that their capacity to detect DA is inadequate. In the population, BIC and AIC favor the
1053   larger model if there is population error, and they are impacted by the magnitude of the
1054   population error, although the latter is more than the former. RMSEA and SRMR presented
1055   a moderately high and moderate balanced accuracy at the sample and the population level
1056   but with performances much lower than the performance observed by GenTEFI.

1057          When considering the results of simulation one (correlated traits vs. bifactor)
1058   separately, we observed that GenTEFI was the most effective fit index to differentiate a
1059   correlated traits from a bifactor structure when the data generation mechanism was, in fact,
1060   a correlated traits model. GenTEFI presented low percentage correct values when the
1061   correlation between factors was .5, the loadings were high, and the number of items per
       GENERALIZED TOTAL ENTROPY FIT INDEX                                                            58


1062   factor was four. With high loadings and increased factor correlation, the uncertainty of a
1063   general factor decreases because, in this condition, the overall system of variables has a
1064   higher entropy or uncertainty than the average entropy of its individual factors. The global
1065   interactions of the variables contribute more to its entropy than the internal structure of its
1066   factors, and therefore, the uncertainty of a hypothetical second-order general factor
1067   decreases. However, increasing the number of variables per factor addresses the issue caused
1068   by the moderate correlations and high loadings, leading GenTEFI to a percentage correct
1069   above 87%. Our results show that BIC is more conservative the smaller the sample size, thus
1070   will favor the smaller model (correlated traits model) with smaller samples. With increased
1071   sample sizes, BIC performs worse, favoring the larger (bifactor) model. When the data came
1072   from a bifactor model, then the fit indices performed as expected, and all presented percent
1073   correct scores above 90%. This result aligns with the most recent evidence (Bonifay & Cai,
1074   2017; Greene et al., 2019; Morgan et al., 2015, 2015) showing that traditional fit indices tend
1075   to be biased toward bifactor structures over correlated factor structures.

1076          In the third simulation, the results indicate that RMSEA was the most accurate fit
1077   index in identifying the correct bifactor structure when comparing it to several misspecified
1078   structures that either underestimated or overestimated the number of group and/or general
1079   factors, both at the population and at the sample level. All fit indices presented percent
1080   correct scores around or above 80% at the population level and above 90% at the sample
1081   level. This indicates that the fit indices are very good at identifying the correct bifactor
1082   structure compared to misspecified group or general factor structures. The “Over GF”
1083   structure was the most difficult structure misspecification tested in the third simulation since
1084   it overestimates the number of general factors. Overall, GenTEFI, BIC, and RMSEA
1085   presented the highest percent correct scores in this condition and worked well or very well
1086   when the loadings in the general factor were moderate.

1087          It is interesting to note that the traditional fit indices work well in the “Over First”
1088   condition but not in the “Over GF.” When the number of first-order factors increases,
       GENERALIZED TOTAL ENTROPY FIT INDEX                                                            59


1089   compared to the number of group factors in the true population model, it does not add any
1090   factor correlations to the model because these are orthogonal group factors in the bifactor
1091   model. Whereas when one additional general factor is added in the “Over GF” condition,
1092   several parameters are added in the new factor correlations. Thus, “Over GF” actually
1093   implies a larger model than the true bifactor model used to generate the data, and the
1094   traditional fit indices have a tendency to favor larger, more complex models. GenTEFI, on
1095   the other side, identifies the correct structure in the “Over GF” condition perfectly when the
1096   loadings in the general factors are moderate. Items that are more informative with respect to
1097   the general factors are all that GenTEFI needs, as shown by the results of our third
1098   simulation. The issue of the number of parameters also helps explain why traditional fit
1099   indices tend to select a bifactor model even when the data is generated from a correlated
1100   traits model. The bifactor model posits additional loading parameters for each item on the
1101   general factor, thus constituting a notably larger model than the correlated traits one.

1102          Taking the results across simulations together, there are a clear set of
1103   recommendations that applied researchers should follow. First, based on simulations 1 and 2,
1104   GenTEFI should be the sole metric used to decide between a correlated traits or bifactor
1105   model. No other fit index achieved results that would satisfy a balanced, unbiased estimate
1106   of the generating model. If GenTEFI suggests a bifactor model, then, based on simulation 3,
1107   traditional fit indices (e.g., RMSEA, BIC ) and GenTEFI are acceptable and can be used in
1108   combination to determine the appropriate structure among competing bifactor structures.
1109   Undoubtedly, the first step is the most crucial one.

1110   Conclusion

1111          The development of GenTEFI represents a significant advancement in psychometric
1112   research. By leveraging information theory and quantum information theory, GenTEFI
1113   provides a principled and objective criterion to evaluate dimensionality structures in two
1114   different levels (first and second-order). Unlike traditional fit indices, which rely on the
       GENERALIZED TOTAL ENTROPY FIT INDEX                                                              60


1115   distance or equivalence between model-implied and observed covariance matrices, GenTEFI
1116   captures the reduction in uncertainty or disorder achieved by partitioning a multidimensional
1117   space into subsets of variables in two different levels. This entropy-based approach offers a
1118   fresh perspective on model fit that is less sensitive to the biases and limitations of
1119   conventional methods.

1120          In practice, GenTEFI can be a valuable tool for researchers investigating the structure
1121   of psychological constructs. It is particularly advantageous when comparing correlated traits
1122   and bifactor models, a common challenge in fields such as personality, intelligence, and
1123   psychopathology. By accurately identifying the underlying structure, GenTEFI can help
1124   researchers develop more valid and reliable measurement instruments and advance
1125   theoretical understanding. Moreover, GenTEFI’s ability to handle complex, hierarchical
1126   structures makes it suitable for a wide range of research questions and data types.

1127          Our simulation studies provide compelling evidence for the superiority of GenTEFI in
1128   certain contexts. When the true structure follows a correlated traits model, GenTEFI
1129   consistently outperforms traditional fit indices in selecting the correct structure; when the
1130   true structure follows a bifactor structure, GenTEFI exhibits high accuracy in detecting the
1131   best fitting structure. GenTEFI also presented a moderately high accuracy in the third
1132   simulation comparing bifactor with bifactor structures with different types of
1133   misspecifications, such as underfactoring or overfactoring at different levels. However, it is
1134   important to acknowledge that there may be conditions under which researchers might prefer
1135   conventional fit indices. For instance, if the primary goal is to assess the absolute fit of a
1136   single model rather than compare competing structures, indices like RMSEA or CFI may be
1137   more appropriate.

1138          Our empirical examples provide valuable insights into model selection. In a large
1139   dataset using the Experiences in Close Relationships Questionnaire, traditional fit indices
1140   (CFI, RMSEA, SRMR, AIC, and BIC) favored a bifactor model over a two-factor model of
       GENERALIZED TOTAL ENTROPY FIT INDEX                                                           61


1141   attachment styles in close relationships. However, the Generalized Total Entropy Fit Index
1142   indicated a superior fit for the two-factor structure. This aligns with the strong theoretical
1143   and empirical support for the two-factor structure, as well as its practical utility and
1144   interpretability in conceptualizing adult attachment (Lafontaine et al., 2015). It’s noteworthy
1145   that Lafontaine et al. (2015) did not directly compare the two-factor and bifactor models.
1146   Such comparisons often encounter the well-documented issue of fit indices being biased
1147   towards bifactor models, which contradicts both attachment theory and the patterns
1148   observed in ECR data when using Exploratory Graph Analysis (as shown in our paper).

1149          Conversely, our second empirical example demonstrates that the Misinformation
1150   Susceptibility Test is better represented by first and second-order communities/factors, as
1151   evidenced by the Generalized Total Entropy Fit Index results. This finding aligns well with
1152   the theoretical framework of misinformation susceptibility. These contrasting examples
1153   highlight the importance of considering both statistical indicators and theoretical
1154   foundations when selecting appropriate models for psychological constructs.

1155          Future research could explore several promising directions to further refine and
1156   extend GenTEFI. One avenue is to investigate its performance under a broader range of data
1157   conditions, such as different sample sizes, variable distributions, and model complexities.
1158   Additionally, integrating GenTEFI with other advanced psychometric techniques, such as
1159   exploratory structural equation modeling, could yield novel insights into the structure of
1160   psychological phenomena.

1161          By addressing the limitations of traditional fit indices and providing a theoretically
1162   grounded approach based on entropy reduction, GenTEFI offers researchers a powerful new
1163   tool for understanding the structure of psychological constructs. Our findings underscore the
1164   potential of GenTEFI to enhance the validity and reliability of measurement instruments
1165   and contribute to theoretical advancements in psychology. As researchers continue to grapple
1166   with the challenges of modeling complex, hierarchical structures, GenTEFI provides a
       GENERALIZED TOTAL ENTROPY FIT INDEX                     62


1167   promising avenue for future research and application.
