A B S T R A C T

Keywords:
Large language model
Psychometrics
Cross-cultural adaptation

The introduction of Large Language Models (LLMs) has transformed research, particularly in psychometrics,
where cross-cultural adaptation of psychological tests presents significant challenges. Traditional adaptations
require precise translation and cultural adjustments to ensure measurement validity, making them resourceintensive. LLMs, with their linguistic capabilities, have the potential to streamline this process. However, their
risks, including biases and reliability issues, require empirical evaluation. Despite these risks, systematic studies
on LLMs' role in test adaptation remain limited. This study assessed the feasibility of adapting psychological tests
using LLMs, illustrated by adapting an organizational commitment scale from English to Polish. ChatGPT-4 was
employed with a zero-shot learning approach, using structured prompts and psychometric guidelines. The LLMadapted scale was compared to a human-adapted version through an observational study with 785 participants.
The study results indicate that the LLM-adapted scale demonstrated satisfactory psychometric properties,
including factor structure, measurement invariance, and convergent and divergent validity, comparable to or
exceeding those of the human-adapted version. Although the reliability of the LLM-adapted scale was slightly
lower than that of the human-adapted version, it remained within acceptable standards. These findings suggest
that LLMs can perform efficient and psychometrically sound test adaptations. This study contributes to the
growing discourse on integrating AI into psychological research, offering a scalable method to reduce cultural
barriers while emphasizing the importance of oversight to mitigate biases and ensure scientific rigor.

1. Introduction
The introduction of Large Language Models (LLMs) to the public
domain, exemplified by the release of ChatGPT, has reshaped the
landscape across numerous disciplines, including scientific research
(Van Noorden & Perkel, 2023). The application of AI, particularly LLMs,
has steadily gained traction among researchers seeking to enhance
various facets of their work (Gray, 2024; Kobak et al., 2024) within a
growing array of scientific fields, including engineering, medicine, so­
cial sciences, and psychology (Fan et al., 2024; Yu et al., 2024). Within
psychology, psychometrics—a subfield dedicated to the theory and
methodology of psychological measurement (Hornowska, 2018)—has
shown particular interest in the integration of advanced statistical
techniques and algorithms to refine research methodologies and prac­
tices (Dixon-Román, 2024; Galatzer-Levy & Onnela, 2023). Among the
numerous challenges psychometrics faces, the cross-cultural adaptation
of psychological assessments (i.e., the translation and adjustment of tests
for culturally distinct contexts) is notably well-suited for improvement

through LLMs. Ensuring the reliability and validity of adapted psycho­
logical measures is crucial to robust scientific and practical outcomes;
however, the adaptation process is often so resource-intensive that it
may be more feasible to develop a new test from scratch (Hambleton &
de Jong, 2003; Krach et al., 2017). Given these demands, psychome­
tricians could greatly benefit from incorporating AI and LLMs to
streamline the cross-cultural adaptation process, thus enabling more
efficient and accurate test adaptations.
Psychological tests are essential for advancing social science. As
research globalizes (Bolaños-Medina & González-Ruiz, 2013), crosscultural adaptation of these tests is critical to maintaining scientific
rigor and fairness (Hambleton & de Jong, 2003). However, the adap­
tation process presents considerable challenges. Cultural factors can
significantly influence both the measurement process and how test items
are perceived by respondents (Mirza et al., 2023; Thomas-Presswood
et al., 1997). For instance, the measured construct may differ between
the culture in which the test was initially developed and the target
culture (Hambleton & de Jong, 2003). Additionally, translation can

result in the loss of essential connotations or alter the perceived diffi­

cultural context, impacting the test's validity. These challenges highlight

Through advanced linguistic processing, they facilitate accurate trans­

However, the use of LLMs is not without significant risks. LLMs have
been reported to be prone to misuse with excessive reliance, potentially
undermining the rigor and quality of scientific inquiry (Abdurahman
et al., 2024; Binz et al., 2023). LLMs can generate fabricated data,
perpetuate biases, risk plagiarism, and lack reproducibility and trans­
parency (Binz et al., 2023; Van Noorden & Perkel, 2023; Weidinger
et al., 2022). Concerns also include their limited representation of
human psychology's diversity and insufficient depth in psychological
expertise, questioning their suitability for test adaptation (Abdurahman
et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024). Assessing LLMs' feasibility in crosscultural test adaptation is crucial, as their limitations may affect val­
idity and reliability. Controlled studies are needed to ensure they
enhance adaptation without added risks.
Despite this, to our knowledge, the potential of LLMs in supporting
the cross-cultural adaptation of psychological tests has yet to be sys­
tematically investigated. Nevertheless, the field of psychometrics has
already begun leveraging AI and LLMs in various capacities. AI-driven
methods have been successfully applied to interpreting test results
(Wang et al., 2024; Yu et al., 2024), detecting cheating or inattentive
responding (Pan & Wollack, 2021, 2023; Xi et al., 2024), and integrating
diverse data sources like social media and wearable data into psycho­
metric inference (Galatzer-Levy & Onnela, 2023; Peters & Matz, 2024).
Although the potential of LLMs in supporting cross-cultural adaptation
of psychological tests remains unexplored, the field of psychometrics
increasingly applies AI in various domains. AI-driven methods have
been used to interpret test results (Wang et al., 2024; Yu et al., 2024),
detect careless or dishonest responding (Pan & Wollack, 2021, 2023; Xi
et al., 2024), and integrate social media or wearable data into psycho­
metric inference (Galatzer-Levy & Onnela, 2023; Peters & Matz, 2024).
Recent studies also demonstrate the automatic scoring of open-text data
such as interview transcripts and blogs to assess individuals' values,
mood, and depression (Kebe et al., 2025; Ye et al., 2025), as well as the
use of LLMs to define and develop new psychological constructs tradi­
tionally addressed through psychometric tools (Götz et al., 2023; Hoff­
mann et al., 2024; Marmolejo-Ramos et al., 2025). AI has also been
applied to adaptive testing, dynamically adjusting items to respondents'
previous answers to improve reliability and validity (Safeer, 2025).
Despite this variety, LLMs are primarily used as test item generators,
capable of substantially reducing development time while maintaining
content quality. They successfully produce knowledge- (Chan et al.,
2025; Zeeshan et al., 2024) and skill-based (Kowal et al., 2025; Shin
et al., 2024) questions, though these tend to be easier than those written
by humans (Young et al., 2025). Automated item generation (AIG)
continues to yield results comparable to expert methods (Falcão et al.,
2023; Gierl et al., 2016). LLMs are also used to generate items for selfreport and situational judgment tests (Götz et al., 2023; Krumm et al.,
2024; Li et al., 2024), producing construct-aligned items, realistic sce­
narios, and behaviorally anchored options (Marmolejo-Ramos et al.,
2025; Oeljeklaus et al., 2025). These efforts employ templating, ratio­
nale prompting, and constrained generation to enhance coherence
(Hernandez & Nie, 2022; Lee et al., 2025; Russell-Lasalandra et al.,
2024; Symeonaki et al., 2024; Terry et al., 2025). Beyond generation,
LLMs evaluate items for clarity, bias, and psychometric quality in­
dicators such as difficulty and discrimination (Laverghetta et al., 2024;
Lee et al., 2025; Russell-Lasalandra et al., 2024), effectively prescreening item pools and streamlining expert review. Nevertheless,
limitations persist: LLM-generated items may lack content validity (Ho,
2024), show linguistic or contextual inaccuracies, inconsistencies, and

remain dependent on domain-specific prompting and expert supervision
(Chan et al., 2025; Shin et al., 2025; Kowal et al., 2025).
This study aims to conduct an initial empirical investigation into the
feasibility of using LLMs for the cross-cultural adaptation of psycho­
logical tests while preserving key psychometric properties, such as
reliability and validity. In this research, ChatGPT 4 was employed using
a zero-shot learning approach to adapt an English-language psycholog­
ical test into Polish. Through a structured prompt, ChatGPT 4 was
guided to modify the test items based on provided resources, which
included examples of previously adapted tests and established psycho­
metric guidelines for cross-cultural adaptation. This adaptation was
performed without additional fine-tuning or training. The LLMgenerated Polish version was subsequently evaluated in an observa­
tional study (n = 785) to assess its psychometric properties, comparing
them to an established, human-adapted Polish version of the same, wellvalidated psychological test (Bańka et al., 2002). The findings indicate
that the LLM-adapted test retained essential psychometric characteris­
tics, matching or exceeding those of the human-adapted version. This
study is among the first to demonstrate the potential of LLMs in crosscultural psychological test adaptation, offering significant efficiency
gains while preserving psychometric integrity. It further advances dis­
cussions on AI's role in research, reducing cultural barriers and
enhancing replicability in psychology.
2. Literature review and hypotheses development
This section formulates the study's hypotheses, beginning with the
rationale for employing LLMs in cross-cultural adaptation, followed by
an assessment of key psychometric properties, including factorial
structure, measurement invariance, reliability, and validity. The evalu­
ation framework adheres to the International Test Commission (ITC)
guidelines (Hernández et al., 2020), recognized for their rigor and
comprehensive validation by psychometric experts. The Organizational
Commitment Scale (OCS; Meyer & Allen, 1991) was selected for crosscultural adaptation. To rigorously evaluate the capability of LLMs in
cross-cultural adaptation, it was deemed essential not only to assess the
psychometric properties of the LLM-adapted test in isolation but also to
compare these results against the psychometric outcomes of a validated
human-adapted version of the same tool. Our theoretical framework
builds on the concept of universal semantics, which posits that all
human languages share a core set of meaning primitives enabling con­
ceptual equivalence across linguistic boundaries (Goddard & Wierz­
bicka, 2014). Recent evidence suggests that LLMs approximate this
universal semantic space through multilingual training, creating latent
representations that preserve meaning beyond surface-level linguistic
variation (Baartmans et al., 2025). In this view, LLMs function as
computational meta-semantic systems capable of interpreting and
regenerating text while maintaining conceptual integrity across lan­
guages. Applied to psychometrics, this capacity supports the preserva­
tion of construct equivalence—central to valid cross-cultural adaptation.
Accordingly, our hypotheses are grounded in the assumption that LLMs
encode meaning within a universal semantic space, enabling them to
preserve the conceptual essence of psychological constructs across
languages.
2.1. Factor structure
The factor structure of a psychological test refers to the underlying
organization or arrangement of the constructs it is designed to measure.
This structure ensures that the test assesses conceptually coherent and
distinct constructs aligned with its theoretical foundation, thereby
supporting its construct validity (Geisinger, 1994). Maintaining a
consistent factor structure is essential for the reliability and compara­
bility of cross-cultural assessments (Bullinger et al., 1993). It ensures
uniform construct measurement across cultures, enabling valid com­
parisons. Factor analysis helps establish construct equivalence and

2.4. Convergent and divergent validity
Convergent validity ensures that multiple indicators reliably mea­
sure the same underlying construct, while divergent validity requires
that measures of distinct constructs exhibit sufficiently low correlations
after correcting for measurement error to be considered empirically
separate (Geisinger, 2003). Convergent validity is essential for complex
constructs and cross-cultural adaptation, ensuring indicators align with
the intended construct and that the nomological network is replicated
across cultures (Geisinger, 2003; Lim, 2024). Divergent validity defines
conceptual boundaries between related constructs, ensures scale multi­
dimensionality, and helps address multicollinearity in structural equa­
tion modeling (Lim, 2024; Shiu et al., 2011). Both convergent and
divergent validity facilitate cross-cultural comparisons of nomological
networks, serving as a key strategy to mitigate construct and method
bias in psychological assessments (van de Vijver & Tanzer, 2004).
Since a test's validity relies on its reliability—an unreliable test
cannot be valid (de Souza et al., 2017)—demonstrating LLMs' ability to
produce reliable adaptations directly supports their potential to
generate valid psychological tests in AI-driven cross-cultural adaptation.
LLMs not only enhance reliability but also contribute to the validity of
psychological test adaptations. Instruction-tuning, shown to yield robust
results (Choi et al., 2023), aligns model outputs with user-defined task
requirements, ensuring consistency with the original psychological
constructs. Additionally, LLMs' capacity for contextual inference
(Ferrara, 2023) supports the preservation of construct meaning across
diverse cultural contexts. Furthermore, advanced tokenization tech­
niques segment text into smaller, contextually relevant units (Mielke
et al., 2021), maintaining coherence throughout the adaptation process
and preventing overlap between unrelated constructs, thereby safe­
guarding validity.

2.2. Measurement invariance
Measurement invariance ensures that a measurement instrument
assesses the same construct consistently across different groups, so any
differences in scores reflect true variations in the construct rather than
biases in measurement (Xu & Tracey, 2017). In cross-cultural adapta­
tions, measurement invariance analysis is essential for identifying and
addressing cultural biases in the instrument (Bullinger et al., 1993). It
ensures that constructs are interpreted consistently across cultural
contexts, maintaining the validity and reliability of the measure (Han
et al., 2019; Putnick & Bornstein, 2016).
While fine-tuned supervised models demonstrate a better inclusion
of social context than LLMs (Choi et al., 2023), LLMs still offer a notable
ability to interpret social and cultural nuances. They can identify and
adapt to cultural influences on interpreting psychological constructs by
incorporating norms, idiomatic expressions, and context-specific con­
notations. This enables LLMs to modify test items to align with the target
culture while preserving their conceptual foundation. Despite the
recognized risks of social and cultural bias in LLMs (Ferrara, 2023), their
training on multilingual datasets allows them to generate text in diverse
styles and levels of complexity (Nesmeyanov & Petrova, 2019). This
capacity extends to simplifying content to ensure comprehension across
varied audiences, reducing group differences in understanding psycho­
logical test items, and enhancing their accessibility. Overall, it should
justify the capacity of LLMs to adapt psychological tests while main­
taining measurement invariance.

2000). Ensuring reliability is particularly crucial, as cultural and lin­
guistic differences may influence item interpretation and response pat­
terns, potentially introducing measurement error and compromising the
test's validity. Additionally, inadequate translations or variations in
cultural norms can lead to item bias, further affecting the consistency of
test outcomes across populations (Geisinger, 1994).

Eligibility criteria specified that participants must be employed in
organizations with a minimum of 10 staff members and have at least one
year of tenure in their current organization. Individuals occupying

the 1393 individuals who initiated the study, 43.65 % were excluded,
with the majority (57.9 % of excluded cases) being disqualified due to

The final sample consisted of 785 participants, with a gender dis­
tribution of 51 % female, 43.9 % male, and 5.1 % categorized as other or
not specified. The mean age of participants was 38.23 years (SD =
12.25), with an average of 16.08 years of total work experience (SD =
11.32) and 11.32 years in their current position (SD = 8.24). A sub­
stantial proportion of participants were employed in the service sector
(49.6 %) or industry (16.6 %), while 33.9 % did not specify their field of
work. Regarding occupational roles, 55.8 % were engaged in officebased jobs, 20.6 % performed manual or physical labor, and 18.5 %
reported mixed roles. Regarding work arrangements, 58.9 % worked
exclusively on-site, 36.1 % engaged in remote or hybrid work, and 5.1 %
did not respond. The study used a mixed sampling approach. A research
panel provided 61.9 % of participants, selected randomly and incen­
tivized with non-monetary rewards to reduce fraudulent responses.
Additional participants were recruited via targeted social media sam­
pling through groups linked to major cities and occupations. Both
methods yielded similar completion rates and demographics.
The intended sample size was determined based on the requirements
for confirmatory factor analysis (CFA), the primary statistical method
planned for the study. Assuming three latent variables and 18 observ­
able variables, with a Type I error probability of 0.01, statistical power
of 0.99, and a moderate effect size (informed by prior research, Bańka
et al., 2002), the recommended minimum sample size was 312 (Soper,
2023). To account for potential data loss, outliers, and in line with
Comrey and Lee's (1992) recommendations, the target sample size was
increased by 20 % to 375. As the study design involved presenting each
adaptation (original and AI-based) to half of the participants, the
required sample size was doubled to 750. A stopping rule was imple­
mented to conclude data collection at the end of the day when the target
sample size was reached.

LLM (OCSLLM) was developed as a derivative of the original English scale
to facilitate a comparative analysis of psychometric properties.
ChatGPT-4, a generative AI model, was employed for the adaptation
task, guided by a custom-designed instruction set (prompt) and supple­
mentary resources. The model was provided with a detailed set of 24
adaptation guidelines formulated based on established psychometric
literature on cross-cultural adaptations (Daouk-Öyry & Zeinoun, 2017;
Davoodi et al., 2020; Geisinger, 1994; Hambleton & de Jong, 2003; Nye
et al., 2008). These guidelines aimed to enhance the cultural trans­
ferability and validity of the adapted construct. For example, one
directive emphasized achieving equivalence in the psychological effect
of items between the original and adapted versions to mitigate psy­
chological item bias. Another rule focused on preventing errors such as
mistranslations, improper word usage, and misinterpretation of idioms
or colloquialisms, thereby minimizing linguistic item bias. The
comprehensive list of adaptation rules is available in Appendix A.
Then, an adaptation reference set was provided to the LLM to support
the cultural translation process. This dataset comprised items from
psychological tests that had previously undergone cultural adaptation,
serving as a contextual guide for the LLM, by incorporating 213 items
from 12 adapted tests in work psychology and organizational behav­
ior—spanning constructs such as work engagement, performance, wellbeing, or the meaning of work (e.g., Chirkowska-Smolak, 2018; Jasiński
et al., 2023; Puchalska-Kamińska et al., 2019)—the resource aimed to
enhance the zero-shot learning capabilities of the model. The zero-shot
approach was selected to assess the model's intrinsic generalization
ability, relying on its capacity to infer task structure and translation
principles from the instruction itself (Brown et al., 2020; Kojima et al.,
2022). Using a zero-shot design ensured methodological neutrality by
preventing potential bias or stylistic contamination from specific human
input, allowing for a clearer evaluation of the model's independent
competence in cross-linguistic adaptation.
Following the preparation phase, a single prompt was given to the
LLM, accompanied by the list of English OCSORIG items and the two
previously mentioned attached files:
Act as an expert in psychometrics and cross-cultural psychological
test adaptations. Work in a context of adapting organization
commitment scale (measuring “a psychological state of affective
attachment to the organization, perceived costs associated with
leaving the organization, and obligation to remain with the organi­
zation that characterizes the employee's relationship with the orga­
nization and has implications for the decision to continue or
discontinue membership in the organization”) into the Polish cul­
tural context.
Take the items from the list below and adapt them to the Polish
cultural context. While translating items for the purpose of adapta­
tion, follow strictly the rules stated in the attached file "rules". Make
sure to follow all the rules during the translation of each item. At the
end of your task, evaluate how well you've applied the rules.

3.2. Measurements
3.2.1. Original and LLM-adapted version of psychological test
The Organizational Commitment Scale (OCS) by Allen and Meyer
(1996) was selected as the original questionnaire for cultural adaptation
using generative AI. Several premises informed this choice. First, orga­
nizational commitment is a well-established construct in the fields of
work psychology and organizational behavior (Al-Jabari & Ghazzawi,
2019; Mercurio, 2015), featuring a clear and well-defined structure
(Mustafa et al., 2019; Wnuk, 2017) and demonstrating conceptual
distinctiveness from related constructs such as job satisfaction and
organizational identity (Ng, 2015; Velde, 2021). Second, OCS is a widely
recognized and empirically validated instrument, with extensive
research supporting its reliability and validity across diverse populations
and organizational contexts (Karim & Noor, 2006; Meyer & Allen, 1991;
Vandenberghe, 1996). Third, assessments of construct and convergent
validity have confirmed the scale's applicability in various cultural set­
tings (e.g., Abdullah, 2011; Kanning & Hill, 2013; Karim & Noor, 2006;
Wnuk, 2017), highlighting its cultural transferability and suitability for
this research. Lastly, the scale has previously been successfully adapted
by human researchers with expertise in cross-cultural studies without
the aid of AI or large language models (Bańka et al., 2002). This prior
adaptation (OCSORIG), with established reliability, validity, and factor
structure, provides a robust benchmark for evaluating the LLMgenerated adaptation in this study. OCSORIG comprises 18 items evenly
distributed across three subscales: affective commitment, continuance
commitment, and normative commitment. Participants respond to each
item using a seven-point Likert scale. A sample item is, “I would feel
guilty if I left my organization now.”

Additionally, we developed a third version of the ques­
tionnaire—OCSSIMPLE—by prompting GPT-4o mini to translate the
original English items into Polish without the use of any prompt engi­
neering, contextual instructions, or adaptation guidelines. The prompt
used was a simple directive: “Translate this into Polish.” The full output
is presented in Table B.1 in Appendix B. This version was generated to
evaluate whether the use of structured translation guidelines meaning­
fully improved the quality of the adaptation. A comparison between the
OCSLLM and OCSSIMPLE versions revealed that 14 items differed in minor
or stylistic ways, typically in favor of OCSLLM. For instance, in Item 9, the
word “disrupted” was translated into a more contextually appropriate
Polish term, and in Item 14, the OCSLLM version featured a simpler and
more comprehensible sentence structure. Two items were identical
across both versions, while two items (Items 4 and 13) exhibited sub­
stantial discrepancies that we classified as clear translation errors in the
OCSSIMPLE version. For example, the item “I owe a great deal to my or­
ganization” was translated in the OCSSIMPLE version using phrasing that
conveys a sense of burden or obligation, potentially suggesting an un­
wanted duty toward the organization. In contrast, the OCSLLM version
rendered the item using language more consistent with an expression of
gratitude, aligning more closely with the original intent of the English
item. Thus, the OCSLLM translation better preserved the intended
meaning of the source material.
We also examined the linguistic differences between the humantranslated version and OCSLLM. One of the most prominent distinctions
is the consistent use of the term “firma” in OCSORIG to translate the word
“organization.” This choice effectively narrows the scope of the item by
implying a “company” or private-sector entity, thereby excluding noncommercial or public-sector organizations. Beyond this systematic dif­
ference, we identified fourteen items with minor or stylistic variations
between the two versions. No items were identical. Four items (Items 9,
10, 13, and 17) were classified as substantially different, with the
OCSLLM versions judged to better preserve the original meaning. For
example, the item “This organization deserves my loyalty” was trans­
lated in OCSORIG using a phrase that could be back-translated as “This
company deserves to be treated fairly by me,” which shifts the tone away
from the concept of loyalty. In contrast, the OCSLLM version provided a
direct translation that retained the core term “loyalty” and more accu­
rately reflected the intent of the original item. Another noteworthy
example is Item 11, where the OCSORIG version refers specifically to
working until retirement, while the OCSLLM version uses a more inclu­
sive phrase that aligns with the idea of staying for the remainder of one's
career, thereby offering a closer match to the original item's intent.
Accordingly, OCSLLM appears to be the most accurate adaptation of the
original scale, as it consistently preserved the intended meaning more
effectively than OCSORIG or OCSSIMPLE in all cases involving substantial
differences.

The scale consists of 12 items, each rated on a five-point Likert scale. A
sample item from the scale is, “Overall, I am satisfied with myself.”
The Job Satisfaction Scale (JSS; Zalewska, 2003) assessed the cogni­
tive component of the general attitude toward one's job. This scale was
selected based on the well-established evidence of a relationship be­
tween job satisfaction and organizational commitment, which, while
related, remain distinct constructs (Tett & Meyer, 1993). This relation­
ship was also replicated in the adaptation study for OCSLLM (Bańka et al.,
2002). The JSS comprises five items, each rated on a seven-point Likert
scale. A sample item from the scale is “I am satisfied with my job.”
The Job Involvement Scale (JIS; Lodahl & Kejnar, 1965), which
measures the extent to which an individual's self-esteem is influenced by
their work performance, was employed in its short form. This scale was
selected due to its established relationship with organizational
commitment while demonstrating a notably weaker relationship with
the continuance commitment dimension in a prior validation study
(Bańka et al., 2002). The scale comprises six items, each rated on a
seven-point Likert scale. A sample item is, “I live, eat, and breathe my
job.”
Turnover intention, an employee's self-reported willingness to leave
their organization within a specified period (Lazzari et al., 2022), was
assessed using two custom-developed items. These items were rated on a
ten-point scale. The first item was, “If you were offered a better-paying
job right now, in the same position at another company, how likely are
you to accept it?” The second item was, “What is the likelihood that you
will leave your current job within the next three months?”
Finally, participants' job experience was assessed using two mea­
sures. Total job experience was evaluated as the overall number of years
across all previous jobs. Additionally, current job experience was re­
ported, referring to the number of years an employee had worked in
their current organization.
3.3. Procedure
The study procedure consisted of two primary phases. First, the
adaptation of the OCS was conducted using a LLM to develop a culturally
adapted version—OCSLLM—with the adaptation process described in
detail in the Method section. The OCSLLM was created to compare its
psychometric properties to those of a human-adapted version. The sec­
ond phase involved a cross-sectional study designed to collect data for
psychometric validation.
In this phase, participants were invited to an online testing platform,
where they were first informed about their rights and provided with
detailed instructions. Afterward, informed consent was obtained, and
eligibility criteria were assessed through a series of closed-ended ques­
tions. Eligible participants were then randomly assigned to complete
either the OCSORIG or OCSLLM version of the scale. Following this, all
participants proceeded with the same subsequent steps: completing
additional questionnaires measuring self-efficacy, job satisfaction, job
involvement, and turnover intention, followed by demographic
information.

The first hypothesis, concerning the factorial structure, was evalu­

Variance Adjusted (WLSMV) estimator, well-suited for Likert-scale data.
A comprehensive set of fit indices was calculated, encompassing abso­
lute, relative, and parsimonious fit indices, as well as noncentrality and
absolute residual-based indices. To support Hypothesis 1, the fit indices
for the OCSLLM should meet or exceed those observed for the OCSORIG.
The results of CFA are presented in Table 2. Several fit indices,
including the GFI, AGFI, and PGFI, were identical for both versions of

both versions achieved satisfactory levels across all fit indices (Awang,
2012; Hooper et al., 2008), indicating an adequate fit to the data. These
findings provide empirical support for Hypothesis 1.

5. Discussion
This study aimed to empirically evaluate the feasibility of using LLMs
for the cross-cultural adaptation of psychological tests, focusing on
preserving key psychometric properties. ChatGPT-4 was employed to
adapt the OCS from English to Polish using a zero-shot learning
approach guided by structured prompts and established psychometric
guidelines to achieve this. The LLM-adapted version was then compared
to a human-adapted version through an observational study involving
785 participants. The results demonstrated that the OCSLLM retained
essential psychometric properties, with hypotheses largely supported:
the AI-adapted version's factorial structure (H1) and measurement
invariance (H2) were equivalent to or exceeded those of the humanadapted version. While the reliability of the OCSLLM (H3) was slightly
lower, it remained within acceptable standards. Notably, the AI-adapted
version exhibited superior convergent (H4) and discriminant validity
(H5). These findings underscore the potential of LLMs to enhance the
efficiency and precision of cross-cultural test adaptation while main­
taining psychometric integrity.
The support for Hypothesis 1, showing that the LLM-adapted version
of the OCS preserved the original three-factor structure, highlights the
importance of maintaining psychological constructs' conceptual integ­
rity in cross-cultural adaptations (Bullinger et al., 1993). Preserving
factor structure ensures the test measures intended constructs consis­
tently, enabling valid cross-cultural comparisons. LLMs, with advanced
semantic processing (Chang et al., 2024), effectively capture itemconstruct relationships during adaptation. Human adaptations, while
effective, may struggle with replicating the original factor structure due
to subjective biases or subtle translation deviations, potentially affecting
construct equivalence in cross-cultural assessments (e.g., Chaudhary
et al., 2012; Kulikowski, 2019). By preserving factor structure, LLMs
offer a complementary, systematic approach to test adaptation.
The support for Hypothesis 2, demonstrating that OCSLLM main­
tained measurement invariance, highlights the potential of LLMs to
handle psychometric tools across demographic boundaries reliably.
While LLMs have been shown to internalize, spread, and sometimes
magnify biases present in their training data (e.g., Chang et al., 2024;
Nadeem et al., 2020), testing their ability to produce unbiased adapta­
tions was essential in this context. The results suggest that LLMs can
effectively mitigate such risks when guided with appropriate prompts
and contextual information, preserving the integrity and fairness of
cross-cultural psychological assessments.
The results for Hypothesis 3 revealed an intriguing paradox: while
the AI-adapted version demonstrated better validity, it exhibited slightly
lower reliability than the human-adapted version. This finding suggests
a potential trade-off between consistency and validity in AI-driven test
adaptations. Similar concerns about LLMs' inconsistency have been
raised in other contexts (Deroy et al., 2023; Xie et al., 2024), high­
lighting that while LLMs can achieve impressive validity outcomes, their
responses may vary in ways that affect internal consistency. While
reliability is traditionally seen as a necessary condition for validity, the
possibility of validity exceeding reliability has been previously theorized
in psychometric literature (Moss, 1994); nevertheless, such cases remain
challenging to interpret. Although the AI-adapted test met established
reliability criteria and aligned with previous research on AI-generated
tests (Götz et al., 2023; Hernandez & Nie, 2022), the human-adapted
version appeared more consistent in certain aspects, underscoring the
value of human expertise in ensuring robust reliability. Given the

5.1. Theoretical contribution
This study combines insights from psychometrics and AI studies,
offering a multidisciplinary perspective on psychological testing. By
exploring the potential of LLMs to adapt psychological assessments
while maintaining key psychometric properties it contributes to the
growing discussion on the reliability and applicability of LLMs in psy­
chological science (e.g., Abdurahman et al., 2024; Williams et al., 2023).
This study is among the first to demonstrate the feasibility of using
LLMs for cross-cultural adaptation of psychological tests. By showing
that AI can maintain comparable or even superior psychometric prop­
erties, such as factorial structure and measurement invariance, to
human-adapted versions, it challenges traditional approaches to test
adaptation. This finding is particularly significant as it addresses the
time and resource demands associated with conventional methods, as
highlighted in prior literature(Hambleton & de Jong, 2003). Moreover,
the study contributes to reducing biases inherent in human-led trans­
lations, which can alter the conceptual equivalence of constructs
(Geisinger, 2003).
The paper introduces a novel methodological approach combining
zero-shot learning with structured prompts and reference datasets,
setting a new standard for integrating AI into psychometric practices.
This method ensures that LLMs adapt tests while preserving construct
validity and linguistic precision, addressing common issues like trans­
lation loss and cultural misinterpretation (Hahn et al., 2005; Krach et al.,
2017). It provides a replicable framework for future research, promoting
more efficient and transparent psychometric adaptations. The study also
highlights the scalability of AI-driven methods across cultural and lin­
guistic contexts, demonstrating their potential to create adaptable tools
for global psychological research. Advanced algorithms increase acces­
sibility and reduce methodological errors, making psychometric tools
more robust (Galatzer-Levy & Onnela, 2023).
This study contributes to the expanding discourse on incorporating
AI and LLMs into science and psychology. This validation reinforces the
need to maintain scientific rigor when incorporating AI into sensitive
fields, as highlighted in prior research (Binz et al., 2023; Wang et al.,
2024). The findings also redefine the role of psychologists, shifting their
focus from manually adapting tests to supervising AI-driven processes,
which raises important ethical and practical considerations. These
include risks such as bias, lack of transparency, and potential overreliance on AI-generated outputs, all of which have been extensively
discussed in the broader context of LLM applications (Abdurahman

5.2. Limitations and future guidelines

Both the hypotheses and the procedural methodology of the study
were pre-registered with the Open Science Framework at https://osf.
io/hksmp. No significant deviation were identified between preregistration and the study.

Despite its contributions, this study has certain limitations that
warrant careful consideration. First, the reliance on a single LLM and
adaptation task limits the generalizability of the findings. Different LLMs
may vary in their ability to preserve psychometric properties, and
broader validation across various models and adaptation contexts is
necessary. Second, while the AI-adapted test demonstrated promising
psychometric properties, the study did not account for the potential
influence of cultural nuances that LLMs may not fully capture. This
raises questions about the depth of contextual abilities and cultural
sensitivity inherent in AI-driven adaptations. Lastly, the study primarily
focused on quantitative measures, such as reliability and validity, but
lacked qualitative insights from end-users, such as psychologists or testtakers, regarding the usability and appropriateness of the AI-adapted
tests.
Looking ahead, future research should focus on evaluating the per­
formance of multiple LLMs across diverse psychological constructs and
cultural contexts. Comparative studies could help identify best practices
for selecting and fine-tuning LLMs for specific adaptation tasks. Addi­
tionally, researchers should explore hybrid methodologies that combine
AI-driven adaptation with human oversight to address potential biases
and ensure cultural and linguistic nuances are adequately represented.
By integrating qualitative feedback from diverse stakeholders, future
work can ensure that AI-driven psychometric tools are both scientifically
rigorous and practically applicable. Future studies may also explore
prompting LLMs not only to produce adapted items but to explicitly
articulate the cultural reasoning underlying each adaptation, which
could enhance transparency and interpretability of the adaptation pro­
cess. Additionally, it would be valuable to assess whether the psycho­
metric performance of LLM-adapted tests generalizes to cultural
contexts that are more distant from the source culture than the one
examined in this study. Finally, the psychological test adaptation
detailed in this paper was conducted from English to Polish, represent­
ing a cross-cultural translation between two nations that, while pos­
sessing distinct cultural profiles, share a broad Western European
heritage. Although the Anglosphere's more individualistic and indulgent
orientation differs from Poland's more collectivistic and duty-focused
values, these distinctions exist within a relatively short cultural dis­
tance (Beugelsdijk & Welzel, 2018; Wierzbicka, 1985). To fully evaluate
the capacities of Large Language Models in cultural adaptation, future
research would necessitate testing across cultures with a more sub­
stantial divergence in their fundamental value structures.
