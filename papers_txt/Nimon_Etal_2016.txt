RESEARCH PAPER



Construct Overlap Between Employee Engagement
and Job Satisfaction: A Function of Semantic
Equivalence?

Kim Nimon1 • Brad Shuck2 • Drea Zigarmi3




Published online: 18 April 2015
 Springer Science+Business Media Dordrecht 2015


Abstract The evidence for employee engagement as a distinct construct, separate from
related work attitudes such as job satisfaction, has been contradictory. Despite a growing
inventory of research, current statistical techniques fall short in providing additional an-
swers beyond those previously documented through discriminant and incremental validity,
among others. Understanding the semantic similarity between scales however could un-
cover the potential bounds of current evidence and highlight areas of further exploration.
This study examined the semantic similarity between three measures of employee en-
gagement frequently found in the research literature and a commonly used measure of job
satisfaction. Examining a relatively new indicator of validity, manifest validity, semantic
relatedness between items from employee engagement and job satisfaction instruments was
assessed using latent semantic analysis. Semantic similarity between employee engage-
ment and job satisfaction items predicted moderate to high amounts of variance
(R2 = 25–69 %) in corresponding correlations of survey responses. Findings suggest that
high correlations between certain employee engagement measures and job satisfaction
scores may be a function of semantic equivalence rather than individual perceptions of
separate theoretical constructs.

1 Introduction

Numerous researchers have employed survey research as a primary method for the study of
employee engagement. As a result, survey research has led to a growing understanding of
the experience of the phenomenon (Albrecht 2010; Macey and Schneider 2008; Nimon
et al. 2011; Shuck and Reio 2013), antecedents associated with engagement (Christian
et al. 2011; Shuck et al. 2011), as well as the expected outcomes of highly engaged
employees (Rich et al. 2010; Xanthopoulou et al. 2009). Current research on data from
employee engagement measures such as the Utrecht Work Engagement Scale (UWES;
Schaufeli and Bakker 2003), the Job Engagement Scale (JES; Rich et al. 2010), and the
Harmonious Passion Scale (HPS; Vallerand et al. 2003) has provided evidence for the
validity of employee engagement perceptions by primarily reporting indices associated
with factor structure stability, convergent validity, and reliability (Shuck et al. 2014).
While the psychometric properties for many of the employee engagement measures re-
ported in academic literature appear to be strong, other research (e.g., de Bruin and Henn
2013; Shirom 2003; Wefald and Downey 2009) questions the validity of employee en-
gagement as a unique construct.
   Some scholars have suggested engagement as old wine in a new bottle (Newman et al.
2010, 2011). For some, the construct of employee engagement is believed to be redundant
and offer little explanatory utility beyond previously established job-related constructs.
This debate has grown in recent years and called for analyses establishing some level of
definitive nomological distinction (Newman et al. 2010; Shuck et al. 2013). At the leading
edge of this debate are questions about employee engagement and overlap with more
traditional measures of workplace-related attitude (Harrison et al. 2006). This is of prin-
cipal focus when comparing the emerging construct of employee engagement and the well-
established construct of job satisfaction, especially where both constructs have provided
important leverage points for workforce scholars and practitioners.
   Conceptually, employee engagement has been defined as an active, work-related
positive psychological state (Parker and Griffin 2011; Shuck et al. 2013). In their posi-
tioning of engagement, Christian et al. (2011) noted the active, dynamic pull of engage-
ment that draws a person towards their work (Kahn 1990). Notwithstanding, job
satisfaction has been commonly defined as a valuation of satiation ‘‘resulting from the
appraisal of one’s job or job experiences’’ (Locke 1976, p. 1300). Conceptually, job
satisfaction has been operationalized as a stationary state and a measure of satiation (i.e.,
contentment with status quo) (Christian et al. 2011; Cooper-Thomas et al. 2010). The
active and stationary states of engagement and job satisfaction embedded within their
definitions suggest that employees should respond differently to survey items that inde-
pendently measure each construct. As a result, researchers might reasonably expect to find
meaningful differences in employees’ responses to items that independently assess en-
gagement and job satisfaction. Nonetheless, conceptual positioning and empirical evidence
remain indistinct.
   For example, Macey and Schneider (2008) noted the seemingly ambiguous conceptual
and empirical overlap between employee engagement and job satisfaction suggesting,
‘‘Many traditional measures of satisfaction … seemingly tap facets that fit [the] conceptual
space for engagement’’ (p. 7). Further, Macey and Schneider as well as Newman et al.
(2010) indicated that Brayfield and Rothe’s (1951) seminal measure of job satisfaction
included overlap with several statements that, at least on the surface, seemed to be in-
tertwined with the nomological net of the engagement concept. Specifically, Newman et al.

(2010) pointed out that the statement ‘‘Most days I feel enthusiastic about my work’’
(Brayfield and Rothe 1951, p. 311) directly overlapped with statements on the UWES
(Schaufeli and Bakker 2003), the latter of which is one of the more popular and widely
cited measures of employee engagement to date (Rich et al. 2010). Conceptually, while the
constructs of engagement and satisfaction can be defined as unique and independent, in
some cases, operationally and empirically, the constructs have been conflated in practice.
Take, for example, the well-cited work by Harter et al. (2002) who specifically defined
engagement as ‘‘satisfaction-engagement’’ (p. 269) and whose measure (i.e., the Q12)
directly taps facets of an employees’ satisfaction level with those conditions under which
they work. These include satisfaction with their manager, satisfaction with access to re-
sources, and satisfaction with the frequency of feedback, among others. Some practitioners
simply measure engagement as direct assessments of satisfaction, trading one word for the
other interchangeably (Macey and Schneider 2008). Connecting the potential conceptual
overlap to empirical measurement, Newman et al. (2011) provided an overall litmus test
for which to examine the conceptual distinction between engagement and other job atti-
tudes such as job satisfaction. They suggested that correlations exceeding .70 could call the
conceptual and empirical nomological utility of the engagement into serious question.
    Within the .70 benchmark, results vary. For example, in meta-analytic work by
Christian et al. (2011), evidence suggested that employee engagement and job satisfaction
were not statistically redundant nor did the two constructs approach the a priori benchmark
(Mq = .53, k = 20, N = 9725). In single studies examining the relationship between
employee engagement and job satisfaction there also seems to be evidence for the dis-
tinction of the two concepts. Rich et al. (2010) correlated total scores from their measure of
engagement to job satisfaction scores from Cammann et al. (1983) and found only a
moderate correlation (r = .56, n = 245). Saks (2006) similarly found moderate correla-
tions between job satisfaction scores from Cammann et al. (1983) and scores (n = 102)
from his two measures of engagement (job [r = .42] and organization [r = .57]). In other
cases when job satisfaction was correlated to employee engagement subscales, correlations
tended to be lower. For example, Ho et al. (2011) used scores (n = 577) from Quinn and
Shepard’s (1974) three-item measure of job satisfaction to correlate with scale scores from
Rothbard’s (2001) engagement instrument and reported lower correlations for absorption
(r = .30) and attention (r = .38).
    Yet evidence for the similarity of job satisfaction and engagement exemplifies the
controversy. For example, Yalabik et al. (2013) correlated scores from UWES-9 (Schaufeli
et al. 2006) with job satisfaction scores from Cammann et al. (1983) and found a strong
correlation (r = .81, n = 377). Similarly, Wefald et al. (2011) correlated scores from
UWES-9 (Schaufeli et al. 2006) with job satisfaction scores from Brayfield and Rothe
(1951) and also found a strong correlation (r = .70, n = 382). Zigarmi et al. (2014) further
explored the relationship of data derived from a set of employee engagement and job
attitudes measures. Employing two measures of employee engagement (JES; Rich et al.
2010; UWES-9; Schaufeli et al. 2006) as well as a measure of work passion (Harmonious
Passion Scale [HPS]; Vallerand et al. 2003), scores (n = 1586) resulting from the en-
gagement-related and job satisfaction measures called into question conceptual distinc-
tiveness (i.e., rUWES = .75, rJES = .69, rHPS = .76).
    Considering both conceptual and empirical research, the evidence for employee en-
gagement as a construct distinct and separate from related job attitudes such as job sat-
isfaction is conflicting at best; yet claims of new wine in old bottles appear to be gaining
support (Rigg 2013). Despite the litany of research on these two constructs, current sta-
tistical techniques and methods fall short in providing any additional answers. This may be

due to current processes and techniques coupled with current limitations of survey re-
search. For example, survey research requires the development of survey questionnaires
that attempt to measure latent constructs of interest (e.g., employee engagement). How-
ever, issues of survey fatigue and the language used in the scales typically go unexamined
in current validation methods, leaving issues of semantic similarity and word meaning
abandoned (Arnulf et al. 2014; Larsen et al. 2008). This seems extraordinary given that the
constructs assessed by survey research methods are derived from items containing words
and that the meanings prescribed to those words are used to derive statistical results from
which theory is constructed and then used in practice. Despite rigorous methods that have
explored numerous types of validity and reliability, language selection within the ques-
tionnaire itself has gone underdeveloped (Arnulf et al. 2014; Larsen et al. 2008). For a
specific example within the employee engagement context, Newman et al. (2010) ques-
tioned the semantic similarity between the words enthusiasm (used in the UWES) and
satisfied (used in several traditional measures of satisfaction; see Brayfield and Rothe 1951
and Cammann et al. 1983). It is possible that high semantic similarity could lead to high
correlations yet go undetected in current research methods. As further pointed out by
Newman et al., the meanings of attitudinal words such as those often used in both en-
gagement and satisfaction survey items can change the context of a specific question based
on worldviews and interpretation. In consequence, ‘‘as respondents with similar experi-
ences or attitudes respond to a questionnaire, high correlations between questionnaire
statements occur’’ (Larsen et al. 2008, p. 1). This is precisely the problem with ques-
tionnaires that purport to measure employee engagement and job satisfaction.
   A new indicator of validity, manifest validity was introduced by Larsen et al. (2008) to
check for language-driven survey results such as, in this case, the reported differences
between the terms enthusiasm and satisfaction. In proposing this new measure of validity,
Larsen et al. offered that:
   Manifest validity is expected to support researchers during the data analysis stage in
   that researchers can compare measures of manifest validity (evaluating the extent of
   semantic difference between different scales) to item correlations computed from
   actual responses. In cases where there is little difference between distances proposed
   by correlation coefficients, the respondents are more likely to have employed shallow
   processing during questionnaire analysis. To the extent that such semantically driven
   results are the result of the language of the questionnaire itself rather the question-
   naire fatigue of a specific response population, standard questionnaires may need
   redevelopment. (p. 3)
   Larsen et al. (2008) found that semantic equivalence between pairs of survey items as
computed by an automated semantic based application predicted observed survey re-
sponses across 22 questionnaires published in information system studies, where the
predictive power in terms of R2 ranged from 0 to 63 %. Although a pioneering first step
towards examining how semantics predict survey responses, it is not clear if Larsen et al.
considered established instruments with published evidence of construct validity from
resulting data. However, more recently, Arnulf et al. (2014) used the results of semantic
analyses to predict survey responses from a number of established instruments that are
purported to measure constructs such as leadership, motivation, and self-reported work
outcomes. They found that semantic similarity of survey items as measured by two types of
text algorithms across four semantic spaces explained 47–79 % of the variance in survey
responses. Only in the case of data from a personality test, did semantic similarity of
survey items not predict inter-item correlations from survey responses.

    At present, commonly used statistical methods fail to reflect potential latent semantic
similarities between question wordings and as a result miss an important indicator of
empirical support that could explain highly correlated results (Arnulf et al. 2014). Within
our interest, it seems possible that the underlying semantic structure of employee en-
gagement and job satisfaction scale items could explain the variability in the correlation
between measures of employee engagement and job satisfaction. Because there is wide
discussion about the semantics of the employee engagement construct and its empirical
relation to other constructs of interest, testing manifest validity using the emerging sta-
tistical technique of latent semantic analysis (LSA) seemed a likely next step forward in
adding to the literature. Moreover, while employee engagement and job satisfaction are
instantiated as survey questions, by looking at the semantic similarity between survey
items we may uncover inferences about the similarity of the underlying constructs. While it
is important to keep in mind that LSA is only comparing the similarity of the survey
questions, understanding the manifest validity between employee engagement and job
satisfaction could provide additional information about the underlying structure of the
employee engagement construct. This information could provide scholars and practitioners
added evidence regarding the practical utility and conceptual clarity of the still emerging
employee engagement construct, alongside a more traditional, widely applied and mature
construct such as job satisfaction. Thus, the purpose of our research was to determine to
what degree, if any, correlations between employee engagement and job satisfaction items
were a function of semantic equivalence. We explored the relatedness using latent se-
mantic analyses (LSA)—a technique that has been infrequently applied to survey items—
and thus tested for the first time, the manifest validity between employee engagement and
job satisfaction. The remainder of this article unfolds as follows: (a) introduction to LSA,
(b) methodology, (c) results, and (d) discussion of results including limitations and im-
plications for research and practice.

items as depicted in Table 1), LSA ‘‘produces a result in which the fact that two words
appear in the same passage is not what makes them similar’’ (Landauer 2011, p. 16). As
evidence, consider the fact that LSA found items JI1 and JI3 to be the most semantically
similar as determined by the size of the angle between the vectors associated with JI1 and
JI3 even though the words life, satisfaction, breathe, live, and eat do not occur in each
item. The result from LSA is, in a way, ‘‘the opposite of word meaning coming from co-
occurrence. LSA learns about the meaning of a word from every meeting with it and from
the composition of all the passages in which it does not occur’’ (Landauer 2011, p. 16).
This example demonstrates the power of LSA when examining manifest validity. Semantic
similarity can also be represented via cosines, where vectors are ordered in the same
manner as correlations and range between -1 and 1 (Landauer 2011). For instance, in our
example, the cosine between items JI1 and JI3 was .99, whereas the cosine between items
JI1 and JI2 was .64.
   Text from other survey items could be ‘‘projected’’ onto our example semantic space,
and distances between them could be calculated. However, the generalizability of our
example semantic space is limited due to the small corpus of documents and dimensions.
Generalizability of semantic spaces to texts not yet encountered (e.g., new survey items)
tends not to be a problem when a large and representative set of documents is used to
create the initial semantic space (Larsen et al. 2008; Quesada 2011).


3 Method

In applying LSA to predict inter-item correlations from employee engagement and job
satisfaction measures, we followed the method from Larsen et al. (2008). Our method
involved (a) defining a semantic space, (b) computing semantic similarity indexes (i.e.,
cosines) among items, (c) obtaining inter-item correlations, and (d) correlating resulting
cosines to inter-item correlations resulting from survey data.

3.1 Semantic Space

In lieu of creating a new semantic space, we followed Larsen et al. (2008) and utilized a
publically available general purpose semantic space. From the spaces available on the LSA
Web site (http://lsa.colorado.edu/), we chose the general reading space for college level.
This space ‘‘uses a variety of texts, novels, newspaper articles, and other information from
the Touchstone Applied Science Associated, Inc. (TASA) corpus used to develop The
Educator’s Word Frequency Guide’’ (Dennis 2011, p. 69). According to Dennis (2011), the
college-level general reading space includes 37,651 documents and 92,409 terms that
cover a variety of academic areas including business.

3.2 Semantic Similarity

To determine the semantic similarity between job satisfaction and employee engagement
items, we conducted LSA using the one-to-many comparisons application, as we were
comparing each job satisfaction item to many employee engagement items. We then used
the document-to-document comparison, thereby employing log entropy weighting (cf.
Dennis 2011).
   Items from two measures of employee engagement (JES; Rich et al. 2010; UWES-9;
Schaufeli et al. 2006) as well as a measure of work passion (Harmonious Passion Scale
[HPS]; Vallerand et al. 2003) and job satisfaction (JS; Luthans et al. 2006) were analyzed.
As shown in Table 5, these engagement measures stemmed from different theoretical
foundations and contain scales that focus on specific characteristics of employee en-
gagement. While there appear to be similarities between the theoretical constructs asso-
ciated with the UWES-9 and the JES (e.g., dedication shares similar characteristics with
emotional engagement), the characteristics associated with HPS are unique in that they
consider how work is internalized in an employee’s identity (cf. Vallerand et al. 2003). We
selected these measures because Shuck, Nimon, and Zigarmi (2014) indicated that the JES,
UWES-9 and HPS scales are theorized to measure some domain of employee engagement
and Zigarmi et al. (2014) found that the employee engagement measures yielded high scale

3.2.1 UWES-9 (Schaufeli et al. 2006)

The UWES-9 (Schaufeli et al. 2006) was developed as a shortened measure of the original
Utrecht Work Engagement Scale (UWES-17; Schaufeli et al. 2002). The UWES-9 includes
three items for each of its scales: vigor, dedication, and absorption. Engagement, as
measured by the UWES (Schaufeli and Bakker 2003), was theorized as the positive an-
tithesis to burnout, and in early research operationalized as reverse scores on the Maslach
Burnout Inventory-General Survey (MBI-GS; Schaufeli et al. 1996), also a 3-factor scale
(e.g., exhaustion, cynicism, and ineffectiveness). Conceptually, what was once ‘‘important,
meaningful, and challenging work became unpleasant, unfulfilling, and meaningless’’
(Maslach et al. 2001, p. 416), demonstrating the theoretical linkage between engagement
and burnout. In psychometric support, Schaufeli et al. (2006) provided evidence of con-
struct validity for the UWES-9, including the confirmation of the 3-factor structure across
10 international samples.

3.2.2 Job Engagement Scale (Rich et al. 2010)

The JES was developed by Rich et al. (2010) to conceptualize Kahn’s (1990) original
conceptualization of the employee engagement construct. The JES is an 18-item instrument
that includes three scales: cognitive engagement, emotional engagement, and physical
engagement. Rich et al. indicated that the cognitive engagement scale was centered on
Rothbard’s (2001) measure of engagement and paralleled the constructs of attention and
absorption, which map theoretically to the UWES (Schaufeli and Bakker 2003) absorption
scale. Emotional engagement was supported from work by Russell and Barrett’s (1999)

3.2.3 Harmonious Passion Scale (Vallerand et al. 2003)

The HPS was constructed by Vallerand et al. (2003) to quantify the ‘‘strong inclination
toward an activity that people like, that they find important, and in which they invest time,
and energy’’ (p. 757). The HPS includes separate scales for the constructs of harmonious
passion and obsessive passion, which were theorized to result from an autonomous and
controlled internalization of activity into one’s identity. Harmonious passion is believed to
connote the more positive, autonomous internalization whereas obsessive passion was
believed to connote a more negative, controlled internalization. Vallerand et al. (2003)
provided initial evidence of construct validity, including confirmation of the two-factor
structure and acceptable levels of reliability.

3.3 Inter-item Correlations

For correlations among survey item outcomes, we used item-level correlations obtained
from the authors of Zigarmi et al. (2014). In their study, 1586 clients and client prospects
of a national training and consulting company completed a survey including items from
several employee engagement measures and outcomes. Of interest to our study were
survey responses from UWES-9 (Schaufeli et al. 2006), the Job Engagement Scale (JES,
Rich et al. 2010), the Harmonious Passion Scale (HPS, Vallerand et al. 2003), and the Job
Satisfaction Scale (JSS, Luthans et al. 2006). In the Zigarmi et al. study, participants
responded to the (a) UWES-9 items via a 6-point Likert scale, with 1 indicating almost
never and 6 indicating always; (b) JES and JSS items via a 5-point Likert scale, with 1
indicating strongly disagree and 5 indicating strongly agree; (c) and HPS items via a
7-point Likert scale, with 1 = strongly disagree and 7 = strongly agree. Coefficient al-
phas reported for the UWES-9, JES, HPS, and JSS scores were .95, .97, 93, and .92,
respectively.

3.4 Analyses

We used ordinary least squares (OLS) regression to assess to what degree correlations
between employee engagement and job satisfaction items based on participant responses
were explained by semantic similarity indices between the employee engagement and job
satisfaction items as reported via cosines. Although the cosines and correlations were
nested by job satisfaction items, hierarchical linear models that nested correlations and
cosines within job satisfaction item did not converge. Influence plots which provided
bubble plots of studentized residuals by hat values were used to identify and delete out-
lying conditions such that we maximized R2 with the maximum number of cosine/corre-
lation pairings (cf. Fox and Weisberg 2011). Remaining cosines and correlations were then
correlated to determine the relationship between participant responses and semantic
similarity among relevant item sets. As it was observed that correlations and cosines did
not vary by job satisfaction item (see Tables 6, 7), averaged correlations and cosines were
also plotted to provide a visual representation of the relationship between participant
responses and semantic similarity.

5 Discussion

Based on the item-level correlations between three select measures of employee engage-
ment and one measure of job satisfaction, this study found that semantic similarity between
items predicted moderate to high amounts of variance in survey responses
(R2 = 25–69 %). For the majority of UWES-9 (Schaufeli et al. 2003) and harmonious
passion (HPS, Vallerand et al. 2003) items, over half of the variance in inter-item corre-
lations with job satisfaction was predicted by the results from an automated LSA appli-
cation. In the case of job engagement (JES, Rich et al. 2010), only a quarter of variance in a
select set of survey outcomes was predicted by semantic similarities among items. Note
that these effects are all higher than the average R2 of 22 % reported in Larsen et al.
(2008), where inter-item correlations from 22 information system questionnaires were
correlated to corresponding inter-item cosines, and are generally in the range of the R2s
(47–79 %) reported by Arnulf et al. (2014) who analyzed inter-item correlations resulting
from a set of leadership, motivation, and self-reported work outcome scales. The results of
this study lead to two noteworthy considerations for discussion. The first consideration is
the relationship between semantic similarity and survey response. The second consid-
eration is that while semantic similarity is assumed to be unintentional, it may arise
because there is a common construct to both employee satisfaction and engagement that
goes unnamed and un-explored within the original intent of a given project.

5.1 Semantic Similarity

According to Larsen et al. (2008), ‘‘in cases where there is little difference between
distances proposed by the semantic analysis of items and distances proposed by correlation

                                                                                             123
1164                                                                            K. Nimon et al.


coefficients, the respondents are more likely to have employed shallow processing during
questionnaire analysis’’ (p. 3). While it may be assumed that survey responses are formed
from deep processing which ‘‘involves reflecting on the nuances and meaning of text and
attaching personal relevance to the information provided’’, survey responses may be
formed from shallow processing through the ‘‘use of surface features of the language
during processing of text’’ (Larsen et al. 2008, p. 3). Because language and environment
have co-evolved (Hauser et al. 2002), it is also possible that some language cannot be
distinguished from experiential knowledge or attitudes; in such cases, survey items may
solicit instinctual responses rather than personally experienced attitudes (Larsen).
   Arnulf et al. (2014) argued, using general psychometric theory as a guide, that while
semantic overlap is required to create internal consistency (e.g., high coefficient alpha),
semantic overlap should not extend across scales. If semantic overlap extends across
scales, ‘‘it is regarded as a contamination of the data since one scale will automatically
correlate with another’’ (Arnulf et al. 2014, p. 2).
   In the present study, semantic similarity predicted up to 69 % of variance in survey
responses. We agree with Larsen et al. (2008) that the existence of high R2 between
semantic similarity indices and inter-item correlations is indicative of a potential under-
lying semantic problem that warrants further consideration. It appears that in some of the
employee engagement measures analyzed in the present study, more work may be needed
to order to create survey items that discriminate between job satisfaction and the still
emerging construct of employee engagement.
   However, it may not be readily understood whether the correlations between employee
engagement and job satisfaction scores were an artifact of shallow processing, instinctual
responses, or contamination of data. Considering the propositions set forth by Larsen et al.
(2008), one might conclude that the UWES and HP correlations to job satisfaction are an
artifact of participants employing shallow processing or responding instinctually to the
employee engagement survey items, given the high correlation between semantic similarity
indices and inter-item correlations. Also note than an alternative explanation to the pre-
dictive power of the semantic similarity indices is survey fatigue. However, given that
semantic similarities between items did not uniformly contribute to variance in survey
outcomes, it appears that survey fatigue may not be a plausible rationale for the observed
relationship between semantic similarities and survey outcomes. According to the survey
semantic response theory offered by Arnulf et al. (2014), it might also be possible that the
UWES and HP correlations to job satisfaction reflect a contamination of data due to
semantic overlap between scales. Although semantic overlap existed between JE and job
satisfaction items, semantic similarity was not as highly predictive of survey responses as
in the case of UWES and HP. While clearly more research is needed to understand how
participants respond to items in the presence of semantic similarity, it appears that in some
cases, participants from Zigarmi et al. (2014) were not able to distinguish their responses to
items that asked about their state of engagement at work from items that asked about their
feelings of job satisfaction due to semantic equivalence.
   The semantic equivalence found between measures of employee engagement and job
satisfaction should not necessarily come as a surprise because they both focus on work and
consider employee emotions. While employee engagement considers an employee’s active
psychological state, job satisfaction is operationalized as a work attitude, focused at the job
level as a temporal, general indicator of employee sentiment (Shuck et al. 2013). It would
appear that the common domain of work, coupled with the emotional aspects of the state of
engagement and the feeling of satisfaction from one’s work, could yield survey items that
prevent employees from thinking deeply about their responses and instead illicit instinctual

responses based on the language of items that has co-evolved over time with the envi-
ronment (cf. Hauser et al. 2002; Larsen et al. 2008).

5.2 Common Construct

Although the JES exhibited the least semantic similarity with employee engagement
constructs, the JES items which did show semantic similarity were concentrated in the
emotional engagement subscale. This finding may suggest that there is a construct common
to employee engagement and job satisfaction, namely affect. It is possible that the concept
of affect has not been adequately conceptualized and separately measured as different
theoretical ideas about satisfaction, engagement or work passion have evolved.
    Brief and Weiss (2002) contended that the concept of satisfaction arose from the ‘‘study
of nonadjustive emotional tendencies experienced by workers on the job’’ and that the
concept of satisfaction was thought of and measured as an affect state (p. 281). They also
asserted that over the history of satisfaction studies conducted between 1930 and 1990,
affect at work was replaced by an approach ‘‘that was conceptually and methodologically
narrow’’, couched exclusively in terms of job satisfaction, and ignoring more clinical or
qualitative aspects and research methods (p. 281).
    As previously discussed, we recognize that some scholars (e.g. Harrison et al. 2006;
Newman et al. 2010, 2011) advocated that the concept of employee engagement is re-
dundant to other previously established work-related constructs. This line of thinking
considers job satisfaction, organizational commitment, and job involvement as types of job
attitudes, where an attitude is defined as a ‘‘psychological tendency that is expressed by
evaluating a particular entity with some degree of favor or disfavor’’ (Eagly and Chaiken
1993, p. 1). Much of the literature summarizing developments in employee engagement
(e.g., Albrecht 2010; Macey and Schneider 2008; Shuck et al. 2013; Zigarmi et al. 2011)
are in agreement with job-attitude advocates when they assert that expressed evaluations or
appraisals of work can be divided into three aspects: (a) cognitive, (b) affective, and
(c) behavioral. For example, Zigarmi et al. (2011) presented a cognitive appraisal
framework for understanding the formation of employee engagement or passion and ad-
vocated that there are two decision-making aspects to the appraisal process: (a) an affective
aspect and (b) a cognitive aspect which then leads to the formation of intentions and
resultant behaviors. In their framework, affect is an ingredient of the work passion or
engagement construct because the formation of engagement or work passion involves both
the heart (feelings) and the head (cognitions), and is a product of both the cognitive and
affective dimensions of the individual’s decision-making process.
    It is also possible that affect is the common ‘‘ingredient’’ in each of the concepts
associated with the nomological net of engagement. Job satisfaction would have an im-
plicit affective component, as would employee engagement, job involvement, and orga-
nizational commitment if defined as a job attitude. Each of these well-known job attitudes
could have an affective component as an expressed evaluative response of favor or disfavor
toward a particular person, place, or thing. Extending this line of logic, it may be asserted
that researchers should continually acknowledge the well-founded separation of attitudes
into cognition, affect, and behavior. That would mean that the concept of job satisfaction,
for example, incorporates the measurement of both a cognitive appraisal of one’s job and
the affective/emotional state that may accompany it (Judge and Illies 2004).

5.3 Limitations

The findings from this study must be taken within the context of the study limitations. First,
the study was limited by one sample of respondents who tended to be managers (76 %),
female (58 %), born between 1961 and 1981 (Gen Xers, 54.6 %) and between 1943 and
1960 (Boomers, 41 %), and with their current company 2 years or longer (83.7 %). It is
possible that other populations may respond to survey items differently. Second, the survey
in the Zigarmi et al. (2014) was administered online. Although there is research that
indicates little difference between online and paper-and-pencil administration of tests, such
research has focused on mean differences rather than inter-item correlations. It is therefore
possible that other techniques of survey administration may yield different results. Third,
the study employed a general purpose semantic space as the basis for the LSA. It is
possible that employing a semantic space designed for self-report questionnaire items may
result in different semantic similarities indices. However, it is important to observe that
creating such a semantic space might result in an over-fitted solution (cf. Larsen et al.
2008). The study also only used one set of semantic similarity indices in predicting inter-
item correlations. In contrast, Arnulf et al. (2014) analyzed four measures of semantic
similarity indices resulting from employing two semantic algorithms across four semantic
spaces. Fourth, the study considered only one measure of job satisfaction and three
measures of employee engagement, which were administered at the same time. It is pos-
sible that other measures of job satisfaction (e.g., Brayfield-Rothe Job Satisfaction Index
Brayfield and Rothe 1951) and employee engagement (e.g., job engagement scale Saks
2006) administered at the same or different times may yield different inter-item correla-
tions and relationships with corresponding inter-item cosines. Future research might use
LSA to objectively quantify the semantic similarity between measures of employee en-
gagement and well-known work concepts such as job involvement, organizational com-
mitment, and employee affect. The distinctions between these well-known ‘‘work
attitudes’’ could become clearer through the technique of latent semantic analysis. For
example, the Meyer and Allen (1991) three-component model of commitment (TCM) has
three subscales labeled affective, continuance, and normative commitment. It is possible
that the affective commitment scale of the TCM might show greater semantic similarity
with a measure of job satisfaction than the other measures of commitment. Fifth, the study
only considered the inter-item correlations between employee engagement and job satis-
faction items. As indicated in the correlation formula (Eq. 1) for composite variables (Cx,
Cy):

5.4 Implications for Research

Notwithstanding addressing the limitations of the present study, there are several consid-
erations for future research. First, manifest validity should be integrated into the unified
theory of construct validity so that it may be considered an integral component of survey
development. As indicated in our work, traditional indicators of construct validity fail to
consider the semantic structure of survey items, which could uncover important challenges
early in the development of new measures. This seems especially salient when examining
latent constructs such as employee engagement whose measurement is often operationalized
by way of survey responses. Second, more research is needed that replicates and extends the
work of the present study, Larsen et al. (2008) and Arnulf et al. (2014). It might be particularly
beneficial to conduct a mixed-methods study to understand how participants interpret items
when the magnitude of inter-scale correlations are about the same but the predictive power of
semantic similarities differs from small to large. Third, it would appreciably advance research
on manifest validity and semantic survey response theory if researchers could contribute their
inter-item correlations to a repository for secondary data analyses (cf. Zientek and Thompson
2009). We encourage researchers to explore this opportunity and to contribute where pos-
sible. Fourth, future research might use LSA to objectively quantify the semantic similarity
between measures of employee engagement and affect.

5.5 Implications for Practice

When interpreting survey results, our recommendation is that practitioners and scholar-
practitioners replicate both the method and analyses reported in this paper and assess the
degree to which semantic similarities accounts for survey responses. In some cases surveys
may need to be designed with fewer items and scales as a means to avoid survey fatigue. In
other cases, it may be necessary to find and develop instruments that have stronger levels
of manifest validity.
    As a method, practitioners and scholar-practitioners may also find LSA useful in other
applications beyond survey data analysis. It could be used alongside other techniques to
determine the appropriateness of aggregating data from multiple instruments. An example
is the meta-analysis on employee engagement conducted by Christian et al. (2011). In their
study, they aggregated data derived from multiple measures of employee engagement.
Although they selected employee engagement instruments that met certain criteria, in-
cluding whether the measure referred to (a) ‘‘the actual work performed’’ and (b) ‘‘a
psychological investment in the work or in the performance of the work’’ (pp. 102–103),
no objective data were provided that indicated how semantically similar the aggregated
employee engagement measures were. Although a practitioner or scholar-practitioner
could subjectively evaluate the semantic equivalence of a set of instruments, LSA offers an
objective measure of semantic equivalence that does not rely on subjective language
perceptions (cf. Larsen et al. 2008).


6 Conclusion

Our work sought to uncover the extent to which correlations between employee engage-
ment and job satisfaction scores were a function of semantic equivalence. Evidence sug-
gested that similarity indices resulting from LSA predicted moderate to large amounts of


                                                                                      123
1168                                                                                       K. Nimon et al.


variance in survey responses. We offered two considerations from our findings including
competing theoretical explanations related to semantic equivalence as well as the potential
that affect serves as a common construct. While our work identified the need for more
research to understand how participants respond to items in the face of semantic similarity,
it illuminates the opportunity that data from semantic text processing algorithms have on
informing survey design and analysis.
    We hope our work can guide researchers who have encountered data where the con-
ceptual distinctiveness of constructs has been called into question, due to high inter-scale
correlations. The method employed in this study is accessible to a wide body of scholars
and scholar-practitioners and offers a way to test for a form of validity (i.e., manifest
validity) that has largely gone ignored or has been considered with less objective means.
Certainly, our work is but one exploration into how semantic overlap among items explain
variation in survey responses. There remains much work for future scholars and practi-
tioners to examine whether correlations among constructs reflect the theoretical influence
of one variable on another or is simply an artifact of semantic overlap.
