Language or syndrome? Investigating the origins of the general
psychopathology factor through large language
model embeddings
Hiroki Kojima, PhD,1† Takafumi Soda, PhD

1†

and Yuichi Yamashita, MD, PhD

Aims: Psychiatric diagnostic categories are challenged by
comorbidity, symptom heterogeneity, and blurred nosological boundaries, sparking interest in trans-diagnostic dimensions such as the general psychopathology factor (p-factor).
Yet it remains unclear whether p-factor represents a valid
psychopathological construct or merely echoes semantic
overlap among questionnaire items. Leveraging recent
advances in large language models (LLMs), we conduct an
empirical investigation that aims to disentangle linguistic
similarity from syndromic co-occurrence in the p-factor.
Methods: We analyzed 145 symptom items spanning eight
diagnostic categories with two similarity metrics: (1) Semantic—
cosine similarity of LLM embeddings; and (2) Syndromic—
polychoric correlations of responses from 985 community
participants. Exploratory factor analyses extracted latent structures, their alignment was quantiﬁed, and factor scores were
tested against lifetime psychiatric diagnoses.
Results: Both similarity matrices converged on a dominant
p-factor with high cross-diagnostic loadings. Loading patterns
The diagnosis of mental disorders has traditionally relied on a
categorical framework, as exempliﬁed by the DSM (Diagnostic and
Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders).1 This approach emphasizes
the assignment of diagnostic labels to distinct mental disorders based
on reported symptomatology. However, this method has notable
limitations, as extensive research indicates that mental disorder diagnoses and symptoms frequently exhibit high correlations.2,3 In many
cases, multiple disorders co-occur simultaneously (i.e., comorbidity),
suggesting that a purely categorical system may inadequately capture
the complexity of symptom overlap.
In response to these limitations, factor analysis has emerged
as a widely utilized methodology for conceptualizing mental
disorders from a transdiagnostic perspective.4,5 This approach
facilitates a dimensional understanding of psychopathology
by grouping symptoms according to shared latent factors. For example, one commonly proposed classiﬁcation scheme identiﬁes internalizing symptoms, externalizing symptoms, and thought disorder
dimensions.6,7 More recently, an even broader conceptualization has
been introduced, the general psychopathology factor (p-factor),
which seeks to account for common elements across diverse mental
disorders.8,9 This framework advances the ﬁeld by shifting the focus

Despite the utility of the p-factor model, several unresolved
questions persist.10,11 One critical issue concerns the underlying origins of the p-factor.12,13 Some researchers attribute the p-factor to
genetic inﬂuences, whereas others contend that it may represent an
artifact of social desirability bias—that is, individuals responding to
assessments in ways that align with socially acceptable norms rather
than accurately reﬂecting their psychological state.11,12,14 Additionally, alternative perspectives suggest that the p-factor reﬂects broader
dispositional traits, such as negative emotionality,15 deﬁcits in selfregulation,16 or variations in cognitive ability.17 From this standpoint,
the p-factor may not constitute a distinct psychopathological entity
but rather emerge from stable personality traits or cognitive capacities. These divergent theoretical perspectives highlight the ongoing
debate regarding the nature of the p-factor and underscore the need
for continued empirical investigation and conceptual reﬁnement.
One potential origin of the p-factor that has not been sufﬁciently
explored is the inﬂuence of linguistic properties inherent in the symptom assessment process. It has long been recognized that descriptions
of human traits are inextricably tied to language. In personality

and properties are reﬂected in our vocabulary.18 Although these
debates provide a conceptual foundation for reconsidering how language and semantics might contribute to the emergence of p-factor,
they have not yet been applied directly to it. Extending these ideas,
we propose a novel hypothesis that the p-factor emerges, in part, from
the linguistic properties of symptom assessments. Speciﬁcally, the
p-factor is typically derived from diagnostic labels assigned to
mental disorders8,9 or responses to questionnaire items designed to
assess psychiatric symptoms.19,20 The linguistic structure and
phrasing of these diagnostic criteria and questionnaire items may
introduce systematic biases that contribute to the emergence of a
general factor. For instance, text similarity, including overlapping
wording, shared semantic content, and common descriptive frameworks, across diagnostic criteria and questionnaire items may artiﬁcially inﬂate correlations among responses independent of any
underlying psychopathological construct.
The present study aims to investigate whether the factor structures of psychopathology, including the p-factor, are shaped by the
textual properties of questionnaire items (e.g., lexical similarity and
semantic overlap) rather than by the syndromic co-occurrence of mental disorders. Historically, quantitatively testing this hypothesis has
been challenging due to methodological limitations. However, recent
advancements in natural language processing (NLP), particularly the
development of language embedding models and large language
models (LLMs), now allow for a more rigorous examination of these
effects. Notably, recent research has demonstrated that personality
factor structures extracted from questionnaire text using language
embedding models closely resemble those derived from actual human
response data.21,22 Building upon these ﬁndings, we examined the
questionnaire items commonly used in mental disorder symptom
assessments and conducted a quantitative analysis of the extent to
which the factor structures of psychopathology can be inferred from
textual similarity using language embedding models. To achieve this,
we utilized language embedding models to quantify the textual similarities among questionnaire items assessing mental disorders. We
then compared these text-based similarities to response similarity patterns derived from actual response scores collected from approximately 1,000 individuals in the general population. This approach
enabled us to assess whether the factor structures, including the
p-factor, identiﬁed through language embeddings align with those
obtained from empirical response data. Additionally, we analyzed the
differences between these two approaches to delineate their respective
contributions to the factor structures of psychopathology.
Through these analyses, we aim to clarify the extent to which
factor structures emerge purely from the linguistic properties of questionnaire items versus those derived from actual symptom cooccurrence in response data. Furthermore, this study highlights the
potential of integrating language embedding models with traditional
factor analysis to enhance the precision of psychiatric assessment.

Results
Comparison Between Similarity Matrices
First, we directly compared the two similarity matrices, S rsp and S emb .
Figure 1 shows the similarity matrices for questionnaire items based
on participants’ responses (left) and text embeddings (right). Upon
inspection, several common block structures were observed in these
similarity matrices, indicating that the matrices were correlated
with each other. The similarities of items within the same category
were relatively high (indicated by strong red), while the similarities between different categories (especially between the AUDIT
and other categories) were relatively low (indicated by weak red).
However, the matrices did not fully align, indicating that while
language embeddings capture some overlap in the underlying
structure, they do not entirely replicate the patterns observed in
the question-response data.

We also visualized the results using a scatter plot (Fig. 2) to
further investigate the similarities and differences between the two
similarity matrices. We found that these two matrices were positively
correlated (r = 0.55), though some discrepancies were observed.
A typical example of a pair of items with high response similarity and low text similarity was between ‘I am mad (item 6 in the
State-trait Anger Expression Inventory: STAXI)’ and ‘I feel like
banging on the table (item 7 in the STAXI)’ (r = 0.89 in participants’
responses, r = 0.23 in text embeddings). On the other hand, a typical
example of a pair of items with low response similarity and high text
similarity was between ‘I ﬁnd it difﬁcult to touch an object when I
know it has been touched by strangers or certain people (item 22 in
the Obsessive-Compulsive Inventory: OCI)’ and ‘I feel very uneasy
talking to people I do not know well (item 21 in the Schizotypal
Personality Questionnaire-Brief: SPQB)’ (r = 0.06 in participants’
responses, r = 0.47 in text embeddings). The similarities between
and within the questionnaires are presented in Figure S1. These
analyses highlight both the commonalities and differences between
the similarity matrices derived from participants’ responses and
text embeddings.
Similarity Between Factor Structures
Then, we applied factor analysis to these two similarity matrices.
First, we analyzed the decay of eigenvalues (Fig. 3). In the similarity matrix of participants’ responses, the ﬁrst principal component
explained 33.2% of the total variance, followed by 5.84% and 5.27%.
Similarly, in the text embeddings, 29.5% of the variance was
explained by the ﬁrst component, followed by 5.02% and 4.16%. This
observation suggests that the variance in both similarity matrices was
primarily accounted for by the ﬁrst component.
Since the number of factors was not clearly deﬁned in participants’ responses, further analysis was conducted using various numbers of factors (details are shown in Table S2). Although we
prioritized the number of factors based on participants’ responses to
ensure the analysis used the same number of factors, the number of
factors cannot be determined for text embeddings. However, following previous research23, we report the factor model with 12 factors in
the main text. The results using different numbers of factors are presented in Figure S1, and the ﬁndings are generalizable.
Next, we derived the factor loadings from the similarity matrix
of text embeddings (bottom panel in Fig. 4) and participants’
responses (top panel in Fig. 4). The loadings for general factors were
higher across all items in both matrices (Fig. 4).
Based on the content of the questionnaire items, we assigned
labels for the speciﬁc factors. The factors identiﬁed in the text
embeddings were interpreted as ‘Alcohol’, ‘Checking’, ‘Ablutomania’,
‘Insomnia’, ‘Amnesia’, ‘Suicide’, ‘Intrusive Thoughts’, ‘Shyness’,
‘Derealization’, ‘Destructive Impulse’, and ‘Depression’. In contrast,
previous research23 indicated that the factors in participants’ responses
could be interpreted as ‘Alcohol’, ‘Dissociation’, ‘Anger’, ‘InsomniaSomatic’, ‘Ablutomania’, ‘Withdrawal’, ‘Checking’, ‘Hopelessness’,
‘Spirituality’, ‘NumberPersistence’, and ‘Bizarreness’. While the labels
between the two types of data share common elements, some differences were also observed. An interesting point is that, while
insomnia symptoms and somatic symptoms formed a single factor
in the participants’ responses (‘Insomnia-Somatic’ factor), only
insomnia symptoms constituted a single factor in the text embeddings (‘Insomnia’ factor). This suggests that, although insomnia
and physical symptoms are not semantically similar as questionnaire items, they might correlate through syndromic co-occurrence
due to bodily or physiological bases.
To quantitatively investigate the similarity between the factors of
participants’ responses and text embeddings, congruence coefﬁcients
were analyzed (Fig. 5). The congruence coefﬁcient for the general
factor showed a sufﬁcient value (0.97). In contrast, the other congruence coefﬁcients (e.g., between speciﬁc factors) did not demonstrate
sufﬁciently high values. Although the highest value was 0.89, which

Discussion
The aim of this study was to determine whether transdiagnostic factors, including p-factors, are driven by text similarity or by underlying
syndromic co-occurrence related to mental disorders. To address this,
we combined language embedding models and factor analysis to analyze mental disorder assessment scales. We then compared the factor
structures derived from text embeddings with those based on actual
participant response scores, using data from approximately 1000 individuals in the general population.
The results revealed similarities between latent factors derived
from text embeddings and participant responses. The similarity matrices generated from both data types demonstrated comparable clustering patterns, with variances primarily explained by the ﬁrst
component. Additionally, both approaches produced general factors
with consistently high loadings across a wide range of questionnaires.
These ﬁndings indicate that most of the factor structure observed in
response data can be reconstructed from the questionnaire texts alone.
In other words, when responses are similar, it is mainly because the
questions asked are similar, rather than reﬂecting underlying syndromic co-occurrence.
However, notable differences were observed. The LLM may have
primarily focused on the behavioral aspects of the questions, potentially overlooking their emotional context, as demonstrated by the
similarity between item 6 and item 7 in the STAXI. Furthermore, as
shown by the similarity between item 22 in the OCI and item 21 in
the SPQB, common wording could result in stronger similarities in
text embeddings, even when the items represent distinct psychopathological characteristics.
Moreover, when examining criterion-related validity through a
history of diagnoses, important differences emerged. Speciﬁcally, the
association between factor scores and mental disorder history was
stronger when using participant response data than when using text
embeddings. This suggests that participant responses capture critical
features related to mental disorders that text embeddings fail to fully
reﬂect. This difference implies that the weighting of response data
may differ between the two quantiﬁcation methods. In fact, when
comparing the factor loadings of the general factor for both
approaches, we found that, for response data, questionnaires related
to emotions had relatively higher loadings. This result suggests that
the latent space of transdiagnostic factors constructed from response
data is somewhat misaligned with that constructed from text embeddings. We suspect that this discrepancy reﬂects syndromic cooccurrences, which may enhance diagnostic predictions.
Previous studies have shown that the p-factor is associated with
a wide range of indicators, including personality traits and cognitive
functioning.15–17 Additionally, genetic studies have reported associations between the p-factor and heritability.24–26 These genetic
associations suggest that the p-factor reﬂects underlying biological
predispositions that cannot be accounted for by text similarities alone.
These non-linguistic components of the p-factor may help explain the
strong associations we observed between the p-factor and diagnostic
history in our ﬁndings.

The utility of LLMs and text embedding models has gained
increasing recognition in psychology and psychiatry.27 Several studies
have analyzed the text embeddings of questionnaires, reporting similarities between text embeddings and response data.21,22,28 For
instance, Cutler and Condon21 and Guenole et al.22 examined the
similarity between text embeddings and participant responses, focusing on personality questionnaires. However, psychopathology questionnaires have not been fully investigated through this type of
analysis, except for the studies by Kambeitz et al.28 and Milano
et al.,29 which measured the correlation between response similarity
and text similarity for several specialized psychopathology questionnaires. Therefore, their study covered only a single, limited domain.
Our study extends these ﬁndings by analyzing the transdiagnostic and
hierarchical factor structure, including the general factor (p-factor),
using multiple questionnaires. Additionally, we evaluated the criterionrelated validity of transdiagnostic factors, revealing that response data
captured unique features related to mental disorders that were not
reﬂected in text embeddings.
The ﬁndings from our study suggest several directions for future
research in psychopathology and psychiatry using natural language
processing, including LLMs. Several studies have highlighted the
utility of LLMs and NLP in these ﬁelds, including analysis of
questionnaire items and narrative data, generation and reﬁnement of
psychological constructs, and the creation of mental disorder
agents.21,22,28–31 However, certain psychopathological features remain
uncaptured by LLMs, emphasizing the need for real human data, such
as behavioral data, alongside LLM analysis results. An interesting
direction for future work is the selection of questionnaire items. Questionnaire items are often designed with deliberate redundancy—
adding semantically similar questions to boost reliability and robustness.32,33 However, this practice can create a correlational structure
driven more by item wording than by true symptom patterns and
result in questionnaires composed of excessively redundant items.
Text similarity measures based on large language models may allow
us to reduce excessive item redundancy without sacriﬁcing measurement precision. Furthermore, methods based on LLMs have also been
proposed to analyze the overlap and distinctions between psychological constructs,34 which may contribute to reﬁning both dimensional
concepts and categorical diagnoses of mental disorders.
Furthermore, the ﬁnding of differences between response data
and text embeddings suggests new assessment strategies for psychopathology. Speciﬁcally, the presence of such discrepancies suggests
that text similarity in language does not fully account for symptom
co-occurrence, and that the two are not equivalent. By identifying and
removing this inﬂuence, it may be possible to extract transdiagnostic
factors based on syndromic co-occurrence, which could quantify the
severity of participants’ ‘pure’ psychopathology. However, such
adjustments only make sense if there is a substantive divergence
between text similarity and symptom co-occurrence. Therefore, it is
important to carefully analyze and distinguish between these two
aspects. In relation to this, methods for estimating individual differences using text embeddings also need further reﬁnement. While we
calculated scores for transdiagnostic factors based on text embeddings
by combining response data with factor structures derived from text
similarity matrices, we did not fully decompose the inﬂuence of
semantic similarity and syndromic co-occurrence. One challenge in
separating latent factors based on semantic similarity from those
based on syndromic co-occurrence may arise from the fact that
participant-speciﬁc data for text embeddings do not exist. Future
research could address this difﬁculty by constructing a Bayesian
model to estimate participant-speciﬁc text embedding scores as latent
variables.
This study has some limitations. First, there are issues related to
the sample. The data were collected through an online survey, which
was limited to the general population in Japan. Future studies should
recruit participants using alternative methods and include data from
other countries to enhance generalizability. Additionally, as this was a
secondary analysis, the possibility of some inherent biases cannot be

Similarity Matrix
Participants’ Responses

For factor analysis, we constructed two types of similarity matrices:
response similarity (S rsp ) and text similarity (S emb ).
To construct response similarity (S rsp ), we utilized response data
obtained from the psychiatric symptom questionnaires described earlier. We computed polychoric correlations between participants’
response patterns for each questionnaire item. Polychoric correlation
was selected as the most appropriate method because the questionnaire items were ordinal and exhibited skewed distributions, making
it more suitable than Pearson correlation for estimating the latent relationships between ordinal variables.

Conclusions
This study examined whether transdiagnostic factors, including
p-factors, stem from questionnaire text properties or underlying syndromic co-occurrence. Comparing text embeddings with participant
responses in approximately 1000 individuals, both methods showed
similar clustering patterns and general factors. However, participant
responses showed stronger associations with mental disorder history
than text embeddings, highlighting their ability to capture some
important features. This study suggests a new direction for the assessment of psychopathology using LLMs.

Methods
Participants, Measures, and Procedure
This study analyzed 145 items from standardized psychiatric symptom
questionnaires, encompassing a broad range of mental health conditions.
The measures included: Schizotypal Personality Questionnaire-Brief
(SPQB), a 22-item measure assessing schizotypal personality disorder
symptoms, using binary response options35,36; Obsessive-Compulsive
Inventory (OCI), a 42-item questionnaire evaluating obsessivecompulsive symptoms, rated on a ﬁve-point Likert scale37,38; Patient
Health Questionnaire (PHQ), a nine-item measure of depressive
symptoms, rated on a four-point scale39,40; Insomnia Severity Index
(ISI), a seven-item scale measuring insomnia severity, with ﬁve-level
response options,41,42 Alcohol Use Disorders Identiﬁcation Test
(AUDIT), a 10-item assessment of alcohol use disorder, with eight items
rated on a ﬁve-point scale and two items rated on a three-point scale43,44;
state anger from State–trait Anger Expression Inventory (STAXI), a
10-item measure assessing irritability symptoms, with four-level response
options,45,46 Dissociative Experiences Scale-II (DES), a 28-item measure
of dissociative symptoms, rated on an 11-point scale47,48; and Millon
Clinical Multiaxial Inventory-II Borderline Scale (MCMI), a
17-item measure assessing borderline personality traits, using
binary response options.49,50 All measures were administered in
their validated Japanese-translated versions, which have demonstrated sufﬁcient reliability.35,38,40,42,43,46,48,49 For all scales, scores were
transformed so that higher scores indicated greater symptom severity.
Response data were collected from 985 participants (female =
607; male = 378) through an online survey conducted in Japan. The
mean participant age was 38.87 years (standard deviation = 9.91).
Participants were limited to those who could read Japanese. Additionally, participants’ diagnostic history was obtained (collected diagnoses
included schizophrenia, mood disorders, anxiety disorders, obsessivecompulsive disorders, developmental disorders, and unspeciﬁed
disorders).
The dataset was originally collected to examine the reliability
and validity of the DSM-5 Level 1 Cross-Cutting Symptom Measure.23 The protocol used to collect the data was approved by the
ethics committees of the Senshu University (20-S002-4). These studies were carried out in accordance with the provisions of the World
Medical Association Declaration of Helsinki. Participants provided
informed consent by clicking ‘I agree’ after reviewing the study
information.

For text similarity (S emb ), we used text embedding vectors
corresponding to the same questionnaire items administered to participants. Some questionnaire items did not explicitly include symptomrelated wording in their main question but rather embedded it within
the Likert scale response options. To ensure accurate semantic representation, we revised the text for these items. This procedure was
applied only to a small number of items (three related to sleep), and
the original wording was preserved (the revised sentences are provided in Table S1).
We employed OpenAI Embeddings (text-embedding-3-small) to
convert each questionnaire item into an embedding vector. The text
was processed in Japanese, and similarity between the resulting
embedding vectors was computed using cosine similarity, a widely
used metric for quantifying semantic similarity in high-dimensional
vector spaces.
Additionally, we assessed the robustness of our ﬁndings across
different embedding methods to ensure reproducibility. First, we generated embeddings using models other than OpenAI’s text-embedding-3-small: speciﬁcally, sBERT (ruri-v3-130m), MiniLM (L12-v2),
and multilingual BERT with pooling. To evaluate any inﬂuence of
the Japanese language, we also used the corresponding Englishlanguage questionnaire items through the OpenAI embedding model
to generate embeddings, then repeated our analyses to see if the
results would replicate. However, for the MCMI, we were unable to
obtain the corresponding English-language items, so we instead used
machine-translated versions of the Japanese items.

Factor Analysis
We applied factor analysis to the obtained similarity matrices as follows. This same procedure was used for different similarity matrices
in our analysis.
First, we explored the number of factors using a similarity matrix
of participants’ responses. Because we could not determine a clear
number of factors using conventional methods (Table S2), we conducted broad models with factors ranging from 3 to 19. A solution
with 19 factors was suggested by parallel analysis. We selected the
factor solution with as many factors as possible for representative
results, ensuring that all factors were interpretable. We focus on
reporting these representative results (i.e., the model with 12 factors),
but the ﬁndings are generalizable. Next, exploratory factor analysis

We analyzed the diagnostic predictive power using factor scores
derived from the results of factor analysis. Factor scores were estimated using the tenBerge method, as preliminary analysis showed
that it effectively reconstructed factor correlations in participants’
responses. It is important to note that when calculating factor scores,
the factor loadings and factor correlations were derived from each
similarity matrix, but the same response data were used in both cases.
Roughly, for text embedding, factor scores for each participant were
calculated from their response data by weighting based on the factor
loadings derived from text similarity. Next, a multiple regression analysis was conducted to predict the presence or absence of a diagnosis
