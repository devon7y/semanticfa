Abstract
Dictionary-based text analysis, where researchers select keywords to measure constructs such
as public sentiment, anxiety, or political attitudes in large text corpora, is widely used in
computational social science. However, keyword selection is rarely subjected to the same
psychometric scrutiny applied to survey instruments: studies seldom report reliability, evaluate
internal structure, or test whether the measurement holds across subpopulations or time points.
Moreover, few existing methods enable the construction of measures that reflect theoretical or
expected relationships among keywords. This paper proposes a method that brings these
capabilities to text analysis by applying Confirmatory Factor Analysis (CFA) to word
embeddings. Keywords are treated as observed indicators of a latent construct, and their
semantic relationships, operationalized as centered cosine similarities between embedding
vectors, serve as the input correlation matrix for CFA estimation. The framework enables
researchers to estimate factor loadings and model fit indices (CFI, TLI, RMSEA, SRMR),
compute reliability coefficients (Cronbach's alpha, Omega), and test measurement invariance
across groups or time periods using multigroup models with structured means. Moreover, the
method allows researchers to compare latent construct intensity across groups or time periods,
transforming keyword-based text measures from descriptive indicators into formally
comparable latent variables. The method is demonstrated through an empirical application of
the discourse of war anxiety during Russia's 2022 invasion of Ukraine. A Monte Carlo
simulation further examines the behavior of fit indices under random keyword selection. The
approach complements existing text analysis methods and can be implemented using standard
software, such as the lavaan R package.
Keywords: computational social science, text-as-data, measurement validity, confirmatory
factor analysis, word embeddings, measurement invariance, latent variable modeling, keyword
selection

Introduction
Digitalization has greatly increased the volume of electronically stored text, including social
media posts, forums, blogs, reviews, news, and digitized publications. These unstructured data
provide social scientists with a rich source of information about beliefs, attitudes, behavior, and
social interaction, making text analysis an increasingly important field of study (Salganik,
2019; Zachlod et al., 2022). More generally, electronic traces from social media platforms
could allow researchers to track outcomes frequently, investigate regional diversity, and
conduct large-scale network analysis quickly and cost-effectively (Golder & Macy, 2014).
Moreover, proponents of user-generated text data indicate that the informal and spontaneous
nature of such data presents a unique opportunity to capture authentic human sentiment,
opinions, and social dynamics that are not easily accessible through structured surveys
(Stephens-Davidowitz, 2017).
However, the field is relatively new and has yet to develop standard procedures to handle all
methodological and statistical issues. This is particularly important given the nonrepresentative nature of user-generated data (Kapoor et al., 2018), ethical concerns (Zimmer &
Kinder-Kurlanda, 2017), biases introduced by algorithms, and user behavior (Olteanu et al.,
2019). Moreover, proper keyword indicator selection that defines a measurement remains a
constant and often overlooked challenge. As Grimmer and Stewart (2013) emphasize, all
quantitative models of text require validation. Yet in practice, studies based on user-generated
text rarely report the reliability of their measures, the structure of the measurement model, or
its fit to the data (Lowe & Benoit, 2013). A growing literature in computational social science
addresses the validation of text-based measures (e.g., van Atteveldt et al., 2021; Boukes et al.,
2020; Kantner & Overbeck, 2020), but confirmatory psychometric tools, specifically CFA with
model fit indices, reliability coefficients, and measurement invariance testing, have not been

applied to researcher-selected keyword sets using word embeddings. This gap is the central
focus of this article.
This paper introduces a statistical approach for constructing and evaluating keyword-based
measures from unstructured text. Grounded in Confirmatory Factor Analysis (CFA), it allows
researchers to assess reliability, internal structure, and measurement invariance, and to model
theoretically expected relationships among keywords.

Figure 1 illustrates the core idea of the proposed approach using a simplified example with
three keywords from a war-related corpus. Each keyword is represented as a high-dimensional
vector learned from the corpus (a). After mean-centering, pairwise centered cosine similarities

share contextual usage patterns. This matrix serves as input to a CFA model, in this example,
a one-factor CFA model (c), where factor loadings indicate how strongly each keyword relates
to a common latent dimension, and error terms capture word-specific variance unrelated to the
shared construct. The resulting fit indices and reliability estimates (d) allow the researcher to
evaluate empirically whether the selected keywords form a coherent measurement instrument,
a step that is standard practice in statistical analysis but has been largely absent from keywordbased text analysis.

Measurement and composite indicators in textual data
Analyzing textual data according to a specific theoretical aim requires the selection of proper
indicators. This often involves selecting keywords or phrases, which can be used to gather,
filter, or highlight data that could be used in subsequent steps of analysis (Bordoloi & Biswas,
2023). For example, Antenucci et al. (2014) developed a "job loss" index by monitoring the
frequency of tweets containing terms like "fired," "axed," and "canned" to predict U.S.
unemployment trends. Similarly, Boon-Itt and Skunkan (2020) analyzed the frequency of
keywords such as "outbreak" and "pandemic" to evaluate public engagement with the COVID19 situation. Abdul Reda, Sinanoglu, and Abdalla (2024) focused on measuring the resource
mobilization efforts of social movements on Twitter. They identified a specific lexicon,
including terms like "sit-in," "protest," "rally," "picket," "strike," etc. Rosenberg et al. (2023)
analyzed Twitter data to gauge public sentiment towards climate action by focusing on 113
keywords such as “energy”, “carbon”, “climate change”, “resilience”, and “global warming”.
Another example is immigration sentiment measured by investigating Twitter posts that
contain, among other keywords: "immigration", "border", "migration", and "refugees" (Rowe
et al. 2021).
In a typical social-science application of textual data analysis, keyword selection yields a
lexicon of relevant terms. This lexicon is then used to estimate relative word frequencies over
time, providing a simple descriptive signal of how salient a phenomenon is in the corpus
(Bordoloi & Biswas, 2023; Zachlod et al., 2022). More complex workflows include sentiment
analysis, which estimates the evaluative tone of texts and can be extended to discrete emotion
categories. For example, Tavoschi et al. (2020) used machine-learning to classify tweets as
supporting or opposing vaccination; Rosenberg et al. (2023) applied VADER, TextBlob, and
BERT to assess sentiment in climate-action tweets; and Loureiro and Alló (2020) examined
expressions of anger, joy, fear, and surprise in energy and climate change tweets.

The process of selecting keywords for textual analysis can be viewed as a process of
constructing a complex indicator intended to reflect a specific phenomenon or population
characteristic. Keywords could serve as observable features of a latent construct in reflective
measurement models or as observable indicators of a composite in formative models (Henseler,
2020, Diamantopoulos & Winklhofer, 2001). In both instances, keyword selection should be
grounded in theoretical considerations and validated through an appropriate measurement
model. This validation may range from assessing the reliability of the keyword-based measure
to performing more advanced analyses, such as factor analysis, which evaluates whether the
measure's structure aligns with the underlying theoretical framework (Bollen & Lennox, 1991).
Additionally, empirical testing of indicator adequacy is essential to ensure that the selected
keywords are valid and reliable. This step also helps identify whether any specific keywords
pose issues that could affect the use of the indicator. This process of constructing indicators,
however, isn’t a novel task unique to text analysis. On the contrary, social scientists have been
facing similar problems for ages, leading to the development of robust and methodological
frameworks that could guide the development and evaluation of state-of-the-art indicators
(DeVellis, 2017). Transferring these practices to the field of textual data analysis would elevate
the scientific rigor of the field. An early example of such transfer exists already in topic
modeling, which employs exploratory methods to identify and cluster words or phrases. This,
however, is an example of exploratory rather than confirmatory analysis (Blei, Ng, & Jordan,
2003).
The field of text analysis currently lacks the tools to assess the validity and reliability of
keyword-based measurements empirically as well tools to compute measures aligned to
theoretical or desired structure. To fill this gap, this paper presents how a set of tools previously
established in the social sciences can be applied in computational linguistics, textual data
analysis, and computational social science in general. By bridging these disciplines, a more

robust framework for verifying and refining keyword selection, ensuring alignment with
theoretical constructs, and enhancing the rigor of sentiment and textual analysis is provided.

Word embeddings
Word embeddings are a technique for representing words as vectors in a continuous, highdimensional space, so that words used in similar contexts have similar representations
(Mikolov et al., 2013). Embeddings underpin many modern language models and have enabled
a range of social-science applications (Arseniev-Koehler, 2022). In Word2Vec, embeddings
are learned either by predicting a target word from its surrounding context (Continuous Bagof-Words, CBOW) or by predicting surrounding context words from a target word (Skipgram).
Embeddings are learned by iteratively adjusting randomly initialized word vectors through
prediction and backpropagation until words appearing in similar contexts receive similar
representations (Mikolov et al., 2013).
In addition to these traditional methods for creating word embeddings, more sophisticated
models have emerged, such as BERT (Bidirectional Encoder Representations from
Transformers). Unlike Word2Vec, BERT uses a transformer architecture and takes a
bidirectional approach to understand the context of a word. Importantly, BERT operates on
sub-word tokens rather than whole words, which allows it to handle rare and out-of-vocabulary
terms by decomposing them into known sub-word units. Instead of using a fixed window,
BERT captures the entire sentence context on both sides of a word to generate embeddings that
are deeply contextualized (Devlin et al., 2018).
Word vectors, generated using algorithms like Word2Vec or BERT, enable models to capture
semantic relationships between words by analyzing their contexts in large text corpora. Words

with similar meanings or that appear in similar contexts are represented by vectors close to
each other in a high-dimensional space. One of the most well-known examples of such a
relationship is between the words “king” and “queen”. Through word vectors, it becomes
evident that the difference between the vectors for "king" and "man" is similar to the difference
between "queen" and "woman." This relationship can be mathematically expressed as king man + woman ≈ queen (Mikolov, Sutskever, et al. 2013b).

the cosine of the angle between two vectors in a multidimensional space. The formula is:

From cosine similarity to correlation
Some researchers argue, however, that CS may not always be the best measure for evaluating
the relationships between word embeddings. For instance, Ethayarajh (2019) highlights that
representations produced by deep models like BERT tend to show high CS between random
word pairs, particularly at the higher layers of the model. This is attributed to a distributional
bias in the embedding space, where vectors tend to be concentrated in narrow regions rather
than spread uniformly. This distributional bias can obscure the true similarity relationships
between word and sentence representations, potentially leading to suboptimal performance in

NLP tasks. Similar concerns were raised by Mu and Viswanath (2018), who also observed that
the over-similarity between embeddings might distort downstream NLP performance.

Both metrics fundamentally focus on comparing the relative orientation and variation of the
vectors, independent of their magnitudes. In other words, once the vectors are mean-centered,
their magnitudes (the actual lengths of the vectors) no longer play a role in the similarity
calculation; instead, the focus shifts entirely to their directional relationship. When the vectors
are centered by subtracting their means, the resulting vectors exhibit zero mean, and the CS
effectively becomes a measure of how two vectors co-vary in their direction. Pearson's

correlation coefficient quantifies this: the degree to which two variables (or vectors, in this
case) co-vary linearly, normalized by their respective standard deviations. Having a correlation
between variables allows us to utilize statistical models such as Confirmatory Factor Analysis
(CFA), which allows for testing hypothesized relationships and the structure of latent
constructs within the textual data.

Confirmatory Factor Analysis with Word Embeddings
Structural models for covariance matrices (Jöreskog 1978), such as CFA and Structural
Equation Modeling (SEM), are standard tools in empirical investigations in many fields of
research. They provide a general framework for testing hypotheses about the relationships
between observed variables and underlying latent constructs, allowing researchers to specify
and evaluate complex relationships in data. This paper shows how word embeddings can be
incorporated into a CFA model (and by extensions to SEM) to analyze the underlying semantic
relationships between words by modeling these embeddings as observed indicators.
In this context, the observed distances between word embeddings (mean-centered vectors) are
modeled using the CFA structure, where the latent factors represent broader semantic
dimensions, such as topics or abstract concepts derived from the text. For one factor, the word
embedding CFA model can be specified as:

We can assess how well the hypothesized latent semantic structure fits the data by fitting this
CFA model to the centered word embeddings. By examining how close the model-implied
covariance is to real covariance (or, in our case, correlations), fit indices could be computed.
The model-fit indices help determine whether the relationships between the word embeddings
and the latent factors are consistent with our imposed structure, and simple indices like
reliability could be estimated. The most commonly used fit indices in CFA include: the Chisquare (χ²) test, which assesses exact model fit (non-significant values suggest good fit); the
Comparative Fit Index (CFI) and Tucker-Lewis Index (TLI), both ranging from 0 to 1, where
values above 0.95 indicate good fit and above 0.90 acceptable fit; the Root Mean Square Error
of Approximation (RMSEA), where values below 0.05 indicate close fit, below 0.08 acceptable
fit, and above 0.10 poor fit; and the Standardized Root Mean Square Residual (SRMR), where
values below 0.08 indicate good fit (Hu & Bentler, 1999). These indices are reported
throughout the empirical examples that follow. This approach allows us to quantitatively
evaluate how well the semantic patterns captured by the word embeddings align with the latent
dimensions in the data, thus offering a method for examining the underlying semantic structure
of textual data.

An important practical question is how researchers should arrive at the hypothesized factor
structure before applying CFA to word embeddings. Several strategies are available, paralleling
standard practice in psychometric research. First, the factor structure may be derived from
substantive theory: if prior research or domain knowledge suggests that a set of keywords
captures distinct but related facets of a construct (e.g., military, economic, and social
dimensions of war anxiety), this theoretical model can be directly specified and tested via CFA.
Second, researchers may use Exploratory Factor Analysis (EFA) on the cosine similarity matrix
as a preliminary step. EFA can reveal the number and composition of latent dimensions in the
keyword set, and its results can then inform a confirmatory specification in a subsequent CFA.
This two-step approach, exploration followed by confirmation, is standard in scale
development (DeVellis, 2017) and translates naturally to the word embedding context. Third,
existing dictionary-based tools (e.g., LIWC categories or domain-specific lexicons) may
provide a starting point for specifying which keywords should load on which factors. Fourth,
topic modeling approaches such as LDA (Blei, Ng, & Jordan, 2003) can serve as an exploratory
complement, identifying clusters of co-occurring words that may correspond to latent
dimensions worth testing confirmatorily. In all cases, the CFA framework proposed here
provides the confirmatory step: it allows researchers to formally test whether the hypothesized
structure fits the data, evaluate model fit, and compare alternative specifications.
There's a caveat, however. Traditionally, structural models rely on covariance matrices, which
preserve the scale of variables, not on correlations that are equivalent to centered cosine
distances. To clarify this equivalence: raw cosine similarity between two word embedding
vectors captures their angular proximity in the full embedding space, including any global
biases in vector orientation. Mean-centering each vector (subtracting the vector’s mean across
dimensions) removes this global component, so that the resulting centered cosine similarity is
mathematically identical to the Pearson correlation coefficient computed across vector

dimensions. In substantive terms, centering strips away the portion of similarity attributable to
the general distributional properties of the embedding space (which can inflate similarity
between unrelated words) and isolates the pattern-specific co-variation between dimensions.
The resulting correlation matrix thus captures how two words share distinctive patterns of
contextual usage, net of global distributional artifacts, making it a more appropriate input for
CFA than raw cosine similarities. Correlation matrices can be used for scale-invariant models
to avoid errors in estimation and interpretation (Cudeck, 1989). Although it may seem
straightforward, determining whether a model is scale-invariant is not always a simple task.
Cudeck proposed a method for testing scale invariance by estimating the model twice: first
using a correlation matrix and then a transformed version of the correlation matrix, such as a
covariance matrix. It is considered scale-invariant if the model exhibits the same goodness-offit and test statistics (e.g., chi-square) before and after scaling the correlation matrix, despite
potential differences in parameter estimates. If the variances or standard deviations of the
observed variables are not known, the correlation matrix used for testing invariance can be
rescaled by assigning arbitrary values to the standard deviations. A simple CFA model, as used
in subsequent examples, is an example of a scale-invariant model. However, even if a model is
scale-invariant, it may still yield incorrect standard errors for parameter estimates that depend
on the scale of the data. This issue particularly affects factor loadings, which, while remaining
invariant in a scale-invariant model, still have scale-dependent parameter values.
In applying CFA to understand the structure of latent variables, inaccuracies in the standard
errors of factor loadings are less critical, as the analysis primarily focuses on factor loadings
and model fit, which are appropriately estimated in this context. It is also worth mentioning
that methods do exist for the correct estimation of standard errors derived from correlation
matrices, though they are not commonly implemented in standard statistical software such as
lavaan R-package or Mplus (Browne & Mels, 1998; Browne, 1982). This area of research has

not seen much further development in recent years, as modern computational power allows
users to conduct analyses on large datasets easily.

Means and covariance structures
Simple CFA models are not the only models we would like to use when analyzing word
embeddings. While a basic CFA can evaluate whether keywords form a coherent construct and
estimate reliability, many research questions require more: comparing the intensity of a
construct across groups (e.g., countries, platforms) or tracking changes over time requires
models with structured means; testing whether a measurement holds equivalently across
subpopulations requires multigroup models with measurement invariance constraints. We will
not present the formulation of those models as there are multiple sources with excellent
descriptions, and providing detailed formulations of more complex models is not necessary to
understand the proposed approach (Bollen 1989; Schumacker & Lomax 2016).
To estimate a model with structured means or a multigroup model, it is necessary to have the
means of the observed indicators and the covariance matrix, which in practice requires
incorporating standard deviations. Two methods are proposed to incorporate a mean structure
into CFA when using word embeddings as inputs, each with distinct advantages and limitations
regarding interpretability.
The first method involves using the average frequency of each word in the corpus under
investigation and calculating the estimated standard deviation. Means and standard deviations
are computed directly from word occurrence frequencies in the corpus. Once the standard
deviations of the indicators are known, transforming the correlation matrix derived from cosine
similarities becomes a simple task (as covariance is correlation multiplied by variances of
variables). This method has the advantage of simplicity and direct interpretability. The latent

means in this approach can be understood as weighted averages of word frequencies; this
allows us to interpret changes in the latent factors as reflecting changes in the frequency of
word usage within the population. However, this method also has its drawbacks. Using word
frequencies for the mean structure may introduce inconsistencies with the covariance structure
derived from word embeddings, as the two are based on different aspects of the data.
Specifically, the inconsistency arises because the covariance structure is derived from semantic
relationships between words (how they co-occur in context windows), whereas the mean
structure is derived from raw word frequencies (how often each word appears). These two data
sources need not align: a word may be semantically central to a construct (high factor loading
from embeddings) yet infrequent, or vice versa. This mismatch means that the covariance and
mean structures reflect different underlying generative processes, which can produce distorted
latent mean estimates if the discrepancy is severe. Additionally, it should be noted that word
frequencies in natural language corpora follow a well-known power-law distribution (Zipf’s
law), where a small number of very common words dominate frequency counts (Piantadosi,
2014). In our application, this concern is partially mitigated because the CFA keywords are
content-specific terms (e.g., “alliance,” “nuclear,” “aggression”) rather than the high-frequency
function words that typically dominate Zipfian distributions. Nevertheless, researchers using
this method should be aware that differences in frequency magnitude across keywords can
influence latent mean estimates and should consider whether log-transformation or other
normalizations are warranted for their particular application.
The second method involves treating the word embeddings themselves as observed indicators
to derive the mean structure. By calculating the means and standard deviations directly from
the word embeddings, we maintain consistency between the mean and covariance structures,
as both are based on the same data source. This method allows researchers to model latent
semantic factors that represent broader concepts or topics within text data, capturing semantic

shifts and changes in discourse over time. Despite its advantages, the interpretability of latent
means derived from word embeddings can be challenging. Word embeddings are highdimensional vectors, and their components are not easily interpretable in the same way as word
frequencies. The latent means in this method represent positions in a semantic space, which
may not have a straightforward interpretation in terms of population-level changes. Comparing
latent means across groups or conditions can reveal differences in semantic usage or conceptual
focus, but not the intensity of phenomena under investigation.
In studies focused on social media, where the key issue is often examining the dynamics of
changes within the population rather than changes in the discourse itself, the first method seems
more appropriate. Using word frequencies to construct the mean structure, we can directly
observe and interpret trends in word usage over time. This aligns with research objectives
focused on understanding shifts in public attention or sentiment, which are critical for
analyzing social media data, and this is the method chosen for the empirical example in this
paper.

Summary of the Proposed Framework
Figure 2 summarizes the proposed framework. The left column presents the data preparation
pipeline. In Step 1, a text corpus is assembled from the data source of interest (e.g., social media
posts, news articles). In Step 2, word embeddings are trained on this corpus using algorithms
such as CBOW or Skip-gram, producing a d-dimensional vector for each word in the
vocabulary. In Step 3, the researcher selects k keyword indicators based on theoretical
reasoning about the construct of interest. In Step 4, the selected keyword vectors are meancentered, and centered cosine similarities are computed, yielding a correlation matrix R that
serves as the primary input for CFA. Optionally, in Step 5, per-keyword frequency statistics

(means and standard deviations) are computed from the corpus, enabling the construction of a
covariance matrix and the incorporation of a mean structure into the model.

Figure 2. Analytical pipeline for Confirmatory Factor Analysis with word embeddings: from

text corpus to psychometric evaluation and multigroup extensions.
The right column presents the modeling and evaluation stages. In Step 6, a CFA model is fitted
to the correlation (or covariance) matrix, where the latent factor ξ represents the hypothesized
construct and factor loadings λ quantify how strongly each keyword relates to it. Step 7 yields
standard psychometric outputs: factor loadings, model fit indices (χ², CFI, TLI, RMSEA,
SRMR), and reliability estimates (Cronbach's α, McDonald's ω). The framework extends
naturally to multigroup CFA, where separate models are estimated for different groups or time
periods and measurement invariance is tested through progressively constrained models
(configural → metric → scalar). Once scalar invariance is established, latent mean comparisons
allow researchers to track construct intensity across groups or over time.

Empirical Examples
The goal of this empirical application is threefold: (1) to demonstrate that keyword-based CFA
with word embeddings produces interpretable factor structures, factor loadings, and fit indices
comparable to those obtained from traditional survey-based CFA; (2) to illustrate how the
framework can be extended to multigroup analysis and measurement invariance testing across
time periods; and (3) to examine whether latent means derived from keyword-based text
measures track temporal trends observed in survey-based measures of a related construct. To
this end, self-reported war anxiety (measured via a survey instrument) and discourse of war
anxiety (captured via Twitter keyword indicators) are modeled in parallel, not as equivalent
measures of the same construct, but as related measures that allow us to showcase the full range
of CFA capabilities in both data contexts.

Data
For the purpose of illustrative analysis, we used data collected in Poland during the early stages
of Russia’s invasion of Ukraine in 2022. Two datasets were utilized. The first is a survey, with
participants aged 18 and older recruited through an internet panel, using quota sampling based
on sex, age, and place of residence. The second dataset consists of posts collected from Twitter
during the same period. In the survey, respondents were asked to assess four statements on a
5-point Likert scale (1 = strongly disagree; 5 = strongly agree): "The war between Russia and
Ukraine may: (1) turn into a world war, (2) turn into a nuclear conflict, (3) spread to Poland
soon, (4) have a disastrous impact on the economy and social life in Poland."
The survey items were designed to capture distinct facets of war-related anxiety: military
escalation (world war, nuclear conflict), geographic proximity of threat (spread to Poland), and
socioeconomic consequences (economy and social life). Following a similar logic, keywords

for the Twitter analysis were selected based on theoretical reasoning about which terms should
appear in public discourse when people discuss similar concerns. This parallel between survey
items and discourse keywords is intentional: just as survey items need not be synonymous to
load on a common factor (each taps a different aspect of the construct), keywords need not
share dictionary definitions to co-vary in discourse about a specific topic. The assumption is
that co-occurrence patterns of theoretically relevant keywords in a war-related corpus may
reflect shared underlying concerns.
This empirical application is intended as a methodological illustration rather than a fully
developed substantive study. Its purpose is to show how CFA can be applied to word
embeddings, not to offer a definitive measurement model of war anxiety.
The Twitter data was collected using the Twitter API for academic research, employing a set
of keywords related to the war. We gathered all posts containing the following words: Ukraine,
War, Refuge, Immigrant, Migrant, Border, Russia, Putin, Warfare, Aid, 500+, Pension, Social,
Crime, Volhynia, Conflict, Atomic, Nuclear, Bomb, Inflation, Crisis, Price, Gasoline, Work,
Prices, Zloty, Franc, Euro, NATO, Tank, Genocide, Imperialism, Fascism, Bander. All posts
were written in Polish; thus, all presented keywords were also applied in its Polish translations.
It is important to distinguish between two sets of keywords that serve different functions in this
analysis. The retrieval keywords listed above were used to query the Twitter API and define
the corpus, that is, to collect all posts related to the war in Ukraine. This is a broad set designed
to capture the full range of war-related discourse. The indicator keywords, introduced below,
are a smaller, theoretically motivated subset selected to serve as indicators of a specific latent
construct (war-related discourse). These two sets are related but not identical: the retrieval
keywords define the textual universe from which word embeddings are trained, while the
indicator keywords are the specific terms whose semantic relationships are modeled as
reflections of an underlying construct. In principle, CFA could also be applied to the retrieval

terms themselves; however, these were chosen for breadth of coverage rather than construct
coherence, and a CFA would likely reveal a multidimensional structure rather than a single
factor.
The details of the data collection are presented in Table 1. It is important to note that during
the first 5 weeks, survey data were collected weekly, while in later periods, data collection
occurred once a month, and only the first three indicators of war anxiety were included. In this
analysis, we utilize datasets from the first eleven weeks.
Standard preprocessing was applied: URL removal, retweet prefix filtering, lowercasing, stopword removal, tokenization, and filtering of tokens shorter than two characters. No
lemmatization or stemming was applied before computing word embeddings. This decision
was deliberate: in Polish, an inflectionally rich language, different morphological forms of the
same lemma (e.g., wojnę, wojny, wojną for “war”) often carry distinct contextual nuances and
appear in different syntactic environments. Word embedding algorithms like CBOW learn
representations from local context windows, meaning that each inflected form receives a vector
shaped by its specific usage contexts. Collapsing these forms through lemmatization would
lose this contextual variation. Moreover, for the CFA analysis, what matters is that the selected
keywords, in their surface forms as they appear in tweets, share systematic semantic variance;
the embedding model captures this directly from the corpus without requiring morphological
normalization. Future research could investigate whether lemmatization affects CFA results,
particularly in morphologically simpler languages.
After cleaning the text data, we trained word embeddings in MATLAB using the default
CBOW settings: 300 dimensions, a context window of 5, five iterations, and a learning rate of
0.05. We retained these settings because they are standard in the literature and help keep the
methodological demonstration transparent and easy to replicate, rather than tailored to a

particular dataset (Mikolov et al., 2013). Nevertheless, hyperparameter choices may affect CFA
results, and future research should examine their impact across corpora and languages.

Next, keywords were selected to serve as potential indicators of war-related discourse. The
selection followed a logic parallel to the survey instrument. If survey items capture fears of
military escalation, nuclear conflict, geographic spread, and socioeconomic consequences, then
public discourse expressing similar concerns should contain words related to these themes.
Specifically:

● alliance and NATO relate to collective security discussions, which in the Polish context
during Russia's invasion of Ukraine are tied to concerns about Poland's safety
guarantees;

● nuclear directly corresponds to fears of nuclear escalation captured by the survey;
● aggression, conflict, and military capture discourse about hostile actions and armed
forces;
● development (Polish: wzrost; also commonly translated as growth), while polysemous
in general language, appears in this war-related corpus predominantly in discussions of
economic consequences, rising prices (wzrost cen), inflation, economic development
prospects, and increases in military spending.

The inclusion of development warrants a brief comment. Economic concerns, including
inflation, recession, trade disruptions, and energy prices, are well-documented components of
public anxiety during armed conflicts. The multidimensional models in Appendix (Table A1)
show that economic terms cluster on a factor correlated with but distinct from the
political/military anxiety factor, consistent with a multidimensional structure of war-related
discourse.

Importantly, good CFA fit indicates that the selected keywords form a semantically coherent
dimension within the corpus, that is, they share systematic variance in their embedding
representations. Substantive interpretation, however, depends on the data context, not on the
keywords alone. Terms such as “alliance,” “nuclear,” “aggression,” and “development” could
co-vary for many reasons in a general-purpose corpus. Here, we interpret the factor as warrelated anxiety discourse because (a) the corpus consists of Polish-language tweets collected
during Russia’s 2022 invasion of Ukraine using war-related retrieval terms, (b) keywords were
selected based on theory paralleling the survey instrument, and (c) latent means track selfreported war anxiety closely (r = 0.873). CFA therefore supports internal coherence and helps
disambiguate a construct within a specific discursive context, while substantive interpretation
must be justified by research design and theory.

The trained word embeddings were used to extract vectors for these specific words. To
standardize the embeddings, the mean for each word vector was calculated and then subtracted
from each vector element, resulting in mean-centered vectors. Cosine similarity was then
calculated for each pair of mean-centered word vectors, resulting in a measure equivalent to
the correlation coefficient. Additionally, the frequency statistics of the words were estimated.
For each term, the mean frequency and standard deviation were calculated. Specifically, for
each CFA keyword, the frequency was computed as the number of occurrences of that word
per tweet, averaged across all tweets in a given weekly corpus; the standard deviation was
calculated across tweets within the same corpus. The unit of measurement for the means and
variances is thus per-tweet word frequency. It is important to clarify that war anxiety discourse
is not measured through sentiment analysis. No sentiment scoring, emotion classification, or
valence detection is applied. Instead, the measurement approach is purely frequency- and cooccurrence-based: the CFA models how frequently selected keywords appear and how their
semantic relationships (captured via word embedding cosine similarities) reflect a common
latent dimension. The assumption is that the intensity of discourse about war-related topics, as
reflected in keyword frequencies and their semantic coherence, serves as a proxy for the
prominence of war anxiety in public discourse.

Fit and reliability of indicators in a one-factor model
In this section, we demonstrate the application of CFA by comparing the traditional surveybased approach with the proposed approach based on word embeddings. The aim is to show
how both approaches can be used side by side to recover interpretable latent structures, while
highlighting differences that follow from the distinct data-generating processes.

The traditional CFA model (Table 2) fitted to self-reported survey data showed strong fit, with
standardized loadings ranging from 0.508 to 0.885 and fit indices close to conventional
benchmarks (CFI and TLI close to 1; RMSEA close to 0). Note, however, that these nearperfect indices should be interpreted cautiously because the model is nearly saturated (df = 2),
leaving limited scope to detect misspecification. Reliability coefficients were computed from
the CFA results (Bentler, 2009; McDonald, 1999): Cronbach’s alpha = 0.858 and omega =
0.874.
In parallel, we applied CFA to word embeddings derived from Twitter posts from the same
period. Keywords such as alliance, nuclear, aggression, and development were treated as
indicators of a latent semantic dimension reflecting conflict-related discourse. Estimation relied
on mean-centered cosine similarities (equivalent to correlations) and was implemented in the
lavaan package (Rosseel, 2012).
Despite the lower factor loadings (ranging from 0.536 to 0.680) compared to the survey model,
the fit indices were still satisfactory, with a CFI of 0.991, TLI of 0.974, and RMSEA of 0.054
(Table 2). Reliability measures, while slightly lower than the survey data, indicated reasonable
consistency, with Cronbach’s Alpha of 0.703 and Omega of 0.705.
The somewhat lower reliability coefficients for the embedding-based model, compared to the
survey model, are expected and reflect an important distinction. When applying CFA to word
embeddings, three sources of disalignment contribute to imperfect scale consistency: (1)
measurement error, (2) idiosyncratic variation in the measured construct, and (3) word uses
and meanings that are simply unrelated to the intended construct. In traditional survey-based
CFA, only the first two sources are typically present, as it is arguably rare that survey
respondents simply answer a different question from the one asked. In contrast, word

embeddings capture the full range of contexts in which a word appears, including those
unrelated to the construct of interest.
Both models demonstrated a similar underlying structure, supporting the use of CFA as a tool
to explore latent constructs across different data types but having similar theoretical
assumptions about the unidimensional latent construct of war anxiety.

Monte Carlo simulations of the fit indices
A key question for applying CFA to word embeddings is whether conventional fit index
thresholds remain informative, or whether the high-dimensional nature of embeddings renders
them problematic. In 300-dimensional word embedding space, any pair of words shares some
degree of vector overlap, and even randomly selected words from a thematically constrained
corpus may exhibit non-trivial cosine similarities. This raises a legitimate concern: could a
CFA model fitted to arbitrary word combinations produce acceptable fit indices simply because
all words in a topic-specific corpus share some baseline semantic similarity? To address this
question empirically, we conducted a Monte Carlo simulation. Words were randomly sampled
from the full vocabulary of our Twitter corpus (which, being collected via war-related retrieval
keywords, is thematically constrained). For each random sample, we computed the centered
cosine similarity matrix and fitted a one-factor CFA model. The logic is as follows: if
theoretically selected keywords produce substantially better fit than random selections from
the same corpus, this constitutes evidence that the theoretical selection captures genuine shared
variance beyond what any arbitrary word combination would yield. Conversely, if random
words routinely achieve good fit, the conventional thresholds would be uninformative for this
type of data. Note that this simulation does not claim that random words lack any shared
variance in a war-related corpus, most words are loosely connected to the overarching topic.
Rather, the simulation asks whether CFA fit indices can discriminate between purposeful
keyword selection (reflecting a theoretically coherent construct) and arbitrary selection
(reflecting only the baseline thematic homogeneity of the corpus).
We examined settings with 4, 6, and 8 indicators, focusing on fit indices (CFI, TLI, RMSEA,
SRMR) and reliability measures (Cronbach’s alpha, omega). For each indicator count, we
sampled 1,000 sets of words from the corpus, estimated the CFA model, and inspected the

resulting distributions. We focus on whether fit indices exceed conventional thresholds at the
5th and 95th percentiles, to quantify how often apparently good fit can arise by chance.
The ideal situation would be one in which the values of the recognized thresholds for CFI and
TLI (measures where a high value is associated with good fit), that is, 0.95, coincide with the
empirical distribution (specifically the 95th percentile). In the case of SRMR and RMSEA, the
5th percentile should coincide with the thresholds known from the literature (0.08 and 0.05,
respectively).

The 4-indicator model (Table 3) results showed a mean CFI of 0.925, with 47.9% of models

exceeding the 0.95 threshold. TLI had a mean of 0.775, with 20.1% of models surpassing 0.95.
These high percentages suggest that CFI and TLI may not be reliable when using a limited
number of indicators. RMSEA was less affected by false positives, with only 16.1% of models
below the 0.05 threshold, while SRMR showed that 95% of models had values below the
acceptable 0.08 threshold, indicating it might not effectively assess fit in this scenario.
Reliability, as indicated by Cronbach's Alpha (0.572) and Omega (0.585), was low, reflecting
poor internal consistency - the results we would expect from random words.
For the 6-indicator model, the mean CFI dropped to 0.826, with only 2.2% of models exceeding
0.95, and TLI had a mean of 0.710, with 0.2% surpassing the threshold. RMSEA improved
slightly, with 0.3% of models below 0.05, while 78.7% of models had SRMR values under
0.08, suggesting SRMR remains overly lenient. Reliability improved moderately, with Alpha
and Omega averaging 0.671 and 0.674, respectively.
In the 8-indicator model, no models exceeded the CFI and TLI thresholds of 0.95, and none
had RMSEA below 0.05, indicating these fit indices become more stringent with more
indicators. However, 65.7% of models still had SRMR below 0.08. Reliability further
improved, with Alpha and Omega averaging 0.730 and 0.726, with below 2% of indices
exceeding the threshold of 0.8 for both measures.
It is worth noting that with eight randomly sampled indicators, the mean reliability already
approaches or exceeds the commonly used threshold of .70 (mean alpha = .730, mean omega
= .726). This means that a more lenient reliability threshold could be met by chance alone in a
thematically constrained corpus. This observation suggests using the stricter .80 threshold, as
it more effectively discriminates between theoretically motivated and arbitrary keyword
selections.
Overall, as the number of indicators increased from 4 to 8, the likelihood of achieving high fit

indices by chance decreased. With six or more indicators, CFI, TLI, and RMSEA thresholds
appear appropriate, making them reliable measures for assessing model fit. For models with
fewer indicators, we recommend focusing on RMSEA and TLI, as SRMR and CFI tend to
indicate a good fit even in randomly generated models. Reliability measures seem to provide
accurate indicators. Overall results show that fit indices are not heavily impacted by false
positives, at least for factors measured by at least six indicators. A smaller number of indicators
could also be evaluated properly by TLI, and RMSEA measures slightly higher thresholds than
those used in classical CFA applications. Full distributions of the simulation results are
presented in Appendix Figures A1 (4 indicators), A2 (6 indicators), and A3 (8 indicators).

Working with word embeddings and a full corpus provides opportunities that a typical survey
researcher lacks. With embeddings, we can index a measure using any theoretically motivated
combination of words (or phrases) in the vocabulary. Adding further indicators is therefore
limited mainly by theory and practical judgment. Below (Table 4), we add two terms, conflict
and military, to the initial four-word set and assess whether they improve measurement quality.
Results show that adding "conflict" and "military" as indicators brought both benefits and trade-

offs to the measurement model. On the positive side, factor loadings for the new indicators
were high (e.g., "conflict" at 0.795 in Model 2 and "military" at 0.780 in Model 3), with other
indicators showing moderate adjustments. Residual variances generally decreased, especially
for "alliance," indicating improved indicator reliability. Reliability indices (Cronbach's Alpha
and Omega) increased with each model, reaching 0.829 and 0.834 in Model 3, confirming
stronger internal consistency.

However, it is important to acknowledge a fundamental trade-off when expanding
measurement models. While adding theoretically relevant indicators increases reliability and
provides more comprehensive construct coverage, it does not guarantee improvement in model
fit. Although chi-square values rose with model complexity, fit indices (CFI, TLI, RMSEA,
SRMR) remained within acceptable ranges across all three models. Nevertheless, absolute fit
index values show slight decreases in CFI and TLI from Model 1 to Model 3, while RMSEA
and SRMR show slight increases. This pattern reflects the well-known tension in CFA between
model parsimony and construct breadth: simpler models with fewer indicators are closer to
saturation and thus have less opportunity to exhibit misfit, whereas more complex models face
stricter tests of the hypothesized structure. The choice of final model ultimately depends on
research objectives: if the priority is construct coverage and reliability, Model 3 with six
indicators may be preferred; if parsimony is paramount, Model 1 provides adequate fit with
fewer assumptions.

Model 1 started with a single-factor structure, grouping all indicators under one dimension.
This model assumed war anxiety as a unified construct; however, the fit statistics indicated a

Model 3 added a third factor specifically for social concerns, placing Social and Society into

distinct yet interrelated aspects of war anxiety.
In Model 4, we further refined the structure by adding residual covariance between Social and
Society, allowing for their shared variance beyond the main factors. The covariance between
Social and Society was introduced to account for additional shared variance between these
indicators, which standard latent factors may not fully capture. These terms share the same
root, with Social being an adjective and Society a noun. So, there is some additional linguistic
relation that is likely to go beyond the structure factor. This final model yielded the best fit
(TLI = 0.920, CFI=0.945, RMSEA = 0.088). It is worth noting that Model 4’s fit, while
acceptable by conventional CFA standards, raises the question of whether the same thresholds
should apply when CFA is used with word embeddings rather than survey data. In surveybased CFA, items are purposefully designed to tap a common construct, and high fit is
expected. In text-based CFA, keywords are natural language terms that serve multiple functions
and carry meaning beyond any single construct, so some residual misfit is inherent rather than
indicative of model misspecification. The Monte Carlo simulation presented earlier in this
paper provides empirical grounding for this question, demonstrating that conventional
thresholds remain informative for distinguishing theoretically coherent keyword sets from
random selections. Nonetheless, developing text-specific benchmarks for CFA fit indices
remains an important direction for future research.

Multigroup Analysis
One of the great advantages of human-generated textual data is its continuous and longitudinal
nature. Unlike survey data, which typically captures discrete points in time or requires a special
design for longitudinal studies, textual data can naturally reflect dynamics and changes over
time without additional design efforts.

In the longitudinal dimension of this study, CFA serves three purposes: it tests whether the
measurement model remains stable over time, enables latent mean estimation at each time
point, and allows comparison with independently collected survey trends. The last comparison
is presented as a methodological illustration rather than a formal test of convergent validity.

For the survey-based analysis (Self-Reported War Anxiety), data from the questionnaire
described earlier (see Tables 1 and 2) are used, with measurements taken at six time points
using three indicators: world war, nuclear conflict, and spread to Poland. The follow-up survey
was administered in week 11, where only questions related to these three indicators were asked.

For the Twitter-based analysis (Discourse of War Anxiety), multigroup (11 groups)
confirmatory factor analysis using the unidimensional Model 3 (see Table 4) was conducted,
where the indicators are alliance, nuclear, aggression, development, conflict, and military. This
analysis captures the evolving discourse around war anxiety as reflected in social media
conversations.

Measurement Invariance Testing
The methodology used in CFA offers significant advantages for trend analyses, particularly
through the testing of measurement invariance, an element often overlooked in textual data
analyses, such as those based on Twitter trends or other social media data. This is particularly

important in social media contexts, where the meaning of specific phrases and the relationships
between them may shift over time, and certain expressions may lose relevance (for instance,
the usage of the term "atomic" in times of peace might reflect something great and positive,
while in time of war, it drastically changes its connotations). Additionally, technical aspects of
data collection can impact the comparability of results, further underscoring the importance of
testing invariance.

In latent trait analysis, measurement invariance testing is critical for validating cross-group
comparisons of latent means. Measurement invariance ensures that a construct is measured
equivalently across groups, enabling valid comparisons of latent means. The typical approach
to assessing measurement invariance is to test increasingly restrictive multigroup models with
additional equality constraints across groups (see Pokropek et al., 2019, Meuleman et. al 2023,
for an overview).

In the basic testing of measurement invariance, the process begins with configural invariance,
the baseline model with no equality constraints, which verifies that the overall factor structure
is consistent across groups (i.e., measurement occasions). Metric invariance is tested next by
constraining factor loadings to be equal across groups. Metric invariance allows researchers to
compare relationships between indicators and latent constructs, ensuring that observed
differences reflect meaningful distinctions rather than measurement inconsistencies. The final
and most restrictive test, scalar invariance, adds constraints on both factor loadings and
intercepts, indicating that the construct's baseline is equivalent across groups. Achieving scalar
invariance is essential for comparing latent means because it ensures that each group shares a
common starting point, allowing direct comparison of average levels of the latent construct. It
is worth elaborating on why each level of invariance matters, particularly for readers less
familiar with multigroup CFA. Metric invariance (equal factor loadings) ensures that each

keyword indicator contributes to the latent construct with the same relative weight across time
periods. If metric invariance fails, it means that certain keywords become more or less
important for the construct over time, for instance, “nuclear” might dominate the war anxiety
factor in early weeks but become less central later. While such shifts are substantively
interesting, they prevent meaningful comparison of factor scores or structural relationships
across groups. Scalar invariance (equal intercepts) goes further: it ensures that observed
differences in keyword frequencies reflect genuine differences in the latent construct, rather
than shifts in baseline usage rates unrelated to the construct. Without scalar invariance, a
change in latent means could simply reflect that people use certain words more frequently over
time for reasons unrelated to war anxiety (e.g., media coverage patterns). Thus, both levels are
necessary for valid latent mean comparisons: metric invariance ensures the construct is
measured with the same structure, and scalar invariance ensures that differences in observed
scores translate into differences in the latent variable itself (Meredith, 1993; Vandenberg &
Lance, 2000).

Chen (2007) proposed widely used guidelines for judging measurement invariance in
multigroup CFA: invariance is typically supported when ΔCFI ≤ .01, ΔRMSEA ≤ .015, and
ΔSRMR ≤ .030 (configural→metric) or ≤ .015 (metric→scalar). However, because Chen’s
simulations focused mainly on few groups (2–4), these cutoffs can be overly conservative in
settings with many groups. Rutkowski and Svetina (2014), using 10–20 groups (close to our
11 time points), recommended relaxed thresholds for metric invariance (ΔCFI ≥ −.02,
ΔRMSEA ≤ .03), while retaining the traditional criteria for scalar invariance (ΔCFI ≥ −.01,
ΔRMSEA ≤ .01). They did not propose alternative SRMR cutoffs, so we keep Chen’s SRMR
thresholds (.030 for metric; .015 for scalar) and apply Rutkowski and Svetina’s criteria only to
CFI/RMSEA in our multigroup analysis.

For the survey-based model measuring Self-Reported War Anxiety, we do not conduct formal
measurement invariance testing due to the limited number of indicators. With only three
indicators, the configural model is just-identified (df = 0), resulting in perfect fit by definition
and making fit indices uninterpretable for comparison purposes (Bollen, 1989). However, this
limitation is not a major concern for several reasons. First, measurement properties of
standardized self-report scales are generally stable over short time periods (Widaman et al.,
2010; Pokropek et al., 2019), particularly it seems reasonable when the same instrument is
administered to similar populations within weeks rather than years. Second, survey items are
deliberately designed to maintain consistent meaning across administrations, unlike naturally
occurring text where semantic shifts are more likely. Third, and most importantly, we directly
fit the scalar invariance model, which demonstrates excellent fit to the data (χ² = 36.8, df = 30,
CFI = 0.999, RMSEA = 0.012), suggesting that the assumption of equivalent measurement
across time points is reasonable for this construct.

In contrast, measurement invariance testing is particularly valuable for the Twitter-based model
of Discourse of War Anxiety, where the meaning and usage of terms may evolve rapidly in
response to current events. This analysis uses word embeddings derived from text, where word
frequencies can vary significantly over time. When we compute cosine distances and derive
correlation matrices from these embeddings, we effectively standardize the variables. We
calculated the means and standard deviations of the word frequencies for each word each week,
transformed the correlation matrices into covariance matrices using the estimated standard
deviations, and then used these covariance matrices in our multi-group CFA.

of model deterioration, as it is less affected by the number of parameters and more directly

correlations. These results indicate that the Discourse of War Anxiety construct, as measured
through word embeddings, demonstrates measurement equivalence across the 11-week period,
allowing for valid comparisons of latent means over time.

Substantively, this finding carries an informative interpretation: it suggests that imposing
equality constraints on factor loadings and intercepts across the 11 weekly corpora does not
degrade the model’s fit to the data, meaning that the semantic relationships between keywords
and the latent construct are genuinely stable over time. The configural model, with its many
freely estimated parameters, may be capitalizing on week-to-week sampling variability in the
embedding space (analogous to overfitting); constraining these parameters to equality across
groups imposes a more parsimonious structure that the data actually support. In other words,
the “improvement” in fit under stricter invariance reflects the fact that week-to-week
differences in loadings and intercepts are predominantly noise rather than meaningful
measurement change. This is substantively reassuring: it means that the keyword-based
construct maintains stable measurement properties despite the rapidly evolving discourse
context of an armed conflict.

While the global fit index approach described above is the most commonly used method for
evaluating measurement invariance researchers have developed several complementary
techniques to examine measurement equivalence more deeply. These alternative approaches
can provide additional insights, particularly when working with complex data structures such
as word embeddings. One such approach involves examining standardized factor loadings over
time (Millsap, 2011; van de Schoot et al., 2012). This method allows researchers to visually
inspect how the relationship between indicators and the latent construct changes across
measurement occasions. While the global fit indices (ΔCFI, ΔRMSEA) assess overall model
deterioration, examining individual loadings can reveal which specific indicators drive any
observed non-invariance. In our case, since global fit indices already support metric and scalar
invariance, indicating that loading fluctuations across weeks are not statistically significant,
individual loading inspection serves a supplementary, descriptive role rather than providing

essential additional evidence. Nevertheless, visualizing loading patterns can help researchers
identify which specific indicators contribute most to any residual variability.

Discussion
This study demonstrates that integrating Confirmatory Factor Analysis (CFA) with word
embeddings offers a robust methodology for evaluating the coherence and reliability of
keyword-based text measures. Applied to war-related discourse on Polish Twitter, the method
produced interpretable factor structures, adequate model fit, and multidimensional structures
encompassing political/military, economic, and social dimensions. Moreover, our multigroup
analysis indicated that the measurement model remained consistent over time, allowing for
meaningful comparisons of latent means across different periods.
It should be noted that the approach presented here is complementary to, rather than a
replacement for, existing text analysis methodologies. Dictionary-based approaches like LIWC
(Boyd et al., 2022) remain valuable for general-purpose applications; inductive methods
(Kantner & Overbeck, 2020) are essential for discovering relevant vocabulary; and the broader
word embeddings literature (Rodriguez & Spirling, 2022; Kozlowski et al., 2019) provides
important foundations for semantic analysis. Importantly, the CFA framework proposed here
is agnostic about the source of keywords: it can evaluate keyword sets derived from any of
these traditions, as well as from ad hoc theoretical reasoning, corpus-specific exploration, or
interdisciplinary collaboration. The method’s value lies not in prescribing how keywords
should be selected, but in providing a formal evaluative framework that has been absent from
keyword-based text analysis regardless of how the keywords were obtained. The contribution
of the present paper lies in bringing confirmatory psychometric tools, specifically CFA, to
evaluate the coherence of researcher-selected keywords within specific corpora. This
represents a novel integration rather than a competing paradigm.
Limitations and Future Directions
While our study provides promising insights, several limitations must be acknowledged. First,

our analysis relies on data collected from a specific cultural and temporal context, Poland
during the early stages of Russia's invasion of Ukraine. This context-specific focus may limit
the generalizability of our findings to other settings or topics. Therefore, future research should
replicate this methodology across diverse cultural contexts and subject matters to assess its
broader applicability.
Second, the quality and representativeness of social media data pose potential concerns. Social
media platforms may not capture the full spectrum of public opinion due to varying levels of
access and engagement among different demographic groups. Furthermore, the preprocessing
steps and choice of word embedding model (e.g., CBOW) may influence the results. Future
studies could explore the impact of using different word embedding algorithms, such as Skipgram or BERT, and assess how these choices affect the measurement models.
Third, the keyword selection in the presented empirical example, while informed by analogy
with the survey instrument, was not subjected to the rigorous theoretical development expected
in fully substantive applications. This is intentional, the paper's contribution is methodological,
demonstrating that empirical evaluation of keyword coherence is possible, not providing a
definitive operationalization of war anxiety. Future substantive applications should invest
greater effort in theoretically grounding keyword selection, potentially drawing on domain
expertise, existing conceptual frameworks, or qualitative analysis of relevant discourse. The
CFA framework proposed here can evaluate whether a given keyword set forms a coherent
construct, but thoughtful, theory-driven keyword selection remains essential.
Fourth, while training word embeddings is nowadays computationally feasible, the costs are
not negligible and depend on several factors: the embedding model type (e.g., CBOW is faster
than Skip-gram; transformer-based models like BERT require substantially more resources),
the size of the corpus and vocabulary, and the available computational resources (CPU vs.

GPU). In our application, training CBOW embeddings on weekly Twitter corpora of 400,000–
760,000 posts was completed in minutes on standard hardware. However, researchers working
with larger corpora, higher-dimensional embeddings, or more complex architectures should
budget computational resources accordingly. Pre-trained embeddings (e.g., from fastText or
language-specific models; Bojanowski, Grave, Joulin, & Mikolov, 2017) offer an alternative
that avoids training costs entirely, though at the expense of corpus-specific contextual
information.
Fifth, the comparison between survey-based and Twitter-based measures presented in this
paper should not be interpreted as a convergent validity test. Survey data and social media data
differ fundamentally as measurement instruments: surveys capture responses from sampled
populations under controlled conditions, whereas social media data reflect the public
expressions of self-selected, non-representative users whose motivations for posting may be
unrelated to the constructs of interest (Murphy et al., 2014). As Baden et al. (2024) discuss in
detail, these data sources differ not only in who is represented but also in what is being
measured, self-reported attitudes versus publicly performed discourse, making direct
equivalence an unreasonable expectation. The parallel analysis presented here is therefore
intended as a methodological demonstration, it shows that CFA applied to word embeddings
can produce temporally coherent latent means that track independently collected data,
suggesting that the method captures meaningful signal rather than noise.
Finally, our method primarily focused on reflective measurement models. Expanding this
approach to formative models or incorporating more complex structural equation modelling
could provide a more comprehensive understanding of the relationships between textual
indicators and latent constructs. Future research should consider these extensions to enhance
the robustness and applicability of the methodology.

Practical Implications
Our findings suggest that integrating CFA with word embeddings can enhance the rigor of
textual data analysis, providing a robust framework for constructing and validating composite
indicators from large text corpora. This methodology enables researchers to systematically
assess the reliability and validity of keyword-based measures, which ensures the integrity of
findings derived from big data sources.
In monitoring public sentiment, such as war anxiety, our approach allows for real-time analysis
of social media data, offering timely insights that traditional survey methods may not capture
due to longer data collection periods. Policymakers and mental health professionals could
leverage this method to track shifts in public concern, tailor interventions, and communicate
effectively with the public during rapidly evolving situations.
More broadly, our results support the conclusion that applying rigorous statistical models to
word embeddings can mitigate some methodological challenges associated with textual data
analysis. By promoting deliberate and systematic measurement practices, researchers can more
accurately interpret the information available in digital text sources, ultimately contributing to
more informed decision-making across various domains.
