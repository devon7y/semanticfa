Abstract

23

24

As large language models (LLMs) increasingly inform psychometric practice through item

25

generation and semantic analysis, embedding-based approaches offer a pre-empirical pathway

26

for assessing dimensional structure before human response data become available. However,

27

the methodological choices used to recover dimensions from embedding spaces remain

28

underexamined. The present study compared the performance of principal component

29

analysis (PCA), which currently dominates generative psychometric applications, with

30

network-based exploratory graph analysis (EGA) for estimating dimensional structure from

31

item embeddings. Using Monte Carlo simulations across six personality facets nested within

32

two domains, we generated 180 items per condition via LLM prompting and represented

33

them as semantic embeddings. Dimensional recovery was evaluated across four analytic

34

pipelines: Kaiser’s rule with PCA extraction and varimax rotation, parallel analysis with

35

PCA extraction and varimax rotation, EGA, and EGA combined with network-integrated

36

item filtering. Results showed that PCA-based methods severely overestimated

37

dimensionality and yielded poorer recovery of the generating structure. EGA demonstrated

38

markedly improved performance relative to PCA-based approaches, whereas EGA combined

39

with network filtering procedures achieved near-perfect dimensional accuracy and structural

40

recovery. These findings indicate that network psychometric workflows provide more accurate

41

structural representations than component-based approaches when analyzing embedding

42

spaces, with direct implications for generative psychometric workflows in which item pools

43

are evaluated prior to data collection. More broadly, the findings demonstrate that analytic

44

choices made during pre-empirical assessment have substantive consequences for item

45

selection, construct validity, taxonomic clarity, and downstream theoretical interpretation.

Introduction

52

Generative artificial intelligence (AI) is increasingly reshaping core activities in

53

psychometrics, reflecting a broader shift toward treating language as a scalable behavioral

54

trace for psychological science and cognitive modelling (Brickman & Oltmanns, 2025;

55

Debelak & Stachl, 2025; Feuerriegel & Van Bavel, 2025; Jackson & Lindquist, 2022; Kjell,

56

Kjell, & Schwartz, 2024; Van Quaquebeke & Banks, 2025). Large language models (LLMs)

57

are now integrated into item-generation workflows, where they are used to generate, expand,

58

and refine item pools, as well as to support translation and cross-cultural adaptation (Fyffe

59

& Kaplan, 2024; Grobelny & Strozyk, 2025; Hernandez & Nie, 2023; Keane & McNaughton,

60

2025; Kowal & Kantrowitz, 2025; Lee, Son, & Jia, 2025; Perrin et al., 2025; Ruiz-Mendoza &

61

Pedroza-Zúñiga, 2025; Russell-Lasalandra, Christensen, & Golino, 2024; Salah et al., 2025).

62

These applications typically operate under structured human oversight or multi-agent

63

quality-control pipelines (e.g., LLMs evaluating and critiquing each other’s outputs) to

64

reduce redundancy while anchoring item content in theory and lived experience. In parallel,

65

transformer-based models trained on naturally occurring language are increasingly used to

66

predict personality traits and related constructs from open-ended text. In this role, such

67

models function as automated raters and, when fine-tuned, as scoring engines for assessment

68

and psychological text-classification tasks (Bunt & Gillespie, 2025; Hommel, 2023; Kjell et

69

al., 2024; Lee et al., 2025; Maharjan, Jin, Zhu, & Kenne, 2025). As these embedding-based

70

approaches move beyond proof-of-concept demonstrations toward routine application,

71

however, fundamental questions remain unresolved, particularly regarding how to reliably

72

recover dimensional structure from language-based representations.

73

74

Text embeddings sit at the center of many of these developments, providing a general
mechanism for transforming language into quantitative data suitable for psychological

ESTIMATING DIMENSIONAL STRUCTURE IN GENERATIVE PSYCHOMETRICS 5

75

analysis (Brickman & Oltmanns, 2025; Feuerriegel & Van Bavel, 2025; Jackson & Lindquist,

76

2022). An embedding model maps a unit of text, such as an item stem, construct label, or

77

self-description, into a dense vector in a high-dimensional space. Through this mapping,

78

scale items and survey questions can be treated as numeric representations of their semantic

79

content (Fang & Oberski, 2022; Russell-Lasalandra et al., 2024; Stanton & Sang, 2024).

80

These vectors are learned from large text corpora such that semantically similar texts tend

81

to occupy nearby locations, whereas dissimilar texts lie farther apart, yielding a geometric

82

space that encodes graded semantic relations among words, items, and other psychologically

83

relevant concepts (Fang & Oberski, 2022; Golino, 2025; Hussain, Binz, Mata, & Wulff, 2024).

84

Once texts have been embedded, researchers can compute similarity metrics between items,

85

derive distance-based neighborhoods and clusters, or use embeddings as inputs to supervised

86

models that classify items into constructs (Fang & Oberski, 2022; Fyffe & Kaplan, 2024). In

87

this sense, language is rendered into numerical form that can be analyzed using many of the

88

same tools applied to Likert-type response data, including similarity matrices, matrix

89

factorizations, clustering algorithms, and predictive models of scale characteristics (Hommel

90

& Arslan, 2025; Hussain et al., 2024; Stanton & Sang, 2024).

91

Capitalizing on evidence that embedding-based distances and configurations can mirror

92

patterns observed in human response data (Fang & Oberski, 2022; Hommel & Arslan, 2025;

93

Hussain et al., 2024; Russell-Lasalandra et al., 2024; Stanton & Sang, 2024), researchers have

94

applied embedding-based approaches to scale abbreviation without collecting response data,

95

to mapping nomological neighborhoods across hundreds of thousands of indicators, and to

96

pre-data-collection diagnostics of content and construct validity (Fyffe & Kaplan, 2024;

97

Kilmen & Bulut, 2025a; Larsen & Edmondson, 2025; Ravenda & Raballo, 2025; Wulff &

98

Mata, 2025a, 2025b).

99

Complementing these diagnostics, LLMs can also be prompted to emulate specific

100

profiles and generate synthetic respondents whose answers approximate human response

101

patterns, producing pseudo-response data that can be analyzed using conventional

ESTIMATING DIMENSIONAL STRUCTURE IN GENERATIVE PSYCHOMETRICS 6

102

psychometric models (Cipriani & Grassini, 2025; Niszczota & Misiak, 2025; Rosenfelder &

103

Gilead, 2025). Together, these tools have the potential to make scale development,

104

validation, and structural analysis more efficient and less dependent on large human samples

105

during early stages of instrument development. Realizing this potential, however, depends

106

critically on the methods used to recover dimensional structure from embedding spaces, a

107

choice that has received limited scrutiny despite its downstream implications for construct

108

validity and measurement quality.

109

Current approaches to this structural analysis problem rely heavily on methods that

110

may be poorly suited to the task. In embedding-based psychometric applications,

111

dimensional structure assessment is dominated by principal component analysis (PCA)

112

applied to cosine- or correlation-based similarity matrices derived from item, adjective, or

113

construct embeddings (Bhandari & Pardos, 2024; Cutler & Condon, 2023; Huang & Tong,

114

2025; Jung & Seo, 2025; Linden, Cutler, Van der Linden, & Dunkel, 2025; Milano &

115

Marocco, 2025; Teitelbaum & Simchon, 2025). The dominance of PCA in these workflows

116

reflects a long historical tradition in fields such as personality and psychopathology, where

117

PCA has served as the default tool for deriving multilevel structure from large item pools

118

(Ashton & Goldberg, 2004; Forbes & Clark, 2025; Goldberg, 2013; Saucier & Iurino, 2020).

119

Proponents of PCA argue that it offers advantages due to its mathematical simplicity,

120

reduced susceptibility to problematic solutions, and absence of score indeterminacy (Sellbom

121

& Goretzko, 2023a). However, a long-standing methodological literature raises concerns

122

about relying on PCA as a primary basis for determining the dimensional structure of

123

psychological variables, particularly when paired with eigenvalue-greater-than-one or

124

scree-plot heuristics (Goretzko & Bühner, 2021; Kaçak & Kılıç, 2025; Sellbom & Goretzko,

125

2023b; Widaman, 1993; Widaman, 2018).

126

Conceptually, PCA is a descriptive decomposition of total variance rather than a

127

representation of common variance. As a result, components maximize variance in observed

128

variables without separating common, unique, and error variance, providing no guarantee

ESTIMATING DIMENSIONAL STRUCTURE IN GENERATIVE PSYCHOMETRICS 7

129

that the resulting dimensions correspond to psychological attributes (Goretzko & Bühner,

130

2021; Widaman, 2018). Simulation studies indicate that, under conditions typical of

131

psychological data, including low to moderate communalities, few indicators per dimension,

132

and ordinal-level measurement, PCA tends to overestimate factor loadings and can yield

133

biased recovery of the generating structure (Kaçak & Kılıç, 2025; Widaman, 1993).

134

Moreover, the PCA framework is poorly suited to address issues that are particularly

135

relevant for generative AI-based psychometric pipelines, such as item redundancy, structural

136

instability (Russell-Lasalandra et al., 2024), and hierarchical assessment (Samo &

137

Christensen, 2025). Taken together, these conceptual and empirical critiques suggest that

138

PCA-based dimensional solutions, while convenient and historically entrenched, provide an

139

uncertain foundation for inferences about dimensional structure. This concern becomes

140

especially salient when PCA is used to characterize the geometry of embedding spaces and

141

guide downstream modeling choices in generative AI-based psychometrics.

Generative AI Psychometrics with Network Models
Network modeling conceptualizes psychological variables as nodes in a network, with

144

conditional associations between variables represented as edges, typically estimated using

145

Gaussian graphical models estimated via regularized partial correlations, or estimated via

146

triangulated maximally filtered graphs (Christensen, Garrido, Guerra-Peña, & Golino, 2024;

147

Golino, Shi, et al., 2020). Within this framework, Exploratory Graph Analysis (EGA)

148

integrates sparse network estimation with community detection algorithms, such as Walktrap

149

or Louvain, to identify clusters of densely connected nodes corresponding to latent or

150

emergent dimensions (Christensen, Golino, Abad, & Garrido, 2024; Cosemans, Rosseel, &

151

Gelper, 2022; Golino, Thiyagarajan, et al., 2020; Jimenez & Garrido, 2025; Samo &

152

Christensen, 2025). Simulation studies demonstrate that EGA recovers the number of

153

dimensions with high accuracy across a wide range of factor structures and data types, often

154

matching or outperforming traditional factor-retention methods for both continuous and

ESTIMATING DIMENSIONAL STRUCTURE IN GENERATIVE PSYCHOMETRICS 8

155

156

categorical indicators (Cosemans et al., 2022; Golino, Thiyagarajan, et al., 2020).

Building on EGA’s core framework, several specialized network-based tools have been

157

developed to address challenges that are amplified in large item pools. Bootstrap EGA

158

(bootEGA) extends the base approach by repeatedly re-estimating networks across bootstrap

159

samples, yielding indices of structural consistency and item stability (Christensen & Golino,

160

2021). To address item redundancy, Unique Variable Analysis (UVA) integrates network

161

estimation with weighted topological overlap to identify locally dependent items prior to

162

dimensionality assessment, demonstrating strong performance across data types and

163

favorable comparisons to alternative diagnostics (Christensen & Golino, 2023). For

164

interpretation, network loadings provide factor-loading-like coefficients that yield dimension

165

scores whose correlation patterns reflects the generating correlations among dimensions,

166

without requiring rotational decisions (Christensen & Garrido, 2025). When response styles

167

or wording effects are present, random intercept EGA partials out these sources of variance

168

prior to network estimation, improving dimensionality recovery (Garcia-Pardina, Abad,

169

Christensen, Golino, & Garrido, 2024). Finally, hierarchical EGA enables accurate recovery

170

of both general and group factors from item-level data (Jiménez et al., 2023).

171

These developments converge in Taxonomic Graph Analysis (TGA), a comprehensive

172

framework for recovering hierarchical construct structures from large item pools (Samo &

173

Christensen, 2025). TGA integrates the tools described above into a bottom-up workflow:

174

UVA identifies redundant items, random intercept EGA mitigates wording effects, EGA with

175

consensus clustering estimates dimensionality across successive hierarchical levels, bootEGA

176

evaluates structural stability, and network loadings quantify indicator strength and derive

177

scores for higher-order structure estimation. This integrated approach yields multilevel

178

taxonomies that coherently link item-level facets, traits, and meta-traits within a single

179

hierarchy (Christensen & Golino, 2021; Christensen & Garrido, 2025; Christensen & Golino,

180

2023; Samo & Christensen, 2025).

181

The Automatic Item Generation with Network-Integrated Evaluation (AI-GENIE)

182

framework (Russell-Lasalandra et al., 2024) extends this taxonomic logic to generative AI

183

psychometrics by coupling LLM-based item generation with embedding models and applying

184

the same UVA-EGA-bootEGA pipeline to embedding-based similarity structures. This

185

approach identifies nonredundant, structurally stable items from large LLM-generated pools

186

and produces concise scales whose structural properties align with established trait measures,

187

illustrating how network psychometrics can automate early-stage scale development in

188

generative AI contexts (Russell-Lasalandra et al., 2024). To date, however, no study has

189

systematically compared these network-based approaches with the predominant PCA

190

pipeline for recovering dimensional structure from embedding-derived similarity matrices.

191

The Present Study

192

The present study addresses this methodological gap by directly comparing PCA and

193

network approaches to dimensional structure estimation in embedding-based psychometrics.

194

We conducted a Monte Carlo simulation in which LLMs generated item pools for multiple

195

psychological constructs. Text embeddings were then derived from these AI-generated items

196

to construct cosine similarity matrices analogous to those used in current embedding-based

197

structural analysis pipelines (Russell-Lasalandra et al., 2024). Dimensional structure was

198

estimated from these embedding-based similarity matrices using PCA with traditional

199

retention criteria, including the eigenvalue-greater-than-one criterion (K1) or Kaiser’s rule

200

(Kaiser, 1960) and parallel analysis [PA; Horn (1965)], as well as the EGA framework.

201

The accuracy of each method was evaluated based on its ability to recover the

202

generating dimensional structure. Because AI-GENIE’s redundancy and stability filtering

203

procedures are inherently network-based, post–item-filtering analyses were conducted

204

exclusively within the EGA framework. Specifically, we examined how filtering via UVA and

205

bootEGA affected dimensional structure recovery within the network approach. Given the

206

conceptual and empirical limitations of PCA documented in the psychometric literature

ESTIMATING DIMENSIONAL STRUCTURE IN GENERATIVE PSYCHOMETRICS 10

207

(Goretzko & Bühner, 2021; Kaçak & Kılıç, 2025; Sellbom & Goretzko, 2023b; Widaman,

208

1993; Widaman, 2018), we hypothesized that EGA would demonstrate superior accuracy in

209

recovering dimensional structure from embedding spaces and would yield more informative

210

item-level diagnostics following network-based filtering. These comparisons have direct

211

implications for the methodological foundations of generative AI-based scale development,

212

where early structural inferences guide downstream validation and application contexts.

Method

213

214

Simulation Design

215

The accuracy of the dimensional structure estimates was evaluated using Monte Carlo

216

simulation. The GPT-4o model (OpenAI, 2023a) under the default nucleus sampling (p = 1)

217

and temperature (1.0) was used to generate item pools measuring six personality facets

218

taken from two domains (Irwing & Booth, 2024): (1) the facets of achievement striving,

219

perseverance, and self-efficacy for the domain of conscientiousness, and (2) the facets of

220

abstract thinking, aesthetics, and introspection for the domain of openness to experience.

221

A target pool of 90 items per domain was set (180 total), which the LLM distributed

222

approximately equally across the specified facets. We chose to generate a large number of

223

items per facet (30) to mimic expected procedures in real-life scenarios of AI-aided test

224

development where 3, 4 or more times the intended final number of items are generated

225

initially (Koweszko & Silczuk, 2025; Lee & Yao, 2023). A total of 500 replications were

226

simulated.

227

Item Generation and Prompt Engineering

228

Effective use of LLMs for psychological assessment requires systematic prompt

229

engineering—the deliberate design and optimization of input prompts to guide model

230

responses toward accurate, relevant, and coherent output (Chen, Zhang, Zheng, & Luo,

ESTIMATING DIMENSIONAL STRUCTURE IN GENERATIVE PSYCHOMETRICS 11

231

2025). The quality of prompts plays a significant role in determining the consistency and

232

viability of generated content (Jin, Cheng, Shen, Chen, & Ren, 2022; White, Hays, Fu,

233

Spencer-Smith, & Schmidt, 2023), making careful prompt construction essential for

234

psychometric applications. This study employed a comprehensive set of prompt engineering

235

techniques following the AI-GENIE approach (Russell-Lasalandra et al., 2024): role

236

prompting, clear and precise prompting, and few-shot prompting. Such a multi-faceted

237

approach is necessary because the vast universe of potential LLM responses can lead to

238

highly variable outputs; detailed, unambiguous prompts help constrain the model toward

239

generating content aligned with specific research goals (Ekin, Yilmaz, Erol, & Karagöz, 2023;

240

Giray, 2023; see: OpenAI, 2023b).

241

The pipeline began with role or persona prompting, which assigns a specific expert

242

identity to guide the model’s responses. Research demonstrates that priming a model with a

243

knowledgeable persona improves performance by producing more coherent, domain-relevant

244

responses and reducing generalization errors (Zheng et al., 2023). When focused on a

245

particular professional role, the model generates outputs that are more logically consistent

246

and tailored to the specific context. Accordingly, the model was informed: “You are an

247

expert psychometrician and test developer specializing in personality assessment.”

248

Second, clear and precise prompting was used to specify the nature of the task,

249

including the target domains, facets, and operational definitions for the items to be

250

generated (Irwing & Booth, 2024). Providing relevant context and detailed constraints helps

251

the model understand the prompt’s purpose and produce appropriately targeted content.

252

The prompt stated: “Your task is to create high-quality, psychometrically robust items for a

253

personality inventory measuring the following Big Five traits: Conscientiousness and

254

Openness to Experience. Conscientiousness reflects self-efficacy (Propensity to hold the

255

subjective perception that one is capable of performing), achievement-striving (Propensity to

256

be ambitious and goal-oriented), and perseverance (Propensity to continue with and finish a

257

task despite obstacles). Openness reflects introspection (Propensity to examine one’s thoughts,

ESTIMATING DIMENSIONAL STRUCTURE IN GENERATIVE PSYCHOMETRICS 12

258

feelings, motives, and behaviour), aesthetics (Enjoyment of artistic and aesthetic activities),

259

abstract-thinking (Propensity to explore and discuss abstract ideas).”

260

Third, detailed formatting guidelines were provided to ensure predictable, extractable

261

output. LLMs attend closely to emphasized instructions using atypical casing, and repeating

262

key directives improves adherence (Amatriain, 2024; Google Developers, 2025). The

263

formatting instructions were: “Generate psychometrically reliable and valid personality

264

assessment items for each trait’s example item characteristics. Do NOT mix characteristics

265

in single items. EACH item should represent ONE and ONLY ONE characteristic in ONE

266

trait. EACH item should be ONE sentence. You should format your output using: [trait]:

267

[item content]. Do NOT number or add ANY other text to your response. ONLY output the

268

traits EXACTLY as provided. ONLY output the trait and item content – NOTHING else.”

269

Finally, few-shot prompting was employed by providing five to six example items for

270

each facet. This technique encourages quality and consistency by allowing the model to

271

discern patterns from exemplars, improving performance and enabling nuanced

272

generalization (Brown et al., 2020). Including multiple trait-relevant examples also helps

273

ensure that generated items capture important aspects of the target construct rather than

274

being unidimensional. The example items were drawn from the Facet-level Multidimensional

275

Assessment of Personality (Facet MAP) version I (Irwing & Booth, 2024), with all inventory

276

items for the selected facets included in the prompt.

Data Structure Assessment

278

The dimensional structure of the generated items was assessed using cosine similarity

279

matrices derived from item embeddings (Kilmen & Bulut, 2025b). Item embeddings were

280

obtained using OpenAI’s text-embedding-3-small model (OpenAI, 2024). Three analytic

281

procedures were employed to evaluate the dimensional structure of the resulting item pools:

282

K1-PCA-Varimax: Commonly referred to as the “Little Jiffy” procedure (Kaiser,

ESTIMATING DIMENSIONAL STRUCTURE IN GENERATIVE PSYCHOMETRICS 13

283

1970), this approach combines the K1 dimensionality rule with PCA extraction and varimax

284

orthogonal rotation. For over five decades, this procedure has been amongst the most widely

285

used methods for dimensional structure assessment in the behavioral sciences (Fabrigar,

286

Wegener, MacCallum, & Strahan, 1999; Howard & Henderson, 2023; Howard & O’Sullivan,

287

2025; Izquierdo, Olea, & Abad, 2014; Kaiser, 1970; Preacher & MacCallum, 2003).

288

PA-PCA-Varimax: this procedure represents a modification of the Little Jiffy

289

approach in which the K1 rule is replaced with parallel analysis, one the best-performing and

290

most extensively validated dimensionality estimation methods (Garrido & Ponsoda, 2013;

291

Golino, Shi, et al., 2020; Goretzko, 2025; Xia, 2021). This modification is critical, as the K1

292

rule is known to perform poorly at the sample level, particularly in conditions with a large

293

number of variables per dimension (Auerswald & Moshagen, 2019; Golino, Thiyagarajan, et

294

al., 2020; Martínez Ramos & Rondón Sepúlveda, 2025). PA was computed using the

295

component eigenvalues, the mean aggregation criterion, and 100 simulated data matrices per

296

analysis.

297

EGA: the EGA approach was included as a modern, state-of-the-art alternative for

298

dimensional structure assessment on cosine similarity matrices of item embeddings

299

(Russell-Lasalandra et al., 2024). EGA was estimated using the graphical least absolute

300

shrinkage and selection operator with extended Bayesian information criterion for model

301

selection (EBICglasso) (Chen & Chen, 2008; Epskamp & Fried, 2018; Friedman, Hastie, &

302

Tibshirani, 2008), with variable clustering determined via the Walktrap algorithm (Brusco &

303

Watts, 2023; Pons & Latapy, 2006).

304

Item Filtering

305

AI-based psychometric pipelines typically begin with the generation of a large number

306

of initial items per dimension that vary in psychometric quality and may include overlapping

307

content. Consequently, identifying and removing redundant or structurally unstable items is

ESTIMATING DIMENSIONAL STRUCTURE IN GENERATIVE PSYCHOMETRICS 14

308

essential for obtaining robust dimensional structure estimates. In the present study, item

309

filtering was conducted using the Network-Integrated Evaluation approach included in

310

AI-GENIE (Russell-Lasalandra et al., 2024). The filtering procedure began with UVA

311

(Christensen & Golino, 2023), which was used to automatically detect and remove redundant

312

items from the initial item pool. EGA was then applied to estimate the dimensional

313

structure of the retained items. In the final step, bootEGA (Christensen & Golino, 2021) was

314

used to evaluate item stability within each dimension, resulting in the removal of items that

315

did not meaningfully contribute to dimensional stability. A cutoff value of 0.20 was used for

316

UVA to identify item redundancy (Christensen & Golino, 2023; Samo & Christensen, 2025),

317

and a minimum stability threshold of 0.75 was required for item retention in bootEGA

318

(Russell-Lasalandra et al., 2024; Samo & Christensen, 2025). The bootEGA procedure

319

employed the same analytical methods as EGA and was iteratively repeated until all retained

320

items demonstrated adequate stability. Each bootEGA run was based on 500 parametric

321

bootstrap samples generated from the matrix of cosine similarities (Christensen & Golino,

322

2021; Samo & Christensen, 2025). Both UVA and bootEGA were computed using a sparse

323

embedding matrix rather than the full embedding matrix (Russell-Lasalandra et al., 2024).

Discussion

387

The present study compared PCA and network-based approaches for estimating

388

dimensional structure from LLM item embeddings, addressing a critical gap in the rapidly

389

evolving field of generative AI psychometrics. Our primary aim was to evaluate whether the

390

predominant reliance on PCA in embedding-based applications (Bhandari & Pardos, 2024;

391

Cutler & Condon, 2023; Huang & Tong, 2025; Jung & Seo, 2025; Linden et al., 2025; Milano

392

& Marocco, 2025; Teitelbaum & Simchon, 2025) is justified, or whether network methods

393

such as Exploratory Graph Analysis and the related AI-GENIE pipeline (Russell-Lasalandra

394

et al., 2024) offer meaningful advantages for this specific psychometric task. The results

395

revealed substantial and consequential differences in favor of the network approach across

396

multiple criteria, including dimensionality estimation accuracy and coherence of item

397

clustering. These differences have important implications for the validity of dimensional

398

structures recovered from embedding spaces and, by extension, for downstream applications

399

that depend on accurate early-stage structural characterization of psychological constructs.

400

Main Findings

401

Of the two PCA-based pipelines, the popular “Little Jiffy” approach (Kaiser, 1970),

402

which combines Kaiser’s eigenvalue-greater-than-one rule (K1) with PCA extraction and

403

varimax rotation (K1-PCA-Varimax), displayed the poorest performance, with severe

404

overestimation of dimensionality and the weakest recovery of the generating structure. These

405

results are consistent with a long line of research showing that the K1 rule performs poorly

406

in finite samples, exhibiting a pronounced tendency toward overextraction (Auerswald &

407

Moshagen, 2019; Golino, Thiyagarajan, et al., 2020; Martínez Ramos & Rondón Sepúlveda,

408

2025), and that PCA is a data reduction method that is not recommended for identifying

409

dimensions arising from common variance among variables (Widaman, 1993). Proponents of

410

PCA have argued that it offers procedural advantages, including mathematical simplicity,

ESTIMATING DIMENSIONAL STRUCTURE IN GENERATIVE PSYCHOMETRICS 20

411

reduced susceptibility to improper solutions, and absence of score indeterminacy (Sellbom &

412

Goretzko, 2023a). Although these arguments may appear compelling given the method’s

413

widespread adoption and default status in statistical software, the present findings,

414

consistent with decades of methodological research, demonstrate that such conveniences

415

come at the cost of substantial systematic distortions in dimensional structure estimation.

416

The findings also showed that substituting the K1 rule with parallel analysis (PA) in

417

the PA-PCA-Varimax pipeline yielded meaningful improvements in both dimensionality

418

estimation and structural recovery, consistent with PA’s well-established superiority over

419

Kaiser’s rule in traditional psychometric contexts (Garrido & Ponsoda, 2013; Goretzko,

420

2025). Despite this improvement, however, PA-PCA-Varimax continued to markedly

421

overestimate the number of dimensions and yielded less accurate structural recovery than

422

EGA. This persistent overextraction, even with one of the most extensively validated and

423

recommended retention methods in psychometrics, underscores fundamental limitations in

424

applying traditional dimensionality workflows to LLM-generated item pools and

425

embedding-based representations.

426

Eigenvalue-based dimensionality methods such as PA are more susceptible than

427

network approaches to nuisance variance arising from minor dimensions (Jimenez & Garrido,

428

2025). In generative psychometrics, such nuisance variance can stem from multiple sources,

429

including item redundancy and the emergence of narrow subdimensions driven by the large

430

number of items generated by LLMs. In addition, large LLM-generated item pools are more

431

likely to contain solitary items, defined as items that do not load saliently on any dimension.

432

These items can inflate the eigenvalues of noise dimensions, thereby increasing the likelihood

433

that they are retained by methods such as PA (Turner, 1998). Under these conditions, PA

434

may yield dimensionality estimates that conflate major and minor dimensions, resulting in

435

systemic overextraction (Garcia-Pardina et al., 2024).

436

In contrast, EGA demonstrated substantially superior performance in recovering

ESTIMATING DIMENSIONAL STRUCTURE IN GENERATIVE PSYCHOMETRICS 21

437

dimensional structure from unfiltered embedding matrices. The network approach correctly

438

identified the generating number of dimensions in nearly half of all replicates, whereas the

439

PCA-based approaches never provided correct dimensionality estimates, and did so with only

440

a slight upward bias and high structural recovery accuracy. These results extend prior

441

simulation evidence supporting EGA’s robust performance in traditional response-based data

442

(Cosemans et al., 2022; Golino, Thiyagarajan, et al., 2020; Jimenez & Garrido, 2025) to the

443

novel context of embedding-derived similarity matrices, demonstrating that the method’s

444

advantages persist when applied to semantic representations produced by contemporary

445

LLMs. Unlike eigenvalue-based methods, EGA derives dimensional structure from sparse,

446

regularized partial correlation networks and community detection algorithms that identify

447

clusters of variables remaining conditionally dependent after accounting for all other variables

448

in the system. This property makes EGA less susceptible to nuisance variance arising from

449

minor dimensions, which are less likely to disrupt the substantive clustering of the variables

450

in network representations (Garcia-Pardina et al., 2024; Jimenez & Garrido, 2025).

451

The incorporation of network-based item filtering yielded notable improvements in

452

EGA performance. Following redundancy detection with UVA and stability filtering with

453

bootEGA, which together removed one-third to one-half of the generated items, EGA’s

454

performance approached near-ceiling levels across all evaluation criteria. Dimensionality

455

estimation became highly accurate with bias effectively eliminated, correct structure

456

identification occurred in the vast majority of replicates, and structural recovery reached

457

near-perfect levels. These improvements illustrate how network psychometric methods

458

address core challenges in generative AI psychometrics that extend beyond the scope of

459

traditional dimensional structure estimation procedures.

460

The necessity of this filtering reflects fundamental differences between LLM-generated

461

item pools and traditionally developed measures. When human experts craft scale items,

462

they explicitly avoid redundant phrasing, prioritize conceptual distinctiveness within

463

dimensions, and iteratively refine items through qualitative review processes before data

ESTIMATING DIMENSIONAL STRUCTURE IN GENERATIVE PSYCHOMETRICS 22

464

collection (Gehlbach & Brinkworth, 2011). LLMs, by contrast, generate items through

465

probabilistic sampling from learned language distributions, producing item pools that

466

inherently contain paraphrastic variants, semantically overlapping expressions, and variable

467

degrees of construct specificity (Fyffe & Kaplan, 2024; Lee et al., 2025; Russell-Lasalandra et

468

al., 2024). Although this generative process is efficient and yields large item pools rapidly, it

469

also introduces redundancy and potential structural instability that can confound

470

dimensionality estimation when left unaddressed. The AI-GENIE network-based filtering

471

pipeline systematically identifies and removes such problematic content through explicit

472

detection of redundancy and instability, producing refined item pools whose

473

embedding-based similarity structures more clearly represent the intended psychological

474

constructs (Russell-Lasalandra et al., 2024).

475

Taken together, these findings provide clear evidence that current default practices in

476

embedding-based psychometrics, specifically the application of PCA with traditional

477

retention criteria to similarity matrices, yield systematically biased dimensional structure

478

estimates and warrant reconsideration. The substantial overextraction observed even with

479

parallel analysis suggests that the problem reflects fundamental incompatibilities between

480

eigenvalue-based PCA procedures and the properties of similarity matrices derived from

481

large LLM-generated item pools. By contrast, the EGA and AI-GENIE approaches

482

demonstrated robust performance that improved further when integrated with specialized

483

network methods for addressing item redundancy and structural instability.

484

Limitations

485

There are some limitations in this study that should be noted. First, the study relied

486

on a single LLM (GPT-4o) for item generation and a single embedding model (OpenAI’s

487

text-embedding-3-small) to derive semantic representations. Different LLMs or embedding

488

models may produce items or semantic spaces with differing characteristics, which could

489

affect absolute levels of structural recovery. Nonetheless, the observed performance patterns

ESTIMATING DIMENSIONAL STRUCTURE IN GENERATIVE PSYCHOMETRICS 23

490

closely aligned with well-established limitations of PCA-based methods documented across

491

diverse psychometric contexts (Goretzko & Bühner, 2021; Samo & Christensen, 2025;

492

Widaman, 1993). This convergence suggests that the relative superiority of network

493

approaches is likely to persist across different combinations of generative and embedding

494

models, even if absolute accuracy levels vary.

495

Second, the simulation held constant the number of dimensions, consisting of six facets

496

nested within two domains, the target number of items per dimension (30), and the content

497

area (personality). Accordingly, the findings are tied to these constraints, and caution is

498

warranted when generalizing to structures with different characteristics or content domains.

499

It should be noted, however, that these simulation specifications created challenging

500

conditions representative of LLM-based workflows (Lee & Yao, 2023; Russell-Lasalandra et

501

al., 2024), including relatively high dimensionality, large item pools, and hierarchical nesting.

502

Another limitation of the present study is that we did not compare network approaches

503

against the full range of traditional dimensional structure estimation methods that have been

504

applied to embedding-based representations. Although alternative methods to PCA, such as

505

multidimensional scaling, k-means clustering, and factor or structural equation models have

506

been used in embedding contexts, our comparison focused on PCA because it dominates

507

current applications and because traditional factor analytic and clustering approaches also

508

face well-documented challenges that network psychometric methods were explicitly

509

developed to address (Samo & Christensen, 2025). Nonetheless, future research should

510

systematically evaluate the performance of these alternative methods in embedding-based

511

contexts to provide a more comprehensive methodological comparison.

512

Practical Implications

513

514

Our findings provide practical guidance for generative psychometric workflows in which
item pools are produced by LLMs, represented in semantic vector spaces, and evaluated

ESTIMATING DIMENSIONAL STRUCTURE IN GENERATIVE PSYCHOMETRICS 24

515

before human response data are available. In conventional exploratory practice, large item

516

pools require proportionally large samples to stabilize covariance and dimensional structure

517

estimates (Hirschfeld & Thielsch, 2014; MacCallum & Hong, 1999), creating a bottleneck for

518

early prototyping. The present results indicate that embedding-based similarity matrices can

519

be used as a pre-empirical screening step to examine the semantic organization of an item

520

pool, remove redundant or unstable items, and refine construct specifications prior to data

521

collection. This approach is consistent with recent proposals to integrate LLMs into test

522

development while retaining expert oversight (Brickman & Oltmanns, 2025; Fyffe & Kaplan,

523

2024; Kowal & Kantrowitz, 2025; Lee & Yao, 2023; Terry & Waychoff, 2025). Importantly,

524

however, the study shows that dimensional recovery in embedding space depends critically

525

on analytic choices. Across simulations, PCA-based pipelines applied to similarity matrices

526

severely overestimated dimensionality and yielded poorer structural recovery, whereas

527

network psychometric workflows using EGA produced more accurate structural

528

representations, particularly when combined with AI-GENIE filtering procedures based on

529

UVA and bootEGA. Methodological choices at this pre-empirical stage are therefore

530

foundational rather than preliminary, as dimensional structure estimation errors propagate

531

through item selection, dimension definition, construct labeling, and downstream theoretical

532

interpretation in ways that subsequent empirical data analyses may not be able to fully

533

correct.

534

Beyond these procedural considerations, the study also reinforces that semantic

535

structure should be treated as a substantive component of measurement rather than merely

536

a convenient proxy for later psychometric structure. Emerging evidence suggests that item

537

semantics can anticipate empirical item covariation and support cross-instrument prediction,

538

implying that observed dimensional organization is partly constrained by wording and

539

content decisions (Ravenda & Raballo, 2025). This perspective aligns with work arguing that

540

LLM-based embeddings can quantify construct overlap, diagnose jingle–jangle problems, and

541

address taxonomic incommensurability by locating items and construct labels within a

ESTIMATING DIMENSIONAL STRUCTURE IN GENERATIVE PSYCHOMETRICS 25

542

543

shared semantic space (Wulff & Mata, 2025a, 2025b).
Embeddings permit taxonomic evaluation across multiple instruments and broad

544

construct domains simultaneously, a scope of comparison that empirical validation cannot

545

achieve because administering all candidate items together and collecting the necessary

546

sample would be infeasible (Hirschfeld & Thielsch, 2014). In practical terms,

547

embedding-based EGA, taxonomic graph analysis, and AI-GENIE pipelines

548

(Russell-Lasalandra et al., 2024; Samo & Christensen, 2025) provide methodological tools for

549

assessing content validity and conceptual clarity by identifying dimensions that are

550

semantically indistinguishable despite distinct labels, detecting drift of item sets toward

551

adjacent constructs, and revealing misalignment between construct labels and item content.

552

By treating item semantics as an independent source of construct validity evidence rather

553

than a proxy awaiting empirical confirmation, embedding-based workflows position

554

measurement science to more directly address persistent taxonomic ambiguities that have

555

remained difficult to resolve within purely response-based validation frameworks.
