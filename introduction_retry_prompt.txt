Pillar 1 — Theory: What It Means to Analyze the Language of a Scale

Core Question

If psychometric scales are made of language, and language contains information about conceptual structure, then can we analyze the language of a scale itself to learn something about the construct it measures?

The goal of this pillar is to argue that the semantic structure encoded in scale items is itself a meaningful source of psychometric information.

Meaning as Use

- Wittgenstein argued that meaning is use within a language community.
- Words acquire meaning through patterns of use rather than through reference to hidden essences.
- This idea is important because psychometric scales are collections of linguistic statements.
- However, meaning-as-use does not itself provide a quantitative method for measuring meaning.
- The key question becomes: if meaning is use, how can patterns of use be quantified?

Distributional Semantics

- Distributional theory provides a quantitative answer.
- Joos (1950) and Harris (1954) proposed that meaning is reflected in patterns of co-occurrence.
- Words that occur in similar contexts tend to have similar meanings.
- Distributional structure therefore provides an empirical representation of meaning.

- Modern language models are built on this principle.
- Word embeddings compress large-scale co-occurrence patterns into vector spaces.
- Large language models can be understood as highly abstracted and compressed representations of linguistic co-occurrence structure.
- Embeddings therefore provide a quantitative representation of patterns of language use.

Constructs and Nomological Networks

- Cronbach and Meehl (1955) argued that constructs are defined by their lawful relations with observations, behaviors, theories, and other constructs.
- Validity is therefore not a hidden essence but a network of relations.
- A construct is known through the pattern of associations it participates in.
- The meaning of a construct emerges from its position within a nomological network.

Scale Items as Semantic Proposals

- A psychometric scale does not directly observe a construct.
- Instead, researchers write items that express a theoretical interpretation of the construct.
- The item set therefore acts as a semantic proposal for what the construct means.
- A procrastination scale, for example, proposes that procrastination involves behaviors such as delaying tasks, avoidance, poor time management, or deadline-related behavior.
- The language of a scale is therefore not incidental; it is the operationalized theoretical definition of the construct.

The Parallel Between Distributional Meaning and Nomological Networks

- Distributional theory defines word meaning through patterns of co-occurrence with other words.
- Nomological network theory defines construct meaning through patterns of co-occurrence with behaviors, observations, theories, and other constructs.
- Both approaches define meaning relationally rather than through hidden essences.

- This creates a clear structural parallel:

  Distributional semantics:
  Meaning = linguistic relations

  Nomological network theory:
  Meaning = theoretical and behavioral relations

- Psychometric scales sit at the intersection of these two systems because constructs are communicated through language.
- If constructs are encoded in language, then some aspects of nomological structure may also be reflected in semantic structure.

Two Relational Structures

- Traditional factor analysis operates on a person × item response matrix.
- It uses item covariance or correlation to identify latent structure.
- The resulting factors reflect patterns of behavioral co-occurrence.

- Semantic factor analysis operates on embeddings derived from scale items.
- It uses semantic similarity between items to identify latent structure.
- The resulting factors reflect patterns of linguistic co-occurrence.

- Traditional factor analysis therefore recovers behavioral structure.
- Semantic factor analysis recovers semantic structure.
- These are structurally parallel analyses that operate on different sources of information.

Semantic–Behavioral Coherence

- The relationship between semantic structure and behavioral structure can itself be viewed as part of a construct's nomological network.
- When semantic and behavioral structures converge, this strengthens confidence that the scale's language successfully captures the construct's organization.
- When they diverge, the divergence is informative rather than merely problematic.

- Divergence may reveal:
  - item ambiguity
  - theoretical underspecification
  - construct compression
  - jingle-jangle problems
  - measurement artifacts
  - population-specific effects

- The degree of correspondence between semantic and behavioral structure therefore becomes a theoretically meaningful source of evidence.

Why Semantic Factor Analysis Matters

- Because semantic factor analysis operates directly on item language, it requires no respondent data.
- Scales can be evaluated before data collection occurs.
- This enables applications in:
  - scale development
  - item screening
  - construct clarification
  - taxonomy mapping
  - jingle-jangle detection
  - prediction of factor structure

- Semantic factor analysis is not intended to replace response-based factor analysis.
- Instead, it provides a complementary perspective on construct structure.
- Together, semantic and behavioral analyses offer two different views of the same construct.
